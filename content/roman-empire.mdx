## Augustus and the Republic Restored

When [Julius Caesar](/article/julius-caesar) fell, his eighteen-year-old great-nephew and adopted heir, **Octavian**, inherited his name, his fortune, and the loyalty of his veterans. Over thirteen more years of civil war he destroyed his rivals one by one — last of all Mark Antony and Cleopatra, whose deaths in **30 BCE** delivered [Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt) and its grain into his hands. In **27 BCE** the Senate gave him the name by which history knows him: **Augustus**, "the revered one."

Augustus had watched his adoptive father be murdered for looking like a king, and he did not repeat the mistake. He claimed to have "restored the Republic," kept the old offices and the Senate, and called himself merely *princeps* — first citizen. In reality he held the armies, the treasury, and the provinces that mattered. The Empire was a monarchy wearing the mask of a republic, and the disguise was the point.

:::insight The genius of disguise
Augustus understood that Romans would accept the substance of one-man rule as long as they were spared its appearance. By preserving republican forms while hollowing out their power, he founded a system stable enough to outlast him by five hundred years.

## The Roman Peace

The reward for surrendering political freedom was peace. The **Pax Romana** — roughly **27 BCE to 180 CE** — was two centuries in which the Mediterranean world was, for the only time in its history, united under a single government and largely free of war within its borders. A merchant could travel from Britain to Syria on [Roman roads](/article/roman-engineering), paying in a single currency, speaking Latin in the west and Greek in the east, protected by [the legions](/article/roman-army) on the distant frontiers.

> Travel was safe, the seas were cleared of pirates, and a man might journey from one end of the world to the other without fear.

Cities flourished as never before. **Rome itself swelled past a million people** — a scale no European city would match again until London in the 1800s. Aqueducts fed its fountains and baths, grain fleets from Egypt and Africa fed its mouths, and the spectacle of the **Colosseum** fed its appetite for blood and wonder.

## The Good, the Mad, and the Wise

The emperors were a study in extremes. The first century gave Rome the cold competence of Augustus and Tiberius but also the cruelty of **Nero** and the madness ascribed to **Caligula**. Then came the **Five Good Emperors** — Nerva, Trajan, Hadrian, Antoninus Pius, and the philosopher **Marcus Aurelius** — under whom the empire reached its greatest extent and, many later writers thought, the high-water mark of human government.

Under **Trajan** (98–117 CE) the frontiers stretched from the Atlantic to the Persian Gulf. **Hadrian** chose instead to consolidate, walling off the wild north of Britain and fixing the borders he would defend. Marcus Aurelius governed wisely even while writing his private *Meditations* and fighting plague and invasion on the Danube — the model of the reluctant ruler doing his duty.

:::note An empire of citizens
In 212 CE the emperor Caracalla granted Roman citizenship to nearly every free inhabitant of the empire. The city on the Tiber had finally made its whole world Roman — the culmination of the instinct that had driven [Rome](/article/rome) from the beginning.

## Cracks Beneath the Marble

The peace could not hold forever. The empire had no settled rule for succession, so each emperor's death risked civil war; defending thousands of miles of frontier strained the treasury; and in the third century a storm of invasions, plagues, and usurpers nearly tore the whole structure apart. It was rebuilt by hard soldier-emperors like **Diocletian** and **Constantine**, who split the realm to govern it and moved its centre east to a new capital, **Constantinople**.

That eastern half would endure for another thousand years. The western half, exhausted and overrun, would not — a long unravelling told in [the fall of Rome](/article/fall-of-rome). But even in decline the empire left the deepest mark any state has left on the world: its law, its language, its roads, and the very idea that a single civilisation might span a continent.
