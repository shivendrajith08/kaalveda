## Why Preserve the Body?

To the Egyptians, a person was made of several parts — including the **ka** (life-force) and **ba** (personality) — that needed a recognisable, intact **body** to return to. If the body decayed, the soul had no home, and eternal life was lost. Mummification, then, was the indispensable technology of the [Egyptian afterlife](/article/egyptian-religion): the careful preservation of the dead so that they might live again.

## The Seventy-Day Craft

The full process, reserved for those who could afford it, took about **seventy days** and followed a precise sequence:

1. **Removal of organs** — the lungs, liver, stomach, and intestines were taken out and preserved separately in **canopic jars**; the brain, thought useless, was discarded.
2. **The heart stayed** — left in place because it was believed to be the seat of thought and would be weighed in judgement.
3. **Drying** — the body was packed in and covered with **natron**, a natural drying salt, for about forty days until all moisture was gone.
4. **Wrapping** — the desiccated body was anointed with oils and resins and bound in hundreds of metres of linen, with protective **amulets** tucked between the layers.
5. **The mask and rites** — a portrait mask was placed over the face, and the **Opening of the Mouth** ceremony ritually restored the senses for the next life.

:::note Accidental science
Egyptian embalmers accumulated real anatomical knowledge through this work, and the surviving medical papyri show a tradition of careful, empirical observation. In preserving the dead, they advanced the understanding of the living body — a thread that runs into the history of [medicine](/category/medicine).
:::

## Bodies That Outlived Empires

The craft worked extraordinarily well. The embalmed remains of pharaohs like **Ramesses II** and **Seti I** survive in remarkable condition more than three thousand years on, allowing modern scientists to study the diet, diseases, and even the faces of the ancient dead. The most famous of all, the near-intact tomb of **Tutankhamun**, made the boy-king and the art of mummification known across the world.

> The Egyptians set out to defeat death by preserving the body. In doing so, they sent themselves, quite literally, into the future.
