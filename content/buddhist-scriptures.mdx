## The Dharma — More Than a Word

When Buddhists take refuge in the **Three Jewels**, the second, standing between the [Buddha](/article/buddha) and the [Sangha](/article/sangha-monastic-life), is the **Dharma** (Pali *Dhamma*). The word is among the richest in Indian thought. It can mean the *truth* the Buddha awakened to, the *teaching* by which he pointed others toward it, the *path* of practice, and the underlying *law* of reality itself. To hear the Dharma, to practise it, and to realise it are three stages of a single journey — and the scriptures are the Dharma made durable, the teaching preserved so that it could outlast the teacher.

The Buddha himself foresaw this. Appointing no successor, he told his monks at his death that the Dharma and the monastic discipline he had taught would be their teacher when he was gone. The whole vast literature that followed is, in a sense, the working-out of that instruction.

## A Scripture Carried in the Mind

For roughly **four centuries** after the Buddha's death, not a word of his teaching was written down. This was no accident of poverty. India possessed a profound culture of oral preservation — the same discipline that had guarded the [Vedas](/article/vedas) of the older Hindu tradition for a thousand years by memory alone — and Buddhism took it up in its own way. Shortly after the Buddha died, tradition holds, five hundred senior monks gathered at the **First Council** at Rajgir and recited his teachings together to fix them. His attendant Ananda, famed for a perfect memory, recited the discourses; the monk Upali recited the rules of the order.

Thereafter the teaching was preserved by **communal chanting**. Monks specialised in particular collections and recited them in unison, so that no single memory could drift: an error by one voice was corrected by the chorus. The result was a body of scripture guarded, like the Vedas, not on fragile material but in the trained minds of a living community — and much of it still is, chanted daily in monasteries across Asia.

:::insight Why so much repetition?
Readers new to Buddhist scripture are often struck by its relentless repetition — phrases, lists and whole passages recurring almost verbatim. This is not clumsiness but engineering. The texts were built to be *memorised and chanted*, and repetition, numbered lists (the Four this, the Eightfold that, the Five aggregates) and fixed formulas are precisely the features that make a vast body of teaching learnable by heart and checkable in performance. The style is the fingerprint of an oral scripture that spent centuries on the tongue before it ever met the page.
:::

## The Three Baskets

Around **29 BCE**, as famine and war threatened the reciters' survival, monks in Sri Lanka finally wrote the teaching down, inscribing it on palm leaves at the **Aluvihara** cave. The collection they preserved, in the ancient **Pali** language, is called the **Tipitaka** (Sanskrit *Tripitaka*), the **"Three Baskets."** The name recalls the palm-leaf pages once carried in baskets, and it sorts the whole teaching into three:

1. **The Vinaya Pitaka** — the *basket of discipline*: the rules of the monastic order and the stories of how each arose.
2. **The Sutta Pitaka** — the *basket of discourses*: thousands of the Buddha's talks and dialogues, the largest and most read of the baskets, where the [Four Noble Truths](/article/four-noble-truths) and the [path](/article/noble-eightfold-path) are laid out.
3. **The Abhidhamma Pitaka** — the *basket of higher doctrine*: a later, systematic analysis of mind and reality into their ultimate components.

The Pali canon is enormous — many times the length of the Bible — and it remains the scriptural foundation of the **Theravada** school of southern Asia, which regards it as the closest record we have of the Buddha's own words.

## The Rise of the Sutras

Buddhist scripture did not close with the Tipitaka. From around the first century BCE a wave of new texts appeared in Sanskrit, presenting themselves as further discourses of the Buddha — the **Mahayana sutras**. These gave voice to the ideals of the [Mahayana](/article/buddhist-branches), the "Great Vehicle," with its vision of the compassionate **bodhisattva** who vows to save all beings. Among them are some of the most influential religious texts ever written: the **Lotus Sutra**, with its promise that all beings may become Buddhas; the **Heart Sutra** and the **Perfection of Wisdom** literature, with their piercing teaching of emptiness; and the **Pure Land** sutras that shaped the devotional Buddhism of East Asia.

Traditional Mahayana accounts hold these to be teachings the Buddha gave for more advanced students, preserved and revealed in later ages. Historians read them as the inspired creativity of a movement expanding the tradition. Either way, they opened Buddhism to vast new possibilities of thought and devotion, and became the beating heart of the scriptures of China, Korea, Japan and Tibet.

## Tantras, Commentaries, and a Living Library

A third stratum followed with the rise of **Vajrayana**, the "Diamond Vehicle," whose **tantras** taught esoteric rituals, mantras and visualisations as swift paths to awakening. Around every layer of scripture grew a further ocean of **commentary** — the analyses of great teachers like Buddhaghosa in the south and Nagarjuna, Asanga and Vasubandhu in the Mahayana world — that interpreted the texts for each generation.

The physical scale is staggering. The Tibetan collection alone divides into the **Kangyur**, the translated word of the Buddha, and the **Tengyur**, the translated treatises — together hundreds of volumes. The Chinese canon fills a comparable shelf. Buddhism became, over two millennia, one of the most prolifically literate traditions in human history.

> Whatever is well spoken is the word of the Buddha.

That generous saying, cherished across the schools, captures why the library never closed: what matters is not the pedigree of a text but whether it leads toward the ending of suffering.

## From a Spoken Word to a Civilization's Shelf

The story of the Buddhist scriptures is the story of a single spoken teaching becoming the intellectual inheritance of half a continent. It began with a teacher who wrote nothing and a community that remembered everything; it passed through the palm-leaf baskets of Sri Lanka, the soaring sutras of the Mahayana, and the tantras of Tibet; and it was carried, translated and recopied along every road by which [Buddhism spread](/article/spread-of-buddhism). To open these texts is to hear, still, the cadence of monks chanting in unison to keep a memory exact — the Dharma handed forward, voice to voice, across twenty-five centuries.
