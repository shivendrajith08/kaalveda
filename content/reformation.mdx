## A Door in Wittenberg

The traditional story has it that on the last day of October in **1517**, a professor of theology named **Martin Luther** posted ninety-five propositions on the door of the castle church in the German town of Wittenberg. He meant only to invite a scholarly debate about the sale of **indulgences** — papers that promised to shorten the buyer's time in purgatory, peddled to raise money for the rebuilding of St Peter's in Rome. He could not have imagined that he was lighting a fire that would consume the unity of Western [Christianity](/article/christianity).

Luther's protest struck a nerve because it touched a deep and growing unease. Many devout Christians had long been troubled by the wealth, corruption and worldliness of the late medieval church. What began as a quarrel about indulgences swiftly became a challenge to the whole structure of authority — to the power of the pope, the role of the priesthood, and the very means by which a soul is saved.

:::insight The heart of the matter
Luther's central conviction was deceptively simple: that human beings are reconciled to God not by good works, payments or priestly mediation, but by **faith alone**, as a free gift of grace. And the test of truth, he insisted, was not the authority of popes or councils but **scripture alone** — [the Bible](/article/the-bible), which every believer had the right and the duty to read. These two principles dynamited the foundations on which the medieval church had stood.
:::

## The Word Set Loose

What turned a German monk's protest into a European revolution was a machine. Gutenberg's printing press, barely sixty years old, seized on Luther's writings and scattered them across the continent faster than any authority could gather them up. His pamphlets sold in the tens of thousands; his translation of the Bible into vigorous, everyday German put scripture into the hands of ordinary people and helped forge the very language they spoke. To read the Bible for oneself, in one's own tongue, became a revolutionary act.

The movement spread and splintered. In Switzerland **Huldrych Zwingli** and then the formidable **John Calvin** built reformed churches of their own, Calvin's Geneva becoming a model for Protestants from Scotland to the Netherlands. In England the Reformation took a more political turn when **Henry VIII**, denied a divorce by Rome, broke with the pope and made himself head of a national church. Across a single generation the seamless robe of Western Christendom was torn into a patchwork of rival confessions.

> Here I stand; I can do no other. God help me. Amen.

Luther's defiant words, spoken when ordered to recant before the emperor at the Diet of Worms, became a watchword of the new age — the lone conscience standing against the combined authority of church and crown.

## Reform, Counter-Reform and War

The Catholic Church did not stand still. In the **Council of Trent** and the disciplined zeal of new orders like the Jesuits, it mounted a vigorous **Counter-Reformation**, reforming its own abuses while reaffirming the doctrines Protestants had rejected. The contest between the two camps was not only theological. It set off more than a century of brutal religious wars across Europe, culminating in the catastrophic Thirty Years' War, which laid waste much of Germany before exhaustion forced a grudging acceptance that Christendom would remain divided.

:::caution The cost and the gift
The Reformation cannot be told as a simple tale of liberation. It came with terrible bloodshed, with persecution on every side, with the smashing of [sacred images](/article/christian-art) and the burning of dissenters by Catholics and Protestants alike. Yet its long consequences ran deep and often in directions none of the reformers intended — toward literacy, toward the questioning of inherited authority, and ultimately toward ideas of conscience and self-government that recall the civic spirit of [Greek democracy](/article/greek-democracy).
:::

## The Modern World in Embryo

Historians still debate how much of modernity was born in the Reformation, but its fingerprints are everywhere. By insisting that each believer read and judge scripture for themselves, it nourished mass literacy and the habit of private judgement. By breaking the monopoly of a single church, it opened — slowly, painfully, and against the reformers' own intentions — the path toward religious toleration. The sharpened reasoning of [Greek philosophy](/article/greek-philosophy), revived by Renaissance scholars, fed the new readiness to question and to argue. Out of the wreck of medieval unity came a Europe of competing ideas, printed words and individual consciences — the seedbed of the modern mind.
