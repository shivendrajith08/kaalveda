## The Oath Against Kings

The Republic was born of a refusal. According to tradition, in **509 BCE** the Romans drove out **Tarquin the Proud**, the last of their kings, and bound themselves by oath never to let one man rule alone again. Everything in the new system was designed to keep that oath. Where a king had held power for life, the Republic split his authority among many hands and handed it out for a single year at a time.

The word *res publica* means simply "the public thing" — the commonwealth, the affairs of the people held in common rather than owned by a ruler. It was an idea as much as a government, and the Romans guarded it with a near-religious zeal.

## A Machine of Checks and Balances

At the top stood two **consuls**, elected jointly for one year, each able to veto the other so that neither could become a tyrant. Below them ranged a ladder of offices — praetors who judged, quaestors who managed money, aediles who ran the city, censors who counted citizens and policed morals. Climbing this ladder, the *cursus honorum*, was the ambition of every aristocrat.

Behind the magistrates sat the **Senate**, a standing council of several hundred ex-magistrates who held no formal command yet shaped almost everything through sheer authority and experience. And beneath them all stood the **assemblies** of citizens, who elected the magistrates, passed laws, and declared war.

:::insight A constitution of friction
The Greek historian Polybius marvelled that Rome's system mixed monarchy (the consuls), aristocracy (the Senate), and democracy (the assemblies) so that each restrained the others. Its strength was not efficiency but balance — power was deliberately made hard to seize.

## Patricians, Plebeians, and the Struggle for Rights

The early Republic belonged to the **patricians**, a closed circle of noble families who monopolised office and priesthood. The common citizens, the **plebeians**, fought back over two centuries through a remarkable, mostly bloodless weapon: the **secession**. By marching out of the city and refusing to work or fight, the plebs forced concession after concession — their own officials, the **tribunes**, whose persons were sacred and who could veto the acts of magistrates; written law in the **Twelve Tables**; and at last the right to hold the highest offices themselves.

> Let justice be done, though the heavens fall.

This long "Conflict of the Orders" never abolished inequality, but it wove the plebeians into the state and produced a governing class open enough to renew itself — one more expression of Rome's gift for [absorbing rather than excluding](/article/rome).

## Conquest and the Seeds of Ruin

The Republic was a war machine of astonishing endurance. It bound the peoples of Italy into a network of alliances that supplied [soldiers](/article/roman-army) without end, and in three brutal wars with **Carthage** — the Punic Wars — it survived Hannibal's invasion and emerged as the unrivalled power of the western Mediterranean. Greece, Spain, and North Africa followed.

Yet conquest carried a poison. Floods of war captives became slaves who worked vast estates, driving small farmers off the land; the spoils of empire made a few families monstrously rich; and victorious generals began to command armies loyal to themselves rather than to Rome.

:::caution When the army serves the general, not the state
Reforms opened the legions to the landless poor, who now looked to their commander for land and pay. An ambitious general with a devoted army became a power the Senate could not control — the fatal flaw that would destroy the Republic.

## The Long Collapse

The Republic's last century was a slow-motion suicide: the murdered reformers **Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus**, the rival warlords **Marius and Sulla**, civil wars fought in the streets of Rome itself. The system that had been built to stop any one man from ruling could no longer stop the men its conquests had created. The crisis found its resolution in [Julius Caesar](/article/julius-caesar), and through his heir Augustus passed quietly into the [Roman Empire](/article/roman-empire). The Republic was never formally abolished — its offices and Senate survived for centuries — but the public thing had become, in truth, one man's.
