## A Quarter of Humanity

Islam today counts some **1.9 billion** followers — close to a quarter of all the people alive. Only [Christianity](/article/christianity-today) has more adherents, and on present trends the gap is closing fast: with a young population and high birth rates across much of the Muslim world, Islam is the fastest-growing major religion on Earth, and demographers project that the number of Muslims may roughly equal the number of Christians before the century is out. It is a faith not in retreat but in expansion, entering its fifteenth century with extraordinary vitality.

Perhaps the most surprising fact about the Muslim world is where it lies. Although Islam was born among the Arabs and the [Quran](/article/the-quran) was revealed in Arabic, Arabs today make up only about one in five of the world's Muslims. The largest Muslim populations live far to the east: **Indonesia** is the most populous Muslim nation on Earth, followed by **Pakistan**, **India** and **Bangladesh**. The centre of gravity of the faith has long since shifted from the deserts of its birth to the teeming nations of South and Southeast Asia — and Islam is now woven into the life of Africa, Europe and the Americas as well.

:::insight A young and global faith
The single most important fact about Islam's future is its youth. Across the Muslim world the median age is strikingly low, and a faith with a young population is a growing one. This demographic energy, combined with the steady spread of Islam in sub-Saharan Africa, means that the twenty-first century will likely see the Muslim share of humanity rise faster than that of any other tradition. Like [Hinduism](/article/hinduism) and Christianity, Islam remains one of the great living forces shaping the spiritual life of billions — but it is, in a sense, the youngest and the fastest-rising of them all.
:::

## Many Ways of Being Muslim

The Muslim world is no monolith. Beyond the great [Sunni–Shia divide](/article/sunni-shia-divide), Islam is lived in a thousand ways across its vast geography. In the markets of Cairo, the rice fields of Java, the steppes of Central Asia and the suburbs of Paris, the same Five Pillars frame lives shaped by utterly different cultures, languages and histories. There are Muslims who read their faith in conservative and traditional terms, and Muslims who embrace thoroughly modern and liberal interpretations; there are the inward, ecstatic devotions of the **Sufi** orders and the strict legalism of reform movements; there are quiet secular Muslims and fervent activists.

This diversity has been the source of vigorous debate within Islam over how the faith should meet the modern world — questions of law and democracy, of the role of women, of science and tradition, of how a revelation given in seventh-century Arabia should guide life in the twenty-first century. These are live and contested conversations, carried on by Muslims themselves across the whole spectrum of opinion, much as every great tradition wrestles in each age with how to be faithful to its founding vision under new conditions.

> O humankind, We created you from a single pair of a male and a female, and made you into nations and tribes, that you may know one another.

That verse from the Quran, often quoted today, speaks to a faith that has always crossed the lines of race and nation — and that now, more than ever, gathers peoples of every continent into a single community.

## The Pilgrim City and the Global Diaspora

Nothing displays the global reach of Islam more vividly than the annual **hajj**. Each year some two million pilgrims, drawn from virtually every country on Earth, converge on Mecca to perform the rites that [Muhammad](/article/muhammad) established — making it one of the largest peaceful gatherings of human beings anywhere on the planet. In the white-robed throng circling the Kaaba, the nationalities and tongues of the whole world dissolve for a few days into a single act of worship, an image of unity that has moved believers for fourteen centuries.

Beyond the heartlands, Islam is now a permanent and growing presence in the West. Migration over the past century has built substantial Muslim communities across Europe, North America and beyond, where mosques have become part of the urban landscape and Muslims contribute to every walk of public life. This diaspora has made Islam, for the first time, a near-universal religion in the literal sense — present in significant numbers on every inhabited continent.

:::note A faith and its perceptions
In recent decades Islam has often been viewed in the West through the narrow and distorting lens of conflict and a handful of violent extremist movements. Yet such movements represent a tiny fraction of the world's Muslims, the overwhelming majority of whom live ordinary lives of faith, family and work, and many of whom are themselves the principal victims of extremist violence. To understand Islam today is to look past the headlines to the vast, varied and largely peaceful reality of nearly two billion believers — the same faith that once lit the lamps of the [Golden Age](/article/islamic-golden-age) of learning, lived out across the whole face of the Earth.
:::

## Into the Fifteenth Century

By the Islamic calendar, which counts from the Hijra of 622, the faith has now passed into its fifteenth century. It does so as a religion of remarkable energy — young, growing, global, and as deeply rooted in the daily lives of its people as any tradition on Earth. The five daily prayers still turn nearly two billion faces toward Mecca; the fast of Ramadan still empties the streets at dusk across half the world; the pilgrim throng still circles the ancient shrine. From a message proclaimed by a single man in the hills above Mecca, Islam has become the spiritual home of one person in four — and its story, far from finished, is still being written across every continent of the world.
