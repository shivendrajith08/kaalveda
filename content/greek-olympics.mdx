## A Festival Older Than History

The Greeks themselves dated the first Olympic Games to **776 BCE**, and from that year they counted time, reckoning the years in four-year spans called **Olympiads**. So important was the festival that it gave a fractured people one of their few shared calendars. Held at **Olympia**, a sanctuary of [Zeus](/article/greek-mythology) in the green western Peloponnese, the Games were first and last a religious act — an offering to the king of the gods, woven through with sacrifice, prayer, and the smoke of a hundred oxen burning on his altar.

For the first centuries there was only one event: the **stadion**, a sprint of about two hundred metres the length of the stadium, which gave us the very word. Over time the programme grew to include longer races, wrestling, boxing, the brutal all-in combat called the **pankration**, the chariot race, and the five-event **pentathlon** of running, jumping, discus, javelin, and wrestling. The athletes competed **naked**, their bodies oiled, and the Greek devotion to the trained human form fed directly into the [sculpture](/article/greek-architecture) for which the age is famous.

:::insight Counting time by the Games
The Olympiad was so central that Greek historians dated events by it — "in the third year of the such-and-such Olympiad." A sporting festival became the closest thing the scattered Greek world had to a common clock.

## The Sacred Truce

Because the Greeks were so often at war with one another, the Games could only happen under a remarkable institution: the **Olympic Truce**, the *ekecheiria*. For the weeks around the festival, heralds travelled the Greek world proclaiming a sacred peace. Wars were suspended, armies could not enter the territory of Olympia, and athletes and spectators were guaranteed safe passage through hostile lands to reach the Games.

> Let the world lay down its arms.

The truce did not end Greek warfare — the cities went back to fighting the moment the Games were over — but for a few weeks every four years it held, and it expressed something the Greeks believed about themselves: that beneath all their rivalries they were one people, who could meet in peace at the altar of a shared god.

## The Glory of the Victor

There were no prizes for second place, no silver or bronze. The only reward at Olympia was a simple crown of wild **olive** cut from a sacred tree — and the immortal glory that came with it. An Olympic victor brought honour not just to himself but to his entire city, which might welcome him home through a breach knocked in its own walls, reward him with free meals for life, and commission poets like **Pindar** to compose soaring odes in his praise. A statue of the champion might be raised at Olympia itself.

:::note Who could compete
The Games belonged to free Greek men. Women were barred even from watching the main festival on pain of death, though unmarried girls had their own footraces in honour of Hera, and a woman could win the chariot race as the owner of the horses. Like the [democracy](/article/greek-democracy) of Athens, the Olympic ideal was glorious and bounded at once.

## From Olympia to the World

The ancient Games ran for almost twelve centuries, an extraordinary span of continuity, until the Christian Roman emperor **Theodosius I** banned such pagan festivals around 393 CE and Olympia fell into ruin, buried by earthquake and flood. For fifteen hundred years the Games were only a memory in old books.

Then, in 1896, a French educator named **Pierre de Coubertin**, inspired by the rediscovered ruins and the ancient ideal, revived the Olympics as a modern international festival in Athens. Today's Games — the rings, the torch relayed from Olympia, the gathering of nations in peaceful contest — are a deliberate echo of the Greek original. The notion that sport can unite a divided world under a shared truce, first tried at a sanctuary of Zeus, remains one of ancient Greece's most living gifts.
