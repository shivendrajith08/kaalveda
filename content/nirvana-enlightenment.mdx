## The Word That Means "To Go Out"

Everything in Buddhism points toward one destination, and it has a name that is almost shockingly plain. **Nirvana** — in Pali, *nibbana* — comes from a root meaning **"to blow out"** or **"to be extinguished,"** as a flame goes out when its fuel is spent. This is the master image of the whole tradition. The [Second Noble Truth](/article/four-noble-truths) diagnosed our bondage as a kind of burning — the fever of craving — and nirvana is simply what happens when that fire, no longer fed, goes out.

The Buddha named three fires in particular: **greed, hatred and delusion**, the "three poisons" that drive the wheel of [saṃsāra](/article/karma-rebirth-samsara). To reach nirvana is to extinguish all three. What remains when they are gone is not a void but a profound release — the texts call it *the unconditioned, the deathless, the farther shore, the highest bliss.*

## The Peace That Ends the Wheel

Nirvana is the fulfilment of the [Third Noble Truth](/article/four-noble-truths): the cessation of suffering. Because craving is the cause of dukkha, the ending of craving is the ending of dukkha — and because craving is also the fuel of [rebirth](/article/karma-rebirth-samsara), its ending brings the round of rebirths to a close. One who reaches nirvana will, after this life, not be born again. The wheel that has turned for countless ages, for that stream of becoming, finally stops.

This makes nirvana the direct answer to the whole predicament Buddhism describes. It is not a reward bestowed by a god for good behaviour, nor a paradise reached after death, but a **transformation of the mind** achievable by a living human being — as the Buddha himself achieved it beneath the Bodhi tree while still a man of thirty-five, with forty-five years of life yet to live.

:::insight Two nirvanas
The tradition distinguishes two moments. **Nirvana "with remainder"** is the awakening reached *in life* — the mind wholly freed from greed, hatred and delusion, yet still inhabiting a body that grows hungry and tired and old. The enlightened person walks the earth in peace, acting from compassion rather than craving. Then, at death, comes **nirvana "without remainder"** — the *parinirvana*, the final going-out, when the freed stream of becoming is no longer reborn at all. The Buddha lived the first for decades and entered the second at Kushinagar.
:::

## What Cannot Be Said

Ask what nirvana *positively is*, and the Buddha grows deliberately quiet. He describes it far more readily by what it *ends* — the extinction of craving, the cessation of suffering, the stilling of all formations — than by what it is in itself. This reticence is not evasion but precision. Our language and concepts are tools of a mind still shaped by craving and the illusion of a self; they are fitted to the world inside the wheel. To pin nirvana into those categories — to ask whether one who has "gone out" exists, or does not exist, or both, or neither — is, the Buddha said, to misuse questions built for a different country.

He compared it to asking where a flame *goes* when it is extinguished. The question feels natural and yet dissolves on inspection: the flame has not travelled anywhere, it has simply ceased to burn. So the Buddha set such speculations aside as "unconducive" — the things that do not lead to freedom — and turned attention back to the practice that actually opens the door.

> There is, monks, an unborn, unbecome, unmade, unconditioned. If there were not, there would be no escape from the born, the become, the made, the conditioned.

## Enlightenment — Waking, Not Vanishing

Alongside nirvana stands the idea of **enlightenment** or **awakening** — *bodhi*, the very word from which "Buddha" is formed. Where nirvana names the *extinguishing* of the fires, bodhi names the *seeing* that accomplishes it: the direct, liberating insight into the way things really are — into impermanence, suffering and no-self, into the [Four Noble Truths](/article/four-noble-truths) known not as ideas but as reality. The two are the same event seen from two sides: to wake up fully *is* to blow out the fires, and to blow them out *is* to be awake.

This is why the goal is imagined as **waking, not escaping**. The enlightened one has not fled the world into a trance; they see the same world everyone sees, but without the distortions of greed and fear, and so without suffering. The peace of nirvana is available, in a sense, right here — hidden only by the clouds of craving that the [path](/article/noble-eightfold-path) exists to clear.

## Nirvana and Moksha

It is worth setting nirvana beside the liberation sought by the surrounding [Hindu philosophy](/article/hindu-philosophy), because the two are cousins and rivals. Both traditions seek release from the wheel of rebirth; both call that release the highest good. But where the Upanishads describe **moksha** as the reunion of the eternal self, *atman*, with the infinite ground of being, *Brahman* — a *finding* of what one truly is — the Buddha's teaching of no-self reframes the goal entirely. There is no eternal self to be reunited with anything; there is only the ending of the craving and ignorance that fabricate a self in the first place. Liberation is not a homecoming of the soul but the quiet cessation of the fever that invented it.

## The Farther Shore

For all its philosophical subtlety, nirvana was never meant as a puzzle for scholars. It is the promise that makes the whole [Buddhist path](/article/noble-eightfold-path) worth walking — the assurance that suffering is not the final word, that the wheel can be left behind, that a freedom the Buddha called *the highest happiness* is genuinely open to human beings. Every [school](/article/buddhist-branches) that later carried the teaching across Asia would picture that farther shore a little differently — some as a serene extinction, some as a boundless awakened compassion, some as a reality shining through this very world. But all of them point the seeker toward the same going-out of the flame, and the peace on its far side.
