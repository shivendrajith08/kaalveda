## A Faith Born From a Death

Most religions begin with a teaching, a law or a vision. Christianity begins with an execution. Its founder, [Jesus of Nazareth](/article/jesus-of-nazareth), was a Jewish teacher and healer who gathered followers in the villages of Galilee, was arrested in Jerusalem, and was crucified by the authorities of the [Roman Empire](/article/roman-empire) around the year 30. By every worldly measure that should have been the end. Instead his followers proclaimed something astonishing — that he had risen from the dead, that his death had reconciled humanity to God, and that he was the long-promised Messiah, the **Christ**, through whom the world itself would be made new.

From that proclamation everything else flows. The earliest believers were a small movement within Judaism, keeping the Hebrew scriptures and the festivals of their people while insisting that in Jesus the ancient promises had come true. Within a single generation the message had leapt beyond the Jewish world to Greeks, Romans and the peoples of the [Mediterranean](/article/fall-of-rome), carried along the same roads and sea lanes that bound the empire together.

:::insight The scandal at the centre
The cross was not a noble symbol when Christians adopted it — it was the most shameful death the Roman world knew, reserved for slaves and rebels. That the new faith made its emblem an instrument of torture, and called a crucified man the Lord of all, was a scandal to Jews and folly to Greeks alike. Christianity has never lost that strangeness: a God who conquers not by force but by suffering, who is found in weakness rather than power.
:::

## One God, Three Persons

At the heart of Christian belief stands a paradox the tradition spent centuries trying to express. Christians are fierce monotheists, inheritors of Israel's conviction that there is one God and no other. Yet they came to confess that this one God is known as **Father, Son and Holy Spirit** — the doctrine of the **Trinity**, three persons in one divine being. In Jesus, they held, God had not merely sent a messenger but had come in person, fully God and fully human at once.

These were not idle speculations. They were hammered out in argument, prayer and sometimes bitter conflict across the first centuries, in the great councils of the early church. To say exactly who Jesus was — and how the divine and the human met in him — became the labour of generations, and the answers were written into creeds still recited every week by hundreds of millions.

> For God so loved the world that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life.

That single sentence from the Gospel of John has become perhaps the most quoted line of [the Bible](/article/the-bible), and it captures the Christian claim in miniature: that the whole drama is driven by love, and aimed at the rescue of every human being.

## A Religion of the Book and the Table

Christianity is a religion of the word. Its scripture, the Bible, joins the Hebrew writings it calls the **Old Testament** to the **New Testament** of gospels and letters that tell of Jesus and the first communities. But it is also a religion of ritual and gathered life. Its central act is the sharing of bread and wine — the **Eucharist** or communion — in which believers re-enact the last meal Jesus ate with his friends and hold themselves united to him. To be baptised with water and to break this bread together are the gestures that make a Christian.

:::note A faith of many houses
Christianity is not one institution but a vast family. The ancient **Orthodox** churches of the East, the **Catholic** Church centred on Rome, and the many **Protestant** traditions born of the [Reformation](/article/reformation) all share the same scriptures and the same Lord, yet differ deeply in worship, authority and emphasis. Like [Hinduism](/article/hinduism), Christianity contains within one name an astonishing range of ways to be faithful — from silent monasteries to ecstatic revival meetings.
:::

## The Story This Cluster Tells

What follows is a journey through that vast inheritance. We begin with the historical figure of Jesus himself, then turn to the scriptures that record him and the [early church](/article/early-church) that carried his message across the Roman world. We follow the faith into the medieval drama of the [Crusades](/article/the-crusades), into the quiet inner fire of its [mystics](/article/christian-mysticism), through the great rupture of the [Reformation](/article/reformation), and into the soaring [art and architecture](/article/christian-art) that gave it visible form. We end with [Christianity today](/article/christianity-today), a global faith remade once more by the peoples of Africa, Asia and Latin America. It is the story of how a single death in an obscure province came to shape the conscience of the world.
