## The First Poem

Tradition calls the Ramayana the **adi-kavya**, the "first poem" — the work in which the sage **Valmiki** is said to have invented the very art of verse, his grief at the death of a bird crystallising spontaneously into metre. Composed perhaps around 300 BCE and growing over the following centuries, it is shorter and more shapely than the sprawling [Mahabharata](/article/mahabharata): some twenty-four thousand verses telling a single, luminous story with the clean inevitability of a fable.

That story is quickly told. **Rama**, the noble prince of **Ayodhya**, is about to be crowned heir when a palace intrigue sends him into fourteen years of exile in the forest. His wife **Sita** and his devoted brother **Lakshmana** go with him, choosing hardship over separation. In the wilderness the demon king **Ravana**, lord of the island fortress of **Lanka**, abducts Sita and carries her across the sea. To rescue her, Rama allies with an army of monkeys, led by the mighty **Hanuman**, builds a bridge to the island, slays Ravana in a great war, and at last returns home to be crowned. The exile ends; light returns to Ayodhya; and the homecoming is remembered every year in the festival of lamps, **Diwali**.

:::insight A god who does not know he is God
Rama is an avatar of [Vishnu](/article/hindu-gods), the preserver descended to rid the world of a demon no god could otherwise destroy. Yet for most of the poem Rama lives and suffers as a man — grieving, doubting, weeping for his lost wife — apparently unaware of his own divinity. This is the heart of the Ramayana's appeal. It shows the divine choosing the full burden of human duty, and so makes the ideal life feel not remote but achievable.
:::

## The Pattern of the Ideal

More than any other Hindu text, the Ramayana is held up as a model for how to live. Each of its figures embodies a perfection of **dharma**. Rama is the ideal son, king and husband, who keeps his word though it costs him his throne and his happiness. Sita is the ideal of devotion and quiet courage, faithful through abduction and exile. Lakshmana is the perfect brother, Hanuman the perfect servant, and even the demon Ravana is a figure of tragic grandeur, a great scholar and king brought down by a single overmastering desire.

To call someone's reign **Ram Rajya**, "the rule of Rama," is still the highest praise an Indian can give a government: a golden age of justice, plenty and right order. For millennia parents have told the story to teach their children what loyalty, courage and faithfulness look like — a moral education delivered as an adventure.

> Even the deathless gods envied the people of Ayodhya, who in the reign of Rama knew neither famine nor fear, neither sickness nor sorrow.

## An Echo Across the Sea

The Ramayana invites comparison with another great ancient war fought across the water to recover a stolen wife — the Greek expedition against [Troy](/article/trojan-war). Both stories turn on an abduction, a long campaign overseas, and a fortress city brought down at last. But where the Greek epic broods on rage, pride and the futility of glory, the Indian one is finally a story of devotion and order restored — of a hero who fights not for honour but for duty, and a homecoming that heals the world rather than emptying it. The two poems are like mirror images of the heroic age, each revealing what its civilisation most prized.

:::note A story that crossed an ocean
The Ramayana is not India's alone. Carried by traders and monks, it took root across Southeast Asia, where Rama's tale is danced, carved and performed to this day — as the *Ramakien* of Thailand, the *Reamker* of Cambodia, and the shadow-puppet epics of Java and Bali. Few stories on Earth have been loved by so many peoples for so long, or made so thoroughly their own.
:::

## The Living Epic

The Ramayana has never been merely read. It is sung in villages, staged each autumn in the open-air dramas of **Ramlila**, recited in Tulsidas's beloved Hindi retelling, and watched by hundreds of millions in television and film. When a serialised version aired in India, streets emptied as families gathered before their screens. Its hero is among the most worshipped deities in the world, his name a common greeting and a prayer for the dying. Together with the Mahabharata, the Ramayana shows how the deepest ideals of [Hinduism](/article/hinduism) — duty done with grace, love kept through suffering, order restored from chaos — reached the heart of a civilisation not as commandments but as a story too beautiful ever to forget.
