## The Temple as a Machine for Beauty

The Greeks did not build the largest structures of the ancient world. Their temples are modest beside the [pyramids of Giza](/article/pyramids-of-giza) or the vaults of Rome. What they built instead was the most *refined* — buildings of such precise proportion and quiet harmony that they have served as the standard of architectural beauty for twenty-five centuries. A Greek temple was, above all, a problem in proportion solved with mathematical care.

The temple itself was conceived as the house of a god, not a hall for worshippers, who gathered outside at an open-air altar. Its essential form was simple: a rectangular room holding the cult statue, set on a stepped platform and ringed by a colonnade of columns supporting a low gabled roof. Everything depended on getting the relationships right — the ratio of a column's height to its width, of the spacing between columns, of the whole to its parts. The Greeks pursued these ratios with the same passion their [philosophers](/article/greek-philosophy) brought to the order of the cosmos.

:::insight Beauty as mathematics
For the Greeks, beauty was not decoration laid on top of a building but proportion built into its bones. Harmony in stone reflected the same numerical order that **Pythagoras** had found in music and the heavens — architecture as visible mathematics.

## The Three Orders

Greek architecture is written in an alphabet of three **orders**, each a complete system of column and the entablature it carries. The **Doric** order, oldest and sternest, has sturdy fluted columns with plain cushion-like tops, rising straight from the platform without a base — the order of the Parthenon, all strength and restraint. The **Ionic** order is lighter and more graceful, its columns slimmer and crowned with the elegant spiral scrolls called volutes. The **Corinthian**, latest and most ornate, tops its columns with baskets of carved acanthus leaves, and would become a favourite of the Romans.

These were not just decorative choices but a shared grammar that every Greek builder knew and every later architect would inherit. To name a building "Doric" or "Ionic" is still to describe it in a language the Greeks invented.

> Well building hath three conditions: firmness, commodity, and delight.

The Roman architect Vitruvius, summing up the Greek inheritance, named the three demands that good building must meet — that it stand firm, serve its purpose, and please the eye. The Greeks satisfied the third with a subtlety that still astonishes.

## The Perfection of the Parthenon

The supreme achievement was the **Parthenon**, the temple of Athena raised on the Acropolis of Athens in the 440s and 430s BCE under the statesman Pericles, at the height of Athenian [democracy](/article/greek-democracy). To the casual eye it is a simple rectangle of Doric columns. In truth almost nothing in it is straight. The architects **Iktinos** and **Kallikrates** built in a host of tiny, deliberate distortions to correct the errors of human sight.

The platform swells gently upward in the middle, so it will not appear to sag. The columns lean very slightly inward and bulge a little at the centre, so they will not seem to taper or buckle. The corner columns are subtly thicker and more closely spaced. Not a single major line is truly level or vertical, yet the result reads as flawlessly regular — a building bent in secret so that it might appear perfect. It is one of the most sophisticated optical refinements in the history of building.

:::note Colour beneath the white
We picture Greek temples as gleaming white marble, but that is an accident of time. Originally they blazed with paint — the sculptures and mouldings picked out in red, blue, and gold. The serene whiteness we admire is the ghost of a far more vivid original.

## A Style That Conquered Time

Greek architecture did not stay in Greece. The Romans took the orders and married them to their own genius for [engineering](/article/roman-engineering), wrapping Greek columns around their arches, vaults, and domes to build on a scale the Greeks never attempted. Through Rome the classical language passed into the bloodstream of Western building, and across the [ancient architecture](/article/ancient-architecture) of the Mediterranean it became the common tongue of monumental design.

It has never gone out of fashion for long. The Renaissance revived it; the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries built courthouses, museums, banks, and capitols in the image of Greek temples to borrow their air of dignity and reason. When a modern democracy wants its parliament to look serious and enduring, it reaches, still, for a row of Greek columns — proof that the grammar worked out on a few Aegean hilltops remains the most quoted in the world.
