## A Religion Without a Beginning

Most faiths can name a founder and a moment of revelation. Hinduism can name neither. It has no single prophet, no one book, no central authority and no creed that every believer must accept. Instead it is a confluence — the slow merging of the rituals of the **Vedic** peoples who composed sacred hymns more than three thousand years ago with the older devotions of the [Indus Valley](/article/hinduism) and the countless local traditions of the subcontinent. The very word *Hindu* began as a geographic label, the Persian name for the people who lived beyond the river Sindhu, the Indus. Only much later did it come to name a religion.

Its followers more often call it **sanatana dharma** — the eternal way, or eternal law. The phrase captures something essential. Hinduism is less a set of doctrines to be believed than an order to be lived: a *dharma*, a right way of acting, that holds the cosmos and the conscience together. Within that order a worshipper may be a strict philosopher who sees the divine as a single formless absolute, or a villager who garlands a much-loved local goddess, and both are wholly Hindu.

:::insight One reality, many names
The most quoted line in all the [Vedas](/article/vedas) reads: *"Truth is one; the wise call it by many names."* This single idea — that the one supreme reality, **Brahman**, can be approached through countless gods, images and paths — is the key that unlocks Hinduism's astonishing variety. It is not many religions wearing one name, but one vision wearing many faces.
:::

## The Great Ideas

Beneath the variety run a few currents shared across almost every Hindu tradition. The first is **Brahman**, the ultimate reality underlying all things, and the **atman**, the innermost self — which the [Upanishads](/article/hindu-philosophy) boldly declare to be one and the same. To know, in the depths of meditation, that *atman is Brahman* is to glimpse the secret of existence.

The second is the great wheel of **samsara**, the cycle of birth, death and rebirth through which every soul passes, life after life. What carries a soul forward is **karma** — the moral law of cause and effect, by which every action ripens into a consequence. To live well is to sow well. The aim of the whole spiritual life is finally **moksha**: liberation, the release of the soul from the wheel and its reunion with the infinite.

> You are what your deep, driving desire is. As your desire is, so is your will. As your will is, so is your deed. As your deed is, so is your destiny.

That passage from the Upanishads turns the entire moral universe on the quiet axis of the human heart.

## Many Paths Up One Mountain

Because the goal is liberation and the seekers are infinitely various, Hinduism offers not one road but several. The **Bhagavad Gita**, the most beloved of all Hindu scriptures, sets out three great paths that the divine charioteer Krishna teaches to the warrior Arjuna. There is the path of **action** done without selfish attachment to its fruits; the path of **knowledge**, the philosopher's ascent through wisdom; and the path of **devotion**, *bhakti*, the loving surrender of the heart to a chosen form of God. A fourth, the path of disciplined practice through [yoga and meditation](/article/yoga-and-meditation), threads through them all.

:::note A living tradition, not a relic
Unlike the religions of [ancient Egypt](/article/egyptian-religion) or [Rome](/article/roman-religion), which we study only through ruins and texts, Hinduism is fiercely alive. Its hymns are still chanted, its festivals still light up cities, its temples still crowded. Roughly **1.2 billion people** — about one in seven humans alive — follow it today, the third-largest religion on Earth and the only one of the ancient world's great faiths still practised by the people who first composed its scriptures.
:::

## A Civilisation of the Spirit

To follow Hinduism through history is to follow much of the history of India itself. From the sacrifices of the Vedic age to the soaring metaphysics of the Upanishads, from the world-spanning epics of the [Mahabharata](/article/mahabharata) and the [Ramayana](/article/ramayana) to the towering [temples](/article/hindu-temples) of the medieval south and the ecstatic devotional movements that swept the land, the tradition has continually renewed itself without ever discarding its past. The oldest layer and the newest still sit side by side.

What follows in this cluster is a journey through that inheritance: the scriptures that began it, the gods who fill it, the epics that taught it, the philosophy that deepened it, the temples that housed it, the disciplines that train the body and mind toward it, and finally the breathtaking vision of time and cosmos that frames it all. It is the story of how one of humanity's oldest peoples set out to map the whole of reality — and never stopped.
