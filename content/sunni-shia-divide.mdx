## A Question of Succession

When [Muhammad](/article/muhammad) died in the year 632, he left behind a flourishing community but no agreed plan for who should lead it. He had founded a faith, a state and a way of life — but the [Quran](/article/the-quran) did not name a successor, and the Prophet's own intentions were remembered differently by different people. Out of that single silence grew the deepest division in the history of Islam. It was not, at first, a quarrel about belief. Both sides held the same creed, prayed the same prayers, revered the same book. They differed over a question of authority: who had the right to lead the **umma**, the community of the faithful, in the Prophet's place.

One group held that the leader — the **caliph**, or "successor" — should be the most capable and pious man, chosen by the consensus of the community's elders. They accepted the succession of Muhammad's close companions: first **Abu Bakr**, the Prophet's old friend and father-in-law, then Umar, then Uthman, and finally Ali. These became known as the **Sunni**, from *ahl al-sunna*, "the people of the tradition" — those who follow the practice of the Prophet and the consensus of the community. Today they are the great majority, perhaps nine in ten of all Muslims.

The other group held that leadership belonged by right to the Prophet's own family — specifically to **Ali ibn Abi Talib**, Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law, and to Ali's descendants after him. They believed Muhammad had designated Ali as his heir, and that spiritual authority passed through this bloodline alone. These became the **Shia**, from *shiʿat Ali*, "the party of Ali."

:::note A divide of history, not of God
It is worth saying clearly: Sunni and Shia are both fully Muslim. They share the same God, the same Prophet, the same Quran, the same Five Pillars, the same direction of prayer toward Mecca. Their division is rooted in history — in who should have led, and in the events that followed — far more than in the fundamentals of belief. Across most of Islamic history Sunni and Shia have lived side by side, intermarried and shared cities. The bitter sectarian conflicts of modern times are often as much about politics and power as about the ancient theological difference, much as the wars of the European [Reformation](/article/reformation) were never about doctrine alone.
:::

## Karbala and the Birth of Shia Memory

The division might have remained a quiet difference of opinion but for a tragedy that seared itself into Shia memory forever. Ali did at last become the fourth caliph, but his rule was contested and he was assassinated in 661, after which power passed to the **Umayyad** dynasty, whom the Shia regarded as usurpers. In 680, Ali's son **Husayn** — the Prophet's own grandson — rose against the Umayyad caliph and was surrounded with his small band of followers on the plain of **Karbala**, in what is now Iraq. There, cut off from water and hopelessly outnumbered, Husayn and nearly all his companions were killed.

The martyrdom of Husayn at Karbala became the defining event of Shia Islam — a story of righteous suffering against worldly injustice that Shia Muslims relive every year in the mourning rituals of **Ashura**, with processions, weeping and passion plays. Where Sunni Islam tended to make its peace with whatever ruler held power, Shia Islam carried forward a powerful sense of righteous resistance to unjust authority, born at Karbala.

> Every day is Ashura, and every land is Karbala.

That Shia saying captures how the memory of Husayn's sacrifice became a permanent moral lens — every struggle against injustice an echo of that first one.

## Two Traditions Take Shape

Over the centuries the two branches developed into fully distinct traditions. The Shia came to venerate a line of **Imams** — for the largest Shia group, the "Twelvers," a succession of twelve divinely guided descendants of Ali, the last of whom they believe did not die but went into a hidden state, the **occultation**, and will return at the end of time as the **Mahdi** to fill the world with justice. This gave Shia Islam a distinct religious leadership of learned clerics who interpret the faith in the Imam's absence — a structured hierarchy with no real equivalent in Sunni Islam.

The Sunni, for their part, developed their authority through the consensus of scholars (*ulama*) and the great schools of religious law, grounding the faith in the Quran and the collected traditions of the Prophet. Each branch produced its own collections of hadith, its own schools of jurisprudence, its own theologians and saints. Both shared fully in the brilliance of the [Islamic Golden Age](/article/islamic-golden-age); Shia dynasties such as the Fatimids of Egypt founded great cities and centres of learning, and the divide ran through scholarship and philosophy as through everything else.

:::insight An old pattern of faith
The shape of this division — a community splitting over who holds rightful authority after the founder, and over how that authority is transmitted — is one religions know well. The [early Christian church](/article/early-church) wrestled for centuries over the authority of bishops and the see of Rome, divisions that hardened into the lasting split between East and West. In both faiths the deepest fractures grew not from disagreement about God but from the human question of who speaks for Him. To trace the Sunni–Shia divide is to watch a universal drama of religious history play out in Islam's own distinctive key.
:::

## The Divide in the Modern World

Today Sunnis make up roughly 85 to 90 percent of the world's Muslims, with Shia communities concentrated in Iran — where Twelver Shiism is the state religion — and in Iraq, Bahrain, Azerbaijan, Lebanon and parts of the Gulf and South Asia. For most of history the two have coexisted far more often than they have fought, sharing the same lands and the same daily faith. But in the modern era the old religious difference has at times been inflamed by political rivalry between states and movements, turning an ancient theological distinction into a marker of contemporary conflict.

To understand [Islam today](/article/islam-today), one must hold both truths together: that the divide is real and runs deep into the earliest days of the faith, and that what unites Sunni and Shia — the one God, the one Prophet, the one Quran, the one direction of prayer — is far greater than what divides them. The split that began with a question of who should lead has shaped fourteen centuries of history, yet it has never broken the underlying unity of a faith now shared by nearly two billion souls.
