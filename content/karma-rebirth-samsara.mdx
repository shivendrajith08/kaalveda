## The Great Round

Long before the [Buddha](/article/buddha), the religious imagination of India had arrived at a sweeping vision of time and existence: **saṃsāra**, "the wandering on." Life, on this view, is not a single span between one birth and one death but a vast, wheeling repetition — beings die and are born again, and again, across countless lifetimes and countless forms, high and low, human and animal, heavenly and hellish. The wheel has no discoverable beginning, and left to itself it has no end.

Buddhism took up this picture and made it the backdrop of everything. To be caught in saṃsāra is to be subject, endlessly, to the ageing, sickness and loss that the [First Noble Truth](/article/four-noble-truths) names as dukkha. The wheel is not a comfort but a predicament — the very thing the whole path exists to break. And what keeps it turning is **karma**.

## Karma — Action and Its Ripening

**Karma** means, quite simply, "action" — but the Buddha gave the word a sharp inward turn. In the older ritual religion of the [Vedas](/article/vedas), *karman* had meant sacred action, the correct performance of sacrifice. The Buddha relocated it entirely into the moral will:

> It is intention, O monks, that I call karma; having willed, one acts through body, speech and mind.

Karma, for Buddhism, is the moral quality of our **intentional** deeds — and every such deed leaves a trace, a tendency, that ripens sooner or later into an experience of a corresponding kind. Acts rooted in generosity, kindness and clarity ripen toward wellbeing; acts rooted in greed, hatred and delusion ripen toward suffering. This is not a system of reward and punishment handed down by a judge, but something more like a law of moral gravity, as natural and impartial as the way a seed grows into its own kind of plant.

:::note Not fate, but momentum
Karma is often misheard in the West as fatalism — "it was their karma" — but Buddhism teaches almost the reverse. Karma is not a destiny fixed in advance; it is the accumulated momentum of past choices, which the present choice can always redirect. The point of understanding karma is empowerment, not resignation: because you are shaped by what you intend and do, you can, by intending and doing differently, reshape what you are becoming. The whole [Eightfold Path](/article/noble-eightfold-path) is, in one sense, the deliberate cultivation of wholesome karma.
:::

## The Six Realms

Driven by karma, beings are reborn across a spectrum of existences that Buddhist cosmology maps as **six realms**, often painted on the great Wheel of Life. There are the **gods**, blissful but complacent; the jealous **demigods**, consumed by rivalry; the **humans**, uniquely poised between pleasure and pain; the **animals**, ruled by instinct and fear; the **hungry ghosts**, tormented by cravings they can never satisfy; and the beings of the **hells**, suffering the fruit of cruelty. These realms echo, and elaborate, the layered cosmos of [Hindu cosmology](/article/hindu-cosmology), with its long ascents and descents through many worlds.

Yet all six are within saṃsāra, and none is a final home — even the heavens end when the good karma that bought them runs out, dropping the god back into the round. Precisely because it balances comfort and suffering, the **human birth** is prized as the rarest opportunity: the one realm clear-headed enough to hear the teaching and disciplined enough to practise it, and so the one realm from which the wheel can be left behind.

## Rebirth Without a Soul

Here Buddhism makes its most radical and subtle move, and parts company with the [Hindu philosophy](/article/hindu-philosophy) that shaped it. The Upanishads had placed at the centre of the wheel an eternal, unchanging self — the **atman** — which passes from body to body like a traveller changing clothes. The Buddha denied it. His teaching of **anatta**, "no-self," holds that nowhere in a person is there any permanent, independent essence; there is only a flowing process of body, sensations, perceptions, mental formations and consciousness, arising and passing moment by moment.

But if there is no soul, *what* is reborn? The Buddha's answer is that nothing is *transferred*, yet the process **continues** — as one candle lights another. The new flame is not the old flame, nor is it wholly different; it is caused by it, carrying its character forward. So it is with rebirth: the karmic momentum of one life conditions the arising of the next, without any thing crossing the gap. There is rebirth, but no one who is reborn — a stream of becoming with no fixed swimmer in it.

## The Twelve Links

To explain exactly how the wheel keeps turning, the Buddha traced a chain of causes called **dependent origination** — twelve links running from ignorance through craving and clinging to birth, ageing and death, each conditioning the next. Its meaning is both grand and practical: suffering is not imposed from outside but *arises through conditions*, and therefore, if the conditions are removed, it can cease.

The engine at the very hub of the wheel is named by three animals chasing each other's tails: a **cock, a snake and a pig** — **greed, hatred and delusion**, the "three poisons" that power all of saṃsāra. Weaken these, and the wheel slows. Extinguish them, and it stops.

## Stepping Off the Wheel

This is the whole point. Saṃsāra, karma and rebirth are not offered as a doctrine to be admired but as the map of a prison — and Buddhism is, above all, a way out. Where the older Indian traditions called that liberation **moksha**, release of the eternal self into the infinite, Buddhism calls it **[Nirvana](/article/nirvana-enlightenment)**: the going-out of the three poisons, the cessation of the craving that turns the wheel, the end of rebirth itself.

To understand the wheel of becoming, then, is to understand the stakes of the [Buddha's path](/article/noble-eightfold-path). Every mindful breath, every act of kindness, every loosening of a craving alters the karma that shapes what comes next — and, followed to its end, brings the wandering-on at last to rest.
