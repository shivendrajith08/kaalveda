## What Makes a Planet

A planet, as the International Astronomical Union defines it, is a body that orbits the Sun, is massive enough for its own gravity to pull it into a round shape, and has cleared its orbital path of other debris. By that measure the Solar System has exactly **eight** planets. They fall into two clear families, a divide inherited from how the [Solar System](/article/the-solar-system) formed.

The four **terrestrial planets** — Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars — are small, dense, and rocky, with solid surfaces you could stand on. The four **giant planets** — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune — are vast balls of gas and ice with no real surface at all, surrounded by rings and swarms of moons.

:::insight The order, and a way to remember it
Outward from the Sun: **Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune**. A classic mnemonic is "My Very Educated Mother Just Served Us Nachos." Note that the largest planets sit farthest out — a consequence of ice being abundant only in the cold outer Solar System.
:::

## The Rocky Inner Worlds

**Mercury**, closest to the Sun, is the smallest planet — a cratered, airless ball not much larger than the Moon. With almost no atmosphere to hold heat, it swings from scorching day to freezing night more violently than any other planet.

**Venus** is Earth's near-twin in size, but a hellish one. A thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide traps heat in a runaway **greenhouse effect**, making its surface hotter than Mercury's — around 465°C, hot enough to melt lead — beneath clouds of sulphuric acid. It is a sobering example of how a climate can go catastrophically wrong.

**Earth** is, so far, unique: the only known world with liquid water on its surface, a protective magnetic field, a breathable atmosphere, and life. Its single large [Moon](/article/the-moon) stabilises its tilt and drives the tides.

**Mars**, the rusty red desert world, is the most Earth-like of our neighbours and the great focus of exploration — a planet that once had rivers and lakes, and the most likely place to search for past life. Its story is told in full in [Mars](/article/mars).

## The Giants of the Outer System

Beyond the asteroid belt, the planets become enormous.

- **Jupiter** is the king — more than twice as massive as all the other planets combined. This banded gas giant is wrapped in storms, the greatest of which, the **Great Red Spot**, has raged for centuries and is wider than the Earth. Its powerful gravity helps shield the inner planets from stray comets.
- **Saturn** is famous for its breathtaking **rings**, a glittering system of countless ice and rock particles. For all its size, Saturn is so light for its volume that it would float in water.
- **Uranus** is an ice giant tipped onto its side, rolling around the Sun at an extreme tilt that gives it decades-long seasons. Its pale blue-green colour comes from methane in its atmosphere.
- **Neptune**, the most distant planet, is a deep blue world of supersonic winds — the fastest in the Solar System — so far from the Sun that one of its years lasts 165 of ours.

## Worlds Within Worlds

The giant planets are each surrounded by their own miniature systems of moons, some of which are among the most intriguing places in the Solar System. Jupiter's **Europa** and Saturn's **Enceladus** appear to hide liquid-water oceans beneath icy crusts; Saturn's **Titan**, larger than the planet Mercury, has a dense atmosphere and seas of liquid methane. If life exists elsewhere in the Solar System, these ocean moons are among the leading places to look.

:::note Where did Pluto go?
For 76 years Pluto was counted as the ninth planet. In 2006 it was reclassified as a **dwarf planet**, because it has not cleared its orbit — it is one of many icy bodies in the Kuiper Belt. The change reflected a deeper discovery: that the Solar System holds whole populations of small worlds Pluto belongs to.
:::

## Planets Beyond the Sun

For most of history, the eight planets were the only ones we knew. That changed in **1995**, when astronomers detected the first planet orbiting another Sun-like star — a discovery later honoured with a Nobel Prize. These worlds around other stars are called **exoplanets**, and we have now confirmed thousands of them, with billions more thought to exist across the Milky Way.

The variety is staggering: scorching "hot Jupiters" that hug their stars, vast "super-Earths" with no analogue at home, and rocky worlds orbiting in the **habitable zone**, where temperatures might allow liquid water. Each discovery widens the question that the planets first raise: how common are worlds like Earth, and might any of them be home to life?

## A Gallery of Possibilities

Taken together, the planets are a natural laboratory. Venus shows what a runaway greenhouse can do; Mars shows a world that lost its water and air; Jupiter shows how a giant can shepherd and protect its neighbours; the ocean moons hint that habitable conditions may be far more widespread than once believed.

Studying our own eight worlds is what lets us read the billions being found elsewhere — and it shapes our ambitions for the journey ahead, from robotic explorers to human footprints on other worlds, the subject of [space missions](/article/space-missions) and [the future of space](/article/the-future-of-space).
