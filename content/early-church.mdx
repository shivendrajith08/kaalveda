## A Movement Begins

The story of the early church opens, by its own account, in a single room in Jerusalem, where the followers of [Jesus](/article/jesus-of-nazareth) gathered after his death convinced that they had seen him alive. From that small beginning the movement spread with astonishing speed. The Acts of the Apostles tells of the festival of **Pentecost**, when the followers were said to be filled with the Holy Spirit and began to preach — and of thousands joining in a single day.

The crucial figure in carrying the message beyond the Jewish world was **Paul of Tarsus**, a former persecutor of the church who, after a dramatic conversion, became its tireless missionary. Across the middle decades of the first century he travelled the Roman roads to the cities of Asia Minor, Greece and finally Rome, founding congregations and writing them the letters that would become the oldest books of [the New Testament](/article/the-bible). It was Paul, more than anyone, who insisted that Gentiles need not become Jews to follow Christ — a decision that turned a Jewish sect into a universal faith.

:::insight A community that cared
What drew so many to the early church was not only its message but its life. In a hard world, Christians cared for the sick, fed the poor, buried the destitute, and welcomed slaves and free, women and men, to the same table. During the great plagues that swept the [Roman Empire](/article/roman-empire), Christians famously stayed to nurse the dying when others fled. To a watching world, the love these communities showed one another was itself an argument.
:::

## The Age of the Martyrs

For its first three centuries the church lived under the shadow of persecution. Rome was tolerant of many gods but suspicious of those who refused to honour the emperor's divinity or the gods of the [state cult](/article/roman-empire). Christians, who would worship only their one God, were branded atheists and traitors, blamed for disasters, and at intervals hunted, tortured and killed. The deaths of the **martyrs** — the word means simply "witnesses" — became the church's proudest memory.

> The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church.

The early Christian writer Tertullian's famous phrase proved true. Far from extinguishing the faith, persecution seemed to spread it. The courage of ordinary believers facing death in the arena unsettled their persecutors and drew the curious closer. Meanwhile Christian thinkers, trained in the methods of [Greek philosophy](/article/greek-philosophy), began to explain and defend the faith to the educated world, building a bridge between the gospel and the best learning of the age. Egypt's great city of [Alexandria](/article/ancient-egypt) became one of the foremost centres of Christian thought.

## Constantine and the Councils

The turning point came in **313**, when the emperor **Constantine**, who credited a vision of the cross with a battlefield victory, issued the **Edict of Milan** granting Christians freedom of worship. Within a lifetime the persecuted faith had become the favoured religion of the empire, and by the end of the century its official one. The change was staggering — and not without cost, as a church accustomed to suffering had now to learn the harder discipline of power.

With imperial backing came the need to settle what Christians actually believed. In **325** Constantine summoned bishops from across the empire to the **Council of Nicaea**, the first of the great ecumenical councils. There, against the teaching of Arius that the Son was a lesser, created being, the council affirmed that Christ was "of one being with the Father" — fully God. The **Nicene Creed** that grew from this council is still recited by Christians around the world.

:::note The shape of a church
By the time the Western [Roman Empire fell](/article/fall-of-rome) in 476, the church had become the one institution that survived the collapse. Its bishops, its scriptures, its monasteries and its structure of authority outlasted the emperors and carried Roman order, learning and law into the medieval world. In the East, the church flourished under the Byzantine emperors; in the West, the bishop of Rome — the pope — rose to fill the vacuum the legions left behind.
:::

## An Inheritance That Endured

The early church bequeathed to history far more than a set of beliefs. It created the creeds and the canon of scripture, the office of bishop and the rhythm of the Christian year, the monastery and the first hospitals and schools. It preserved much of the learning of the ancient world through the centuries that followed Rome's fall. Above all it proved that an idea held with total conviction, lived out in love and defended unto death, could outlast the mightiest empire on Earth. From that foundation the whole later history of [Christianity](/article/christianity) — its triumphs and its tragedies alike — would be built.
