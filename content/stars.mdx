## What a Star Really Is

A star is, at heart, a balancing act. Gravity is forever trying to crush a star inward, pulling its enormous mass toward the centre. What holds it up is the outward pressure of energy pouring from its core, where temperatures reach millions of degrees and atomic nuclei are fused together. As long as the fuel lasts, those two forces — gravity in, pressure out — stay in near-perfect equilibrium. A star is gravity held at bay by fire.

That fire is **nuclear fusion**. In the core of a star like the Sun, hydrogen nuclei are squeezed together so violently that they merge into helium, releasing a flood of energy in the process. A single star can convert millions of tonnes of matter into pure energy every second and barely notice, so vast are its reserves.

:::insight A star is a slow-motion explosion
Why doesn't a star simply blow itself apart? Because it is exquisitely self-regulating. If the core heats up, the star expands and cools; if it cools, it contracts and reheats. This thermostat keeps a star like the Sun shining steadily for billions of years.
:::

## Born in the Cold

Stars begin, paradoxically, in the coldest places in the galaxy: enormous clouds of gas and dust called **nebulae**. Where such a cloud grows dense enough, gravity takes over and pulls the gas into a collapsing knot. As the knot shrinks it spins faster and heats up, until the centre becomes a glowing **protostar**.

The moment of birth comes when the core finally grows hot enough — around ten million degrees — to ignite fusion. The new star switches on, blows away the surrounding cocoon of gas, and joins the long, stable middle age of its life. Stars are rarely born alone; a single nebula can hatch thousands of them in a great stellar nursery.

## A Star's Life Is Written in Its Mass

Almost everything about a star — its colour, its brightness, how long it lives, and how it dies — is decided at birth by one quantity: its **mass**. Astronomers sort stars by temperature and brightness on the famous **Hertzsprung–Russell diagram**, where most spend their lives along a band called the **main sequence**.

- **Low-mass stars** (red dwarfs) are cool, dim, and miserly with their fuel. They can shine steadily for tens or even hundreds of billions of years — far longer than the present age of the universe.
- **Sun-like stars** burn hotter and brighter, lasting around ten billion years before they swell and fade.
- **Massive stars** are blazing, blue-white, and reckless. They burn through their fuel in only a few million years and end in catastrophe.

Counter-intuitively, the biggest stars live the shortest lives. The more massive a star, the more fiercely it must burn to hold up its own weight, and the faster it exhausts itself.

## How Stars Die

When a star finally runs out of hydrogen in its core, its careful balance breaks down — and the ending depends, once again, on mass.

A star like the **Sun** will swell into a vast, cool **red giant**, large enough to swallow the inner planets. Eventually it sheds its outer layers in a glowing shell called a **planetary nebula**, leaving behind only its exposed, Earth-sized core: a **white dwarf** that will slowly cool over billions of years.

A **massive star** dies far more violently. Its core collapses in a fraction of a second and rebounds in a titanic explosion — a **supernova** — briefly outshining an entire galaxy. What remains at the centre is either an ultra-dense **neutron star**, a city-sized sphere so compact that a teaspoon of it would weigh billions of tonnes, or, if the star was heavy enough, a [black hole](/article/black-holes).

> A supernova is not only an ending. The blast wave compresses nearby gas clouds, triggering the birth of new stars — so each generation seeds the next.

## We Are Made of Star Stuff

The Big Bang produced almost nothing but hydrogen and helium. Every heavier element — the carbon in your cells, the oxygen you breathe, the calcium in your bones, the iron in your blood — was forged inside stars and flung into space when they died. The heaviest elements of all, like gold and platinum, are made in the most extreme events, including the collisions of neutron stars.

:::note Cosmic recycling
The atoms in your body have been through this cycle before. They were assembled in stars that lived and died billions of years ago, scattered across space, gathered into a new cloud, and built into the [Solar System](/article/the-solar-system). To know a star is to know your own origins.
:::

## Our Star, the Sun

For all its importance to us, the **Sun** is a thoroughly average star — a middle-aged yellow dwarf about 4.6 billion years old, roughly halfway through its life. It is so large that more than a million Earths could fit inside it, yet it is only one of perhaps 200 billion stars in the Milky Way galaxy, which is itself one of trillions of galaxies.

The Sun's steady output of light and warmth is the engine of nearly all life on Earth. Its gravity holds the [planets](/article/the-planets) in their orbits, and its eventual old age will reshape the Solar System entirely. The nearest other star, **Proxima Centauri**, lies more than four light-years away — so far that its light takes over four years to reach us, a vivid reminder of just how empty the space between stars really is.

## Reading the Night Sky

When you look up on a dark night, you are seeing stars at wildly different distances and ages, their light having travelled years, centuries, or millennia to reach your eye. Some of those points of light are cool red giants near the end of their days; others are hot blue stars that will not survive the age of human civilization. A few may have already died, their final light still in transit across the gulf of space.

Every star you can see belongs to our own Milky Way — and each is a sun, quite possibly with [worlds of its own](/article/the-planets) circling it. The study of stars is, in the end, the study of how the universe builds complexity out of simplicity: how clouds of the lightest gas became the furnaces that lit the cosmos and made everything else possible.
