## The Point of No Return

To understand a black hole, start with **escape velocity** — the speed something must reach to break free of a body's gravity. From Earth it is about 11 kilometres per second; from a more massive world it is higher. Now imagine packing so much mass into so small a space that the escape velocity climbs all the way to the speed of light. At that point, nothing can leave. Not a rocket, not a particle, not even light itself. That object is a black hole.

The boundary around it is called the **event horizon** — not a physical surface, but a line in space marking the point of no return. Cross it, and every possible path leads inward. From the outside, the horizon is perfectly dark, because no light can climb back out to reach your eye. This is why a black hole is black.

:::insight Black holes are not cosmic vacuum cleaners
A common myth is that black holes roam space sucking everything in. In truth, their gravity at a distance is no stronger than any other object of the same mass. If the Sun were replaced by a black hole of identical mass, Earth would keep orbiting exactly as it does now — it would simply go dark and cold. You only fall in if you get very close.
:::

## Predicted by Einstein, Found in the Sky

Black holes fell out of **Einstein's general theory of relativity**, which in 1915 reimagined gravity not as a force but as the bending of spacetime by mass. Within months, the physicist **Karl Schwarzschild** solved Einstein's equations and found they allowed for a region from which nothing could escape. For decades most scientists, Einstein included, doubted such things could really exist.

They were wrong. Over the twentieth century, astronomers found stars whirling around invisible companions, and gas blazing in X-rays as it spiralled toward something unseen. The evidence mounted until the existence of black holes became impossible to deny — and the once-fanciful name, popularised by the physicist **John Wheeler** in the 1960s, entered the language for good.

## How Black Holes Are Born

Black holes come in a range of sizes, formed in different ways.

- **Stellar-mass black holes** are the corpses of the most massive [stars](/article/stars). When such a star exhausts its fuel, its core collapses under its own gravity with nothing left to stop it, crushing several times the mass of the Sun into a region a few kilometres across.
- **Supermassive black holes** are the giants — millions to billions of times the Sun's mass — that sit at the centre of almost every large galaxy, including our own. How they grew so big so early in cosmic history is still being worked out.
- **Intermediate-mass black holes**, in between the two, are rarer and harder to find, but a growing number of candidates have been spotted.

At the heart of a black hole, our current theories predict a **singularity** — a point of seemingly infinite density where the known laws of physics break down. What truly happens there is one of the great unsolved problems of physics, awaiting a theory that can unite gravity with quantum mechanics.

## Falling In

Suppose you fell toward a black hole feet first. Because gravity grows so much stronger over even a short distance near the horizon, your feet would be pulled far harder than your head. You would be stretched into a long thin stream — a process astronomers cheerfully call **spaghettification**.

> To a distant observer watching you fall, something stranger happens: you would appear to slow down and freeze at the event horizon, your image reddening and fading forever, never quite seen to cross. Time itself runs differently in such extreme gravity.

The black hole does leave clues to its presence. Gas spiralling inward forms a flattened, superheated **accretion disk** that glows fiercely across the spectrum, and some black holes fire colossal **jets** of matter out along their poles at nearly the speed of light. We see the black hole not directly, but by the violence it works on everything around it.

## Hearing and Seeing the Invisible

For most of history, black holes were known only by inference. Then came two breakthroughs that let us detect them almost directly.

In **2015**, the **LIGO** observatories registered a faint quiver in their instruments — **gravitational waves**, ripples in spacetime set off by two black holes colliding more than a billion light-years away. Einstein had predicted such waves a century earlier and doubted they could ever be measured. We have now heard dozens of these cosmic collisions.

Then, in **2019**, the **Event Horizon Telescope** — a network of radio dishes spanning the globe and acting as one Earth-sized instrument — released the first true image of a black hole: a glowing ring of hot gas encircling the dark silhouette of the giant at the centre of galaxy **M87**. In **2022** the same team imaged **Sagittarius A***, the four-million-solar-mass black hole at the heart of our own Milky Way.

:::note A black hole in our own galaxy
The centre of the Milky Way, about 27,000 light-years from Earth, hosts Sagittarius A*. We are in no danger from it — we orbit it at a safe and stately distance, completing one lap roughly every 230 million years.
:::

## Not Entirely Black

In the 1970s, **Stephen Hawking** made a startling prediction: black holes are not perfectly black after all. Quantum effects at the very edge of the event horizon should cause them to leak a faint trickle of radiation — now called **Hawking radiation** — and, over unimaginable spans of time, to slowly evaporate.

For a black hole of stellar mass, this process is fantastically slow, taking far longer than the present age of the universe. But it means that even these eternal-seeming objects may have a final chapter, dwindling away in the deep future. That far-off fate, and the role black holes may play in the ultimate destiny of the cosmos, is part of the story explored in [the future of space](/article/the-future-of-space).

Black holes sit at the meeting point of the very large and the very small, of gravity and quantum theory — and so they remain among the most active frontiers in all of science. Each new detection, from a ripple in spacetime to a ring of fire, turns a little more of the unknown into the known.
