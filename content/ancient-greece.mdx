## A Civilization Without a Capital

Ancient Greece was never a country. There was no single Greek state, no capital, no king of all the Greeks. Instead there were hundreds of small, fiercely independent cities, each clinging to its own valley or island, each with its own laws, gods, coins, and dialect. What bound them together was not a government but a shared idea of themselves: they spoke the same language, worshipped the same Olympian gods, read the same poems of **Homer**, and gathered every four years at Olympia to compete as Greeks against Greeks.

The geography made it so. Mainland Greece is a crumpled land of mountains and narrow plains, its coastline broken into a thousand harbours and fringed by islands scattered across the Aegean like stepping-stones. A people penned into such small, separate pockets of farmland naturally formed small, separate communities. The Greek word for such a community was the **polis** — usually translated "city-state," though it meant something closer to the whole body of citizens bound together by custom and law.

:::insight The polis was the unit of Greek life
A polis was not just a place but a community of citizens who governed themselves. Out of the intense, face-to-face politics of these small cities — where every citizen could know the issues and many could speak — came the radical idea that ordinary people, not kings or priests, should rule themselves.

## From Dark Age to Golden Age

The Greek story did not begin in glory. An earlier civilization, the **Mycenaeans** of Agamemnon's legendary age, collapsed around 1200 BCE, and Greece sank into centuries so poor and illiterate that historians call them a Dark Age. Out of that darkness, slowly, the Greeks rebuilt — and around 800 BCE they borrowed an alphabet from the Phoenicians, founded colonies from Spain to the Black Sea, and began composing the poetry and politics that would change the world.

The high point came in the fifth century BCE, the **Classical Age**. Against all odds the Greek cities, led by Athens and Sparta, threw back two massive invasions by the Persian Empire. The confidence of that victory poured into a single extraordinary generation in Athens. In the span of a lifetime its citizens built the Parthenon, invented the theatre of tragedy and comedy, practised the world's first [democracy](/article/greek-democracy), and — in the questions of **Socrates** — turned [philosophy](/article/greek-philosophy) toward the examined human life.

> Man is the measure of all things.

The sophist Protagoras caught the spirit of the age in that famous phrase. The Greeks placed the human being, with all its reason and folly, at the centre of their art, their politics, and their thought — a turn of mind that the West has inherited ever since.

## Athens and Sparta

Two cities came to embody the poles of the Greek character. **Athens** was a democracy, a sea power, a place of restless talk, trade, art, and argument — open, brilliant, and unstable. **Sparta** was its opposite: a grim militarized state where boys were taken from their families to be raised as soldiers and the highest virtue was discipline in the service of the city. The Greeks themselves debated endlessly which model was better, and their rivalry erupted into the long, ruinous **Peloponnesian War** that exhausted both.

:::note A glory built on contradiction
The same Athens that invented democracy denied all political rights to women, resident foreigners, and a vast population of slaves. Greek freedom was real and revolutionary — and it was also partial, resting on the labour of the unfree. To study Greece honestly is to hold both truths at once.

## The Greek Inheritance

For all their quarrels, the Greeks left a legacy out of all proportion to their numbers. They were swallowed first by the empire of [Alexander the Great](/article/alexander-the-great), a Macedonian who carried Greek language and culture as far as India, and then by [Rome](/article/rome), which conquered Greece militarily but was itself conquered culturally — taking Greek gods, Greek art, and Greek learning as its own. Through Rome and later through the Renaissance, the Greek inheritance flowed into the foundations of Europe.

When we vote in an assembly, watch a drama, reason from evidence, admire a columned building, or compete in the Olympic Games, we are speaking a language first spoken on these rocky shores. Ancient Greece built no lasting empire of its own — and yet, through ideas alone, it conquered the imagination of the world. To trace its threads outward is to follow them into [Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt), into Rome, and into nearly every corner of the West that came after.
