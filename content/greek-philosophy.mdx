## The First Question

Around 600 BCE, in the Greek city of **Miletus** on the coast of Asia Minor, a man named **Thales** asked a question that changed history. Instead of saying that the world was made and ruled by gods, he asked what the world was actually *made of* — and answered, in effect, that everything came from a single underlying substance, which he guessed was water. He was wrong about the answer. But the question was revolutionary, because it sought an explanation in nature itself, by reason, rather than in the will of [the gods](/article/greek-mythology).

These first thinkers, the **Presocratics**, were the world's first scientists as much as its first philosophers. **Anaximander** imagined an infinite, boundless source of all things and even sketched a primitive evolution of life from the sea. **Pythagoras** found that the cosmos was written in numbers and musical ratios. **Heraclitus** declared that everything flows and nothing stays the same, while **Parmenides** argued the opposite, that change is an illusion and reality is one unchanging whole. **Democritus** proposed that all matter is built from tiny indivisible particles he called **atoms** — an astonishing guess, twenty-three centuries before physics confirmed it.

:::insight From myth to logos
The Greek achievement was the shift from *mythos* to *logos* — from story to reasoned account. The Presocratics insisted that the universe is intelligible, that it follows regular principles a human mind can grasp. That single assumption is the seed of all later [science](/article/the-big-bang).

## Socrates and the Examined Life

In fifth-century Athens, philosophy turned from the cosmos to the human soul. **Socrates** wrote nothing and claimed to know nothing, yet he transformed the subject. He wandered the marketplace cornering confident men — politicians, poets, generals — and asking them simple questions: What is justice? What is courage? What is good? Under his relentless questioning their certainties collapsed, revealing that they did not really know what they thought they knew.

> The unexamined life is not worth living.

That defiant claim, spoken at his trial, became the motto of philosophy itself. Socrates' method of probing question and answer — the **dialectic** — taught that truth is reached not by accepting authority but by testing every belief. It made him beloved by the young and dangerous to the powerful, and in **399 BCE** the Athenian [democracy](/article/greek-democracy) condemned him to death on charges of impiety and corrupting the youth. He drank the hemlock calmly, and his execution turned him into philosophy's first martyr.

## Plato and Aristotle

Socrates' greatest pupil, **Plato**, preserved his teacher's voice in brilliant written dialogues and built from it a vast philosophy of his own. Plato argued that the changing things we see are mere shadows of perfect, eternal **Forms** — that somewhere beyond the senses lies the true reality of Justice, Beauty, and the Good, which the mind alone can grasp. In his *Republic* he imagined an ideal state ruled by philosopher-kings, and he founded the **Academy**, the first true university, which endured for centuries.

Plato's own greatest pupil, **Aristotle**, then turned the tradition outward again, back toward the physical world. Where Plato looked to an ideal realm, Aristotle observed and classified the one in front of him — dissecting animals, cataloguing constitutions, laying down the rules of **logic** that governed reasoning for two thousand years. He wrote on physics, biology, ethics, politics, poetry, and rhetoric, an encyclopedic mind whose works became the backbone of medieval learning. And, as it happened, he was hired to tutor a Macedonian prince named [Alexander](/article/alexander-the-great).

:::note A chain of teachers
Socrates taught Plato; Plato taught Aristotle; Aristotle taught Alexander the Great. In four generations the Greek mind passed from the questioning of one barefoot Athenian to the conqueror of the known world — a lineage of teacher and pupil that shaped both thought and empire.

## How to Live

After Alexander, philosophy turned increasingly to a single urgent question: how should one live in an uncertain world? The **Stoics** taught that happiness lay in accepting what we cannot control and living according to reason and virtue. The **Epicureans** sought a quiet life of modest pleasure and freedom from fear. The **Cynics** mocked convention and wealth. These schools offered not just theories of the universe but practical philosophies of life, and they spread far beyond Greece.

When [Rome](/article/roman-engineering) conquered the Greek world, it absorbed Greek philosophy along with everything else, and Roman thinkers like Cicero, Seneca, and the emperor Marcus Aurelius wrote as devoted students of the Greeks. Through Rome, and later through the Islamic world and the European Renaissance, this inheritance flowed down to us. Nearly every question the West still argues over — about knowledge, justice, the good life, and the nature of reality — was first framed by these Greeks who decided that the world could be understood by thinking.
