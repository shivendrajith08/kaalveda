## The Questions Behind the Ritual

The earliest [Vedas](/article/vedas) were hymns of praise and sacrifice, addressed to gods who might grant rain and cattle. But somewhere around 800 BCE, in the forest schools of northern India, a new kind of voice begins to sound in the closing pages of the Vedas — the **Upanishads**. Here the questions turn inward and grow vast. What is the world really made of? What is the self that says *I*? What happens when the body dies? And is there anything that does not change, anything truly, permanently real beneath the flux of experience?

These were the same hungers that would later drive the first thinkers of [Greece](/article/greek-philosophy) to seek a single principle behind the changing world. But the Indian sages pursued them by a different route — not in the marketplace and the law court, but in meditation, in the silence won by the disciplines of [yoga](/article/yoga-and-meditation). The Upanishads are records of that inward exploration, dialogues between teachers and students, kings and sages, even a boy and the god of death.

:::insight Atman is Brahman
The supreme discovery of the Upanishads is summed up in four Sanskrit syllables: **tat tvam asi**, "that thou art." The **atman** — the innermost self, the silent witness behind all your thoughts — is not separate from **Brahman**, the infinite reality underlying the whole cosmos. They are one. To realise this, not as an idea but as a living experience, is **moksha**: liberation, the end of fear and the end of death. It is perhaps the boldest claim in the history of thought — that the ground of the universe is to be found by looking within.
:::

## The Six Visions

Out of these meditations grew, over the centuries, six great classical schools — the **darshanas**, or "viewpoints," each a complete system of reasoning about reality, knowledge and liberation. There was **Samkhya**, which analysed the cosmos into spirit and matter; **Yoga**, its practical partner, which mapped the disciplines of stilling the mind; **Nyaya**, a rigorous logic of debate and proof; **Vaisheshika**, an early atomism that broke the world into indivisible particles; **Mimamsa**, devoted to the meaning of ritual and scripture; and **Vedanta**, the "end of the Vedas," which took the Upanishads as its foundation and became the most influential of all.

What is striking is how *philosophical* this tradition was. These schools argued, refuted one another, built systems of logic and theories of perception, and debated across the centuries with Buddhists and Jains in a great civilisation-wide conversation about the nature of the real. India produced not one philosophy but a contending parliament of them.

> Lead me from the unreal to the real; lead me from darkness to light; lead me from death to immortality.

## Shankara and the One Without a Second

The summit of this tradition is usually placed in the eighth century CE, with the philosopher **Adi Shankaracharya**. In a life of barely thirty-two years he travelled the length of India, debated every rival school, wrote luminous commentaries on the Upanishads and the [Bhagavad Gita](/article/mahabharata), and founded monastic centres at the four corners of the land that endure to this day. His teaching is **Advaita Vedanta** — "non-dualism," the doctrine that there are not two realities but one.

For Shankara, only **Brahman** is finally real. The world of separate things, the sense that you are a distinct self gazing out at a world of others, is **maya** — not an illusion in the sense of being nothing, but a kind of misperception, like mistaking a coiled rope for a snake in the dusk. Wisdom is the moment the lamp is brought near and the snake is seen to have been a rope all along. There was never anything to fear, and never anything separate from the one. Other great teachers would later challenge him — **Ramanuja** and **Madhva** insisting that the soul and God remain forever distinct, that love requires a beloved who is truly other — and from that disagreement flowed the great devotional movements of the medieval age.

:::note Philosophy you can taste
Hindu philosophy never treated truth as something to be merely understood. Its systems all aimed at **moksha**, a transformation of the whole person, not just an adjustment of belief. Reasoning was a discipline of the soul, meant to end in direct experience — which is why philosophy and meditation, argument and contemplation, are in this tradition two halves of a single craft. To know the truth and to be freed by it were never meant to be separate things.
:::

## A Map of the Real

The reach of these ideas is hard to overstate. The doctrine of an unchanging reality behind appearances, of a deathless self, of liberation through knowledge, shaped the whole of Indian culture and travelled far beyond it, kindling the thought of philosophers and poets from the German idealists to the American transcendentalists. And in its grandest speculations — its vision of a single boundless reality underlying all the forms of the world — Hindu philosophy reaches toward the same horizon that modern [cosmology](/article/hindu-cosmology) approaches from the other side, as physics traces every galaxy and atom back to a single origin in [the Big Bang](/article/the-big-bang). Two very different searches for the one thing that underlies everything, conducted three thousand years and half a world apart.
