## Born from a Cloud

About 4.6 billion years ago, in an unremarkable part of the Milky Way, a giant cloud of gas and dust — perhaps nudged by the shockwave of a nearby [supernova](/article/stars) — began to collapse under its own gravity. As it fell inward it spun faster and flattened into a swirling disk, with most of the material piling up at the centre.

When that central mass grew hot and dense enough, fusion ignited and the **Sun** switched on. The leftover material in the surrounding disk clumped together grain by grain, building first into pebbles, then boulders, then worlds. Everything in the Solar System — every planet, moon, asteroid, and comet — condensed from that same primordial disk, which is why they all orbit in roughly the same plane and the same direction to this day.

:::insight One star, almost all the mass
The Sun contains about 99.86 percent of all the mass in the Solar System. Everything else — all eight planets, every moon, and every asteroid combined — makes up barely a tenth of one percent. The Solar System is, overwhelmingly, the Sun plus some rounding error.
:::

## A System in Two Halves

The Solar System divides neatly into an inner and an outer region, separated by the asteroid belt. The split is a direct legacy of how it formed: close to the young Sun it was too hot for ices to survive, so only rock and metal could condense, while farther out, beyond the **frost line**, ice was abundant and worlds could grow huge.

- **The inner Solar System** holds the four small, rocky **terrestrial planets** — Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars — dense balls of rock and iron.
- **The asteroid belt** lies between Mars and Jupiter: a band of rocky debris, the rubble of a planet that never formed, kept stirred up by Jupiter's gravity.
- **The outer Solar System** is the realm of the **giant planets** — Jupiter and Saturn, the gas giants, and Uranus and Neptune, the ice giants — each far larger than Earth and wreathed in moons and rings.

These worlds, and what makes each one distinct, are the subject of [the planets](/article/the-planets).

## The Sun: Engine and Anchor

The **Sun** is the heart of it all. Its gravity holds every planet in its orbit, and its light and warmth drive the weather, the seasons, and almost all life on Earth. A stream of charged particles called the **solar wind** blows constantly outward, carving a vast protective bubble — the **heliosphere** — around the entire planetary system.

The Sun is a middling [star](/article/stars), roughly halfway through a ten-billion-year life. For now it is remarkably steady, but it is not eternal. In several billion years it will swell into a red giant, scorching the inner planets and reshaping the Solar System for its final act — a long-term fate traced in [the future of space](/article/the-future-of-space).

## Moons, Rings, and Rubble

Planets are only the beginning. The Solar System teems with smaller bodies, many of them worlds in their own right.

More than **two hundred moons** orbit the planets, and some are astonishing places: Jupiter's **Europa** and Saturn's **Enceladus** hide oceans of liquid water beneath shells of ice; Saturn's **Titan** has rivers and lakes of liquid methane under a thick orange haze; and Earth's own [Moon](/article/the-moon) shaped the conditions for life on our planet.

Then there are the wanderers. **Asteroids** are rocky leftovers, concentrated in the belt but scattered widely. **Comets** are icy bodies that grow glowing tails when the Sun's heat boils off their surface, swinging in from the cold outer reaches on long, looping orbits.

:::note Why Pluto was reclassified
In 2006 the International Astronomical Union defined a "planet" as a body that, among other things, has cleared its orbital neighbourhood of other objects. Pluto shares its region with many similar icy bodies, so it was reclassified as a **dwarf planet** — a category it shares with Ceres, Eris, and others. It was not a demotion so much as a recognition that Pluto belongs to a whole new population of worlds.
:::

## The Distant Edges

Far beyond Neptune lies the **Kuiper Belt**, a broad ring of icy bodies — Pluto among them — that are frozen relics of the Solar System's birth. Farther still, perhaps a thousand times more distant, astronomers believe a vast spherical shell of icy debris called the **Oort Cloud** surrounds the whole system, marking the true outer boundary of the Sun's gravitational reach. It is so remote that a comet from there can take millions of years to complete a single orbit.

The scale is hard to grasp. If the Sun were the size of a beach ball, Earth would be a peppercorn some 25 metres away, and the edge of the Oort Cloud would lie kilometres distant. The Solar System is mostly empty space.

## Reaching Beyond

Only one human-made object has crossed the heliosphere into interstellar space: **Voyager 1**, launched in 1977, which slipped past the Sun's bubble in 2012 and now drifts among the stars, still faintly calling home. Its journey is the opening chapter of humanity's reach beyond our own neighbourhood — a story continued in [space missions](/article/space-missions).

For all our exploration, the Solar System remains the only place we have ever directly visited, the only star we have studied up close, and — so far — the only place known to harbour life. Understanding it is how we calibrate everything else: a single, well-mapped example against which we measure the billions of other planetary systems now being discovered across the galaxy.
