## The Knowledge That Was Heard

The word *veda* means simply **knowledge** — and to the tradition this was no ordinary knowledge but truth itself, not composed by human authors but *heard* by ancient seers, the **rishis**, in states of heightened vision. For this reason the Vedas are called **shruti**, "that which was heard," and they hold a unique authority: they are eternal, uncreated, the very sound of reality, transmitted through the rishis rather than invented by them.

The oldest and greatest of them is the **Rigveda**, a collection of more than a thousand hymns to the gods, likely taking shape around 1500 BCE among the Sanskrit-speaking peoples of the northwestern plains. Three further collections grew alongside it: the **Samaveda**, the same verses arranged as melody for chanting; the **Yajurveda**, the prose formulas a priest murmured as he performed the sacrifice; and the **Atharvaveda**, a more earthy gathering of spells, blessings and charms for healing and daily life. Together they form a complete liturgy for a religion built around the sacred fire.

:::insight A library carried in the mind
For perhaps a thousand years the Vedas were never written down — and were never meant to be. They were preserved by memory alone, recited by Brahmin families who learned not only the words but elaborate redundant chanting patterns that crisscrossed each syllable forwards, backwards and interwoven, so that no single error could survive. The result is one of the most accurate acts of transmission in human history: scholars believe the text we hear today is essentially identical to what was chanted three thousand years ago. UNESCO has recognised this living chant as a masterpiece of the heritage of humanity.
:::

## A World of Fire and Sacrifice

The religion of the Rigveda was vivid and elemental. Its gods were powers of the natural and moral world: **Agni**, the fire that carried offerings skyward; **Indra**, the storm-king who slew the serpent of drought and released the waters; **Varuna**, the stern guardian of cosmic order; **Surya**, the sun; **Ushas**, the radiant dawn. Worship centred on the **yajna**, the fire sacrifice, in which offerings were poured into the flames so that the gods might in turn grant rain, cattle, sons and victory. It was a religion of exchange and order, and it bears a family resemblance to the open-air ritual religions of the wider ancient world — the bargains with the gods kept by [Rome](/article/roman-religion) and the older reasoning seekers of [Greece](/article/greek-philosophy) who would, far to the west, begin their own search for first principles.

> There was neither non-existence nor existence then; there was neither the realm of space nor the sky beyond. What stirred? Where? In whose protection?

That hymn — the famous *Nasadiya Sukta*, the Hymn of Creation — shows the Vedas already reaching past ritual toward philosophy, daring even to wonder whether the gods themselves know how the world began.

## From Ritual to Insight

Each Veda grew over centuries into layers. After the hymns came the **Brahmanas**, dense manuals explaining the meaning of every ritual gesture, and the **Aranyakas**, the "forest texts" for hermits who had withdrawn from society. Finally, crowning the whole structure, came the **Upanishads** — and here the religion turned inward. Where the early hymns sought rain and cattle from the gods outside, the Upanishads sought the deathless reality within, asking what lies behind the sacrifice, behind the self, behind the cosmos. They are called the **Vedanta**, "the end of the Vedas," and from their meditations grew the whole tradition of [Hindu philosophy](/article/hindu-philosophy).

:::note Why the Vedas still matter
A modern Hindu may never read the Rigveda, yet its presence is everywhere. The sacred syllable **Om**, the fire still lit at weddings, the **Gayatri** verse murmured at dawn, the very claim that there is one truth behind many gods — all descend from these hymns. To call a teaching *Vedic* is still, after three thousand years, to call it true. The Vedas are not a closed museum of an ancient faith but the root from which a living tradition continues to draw.
:::

## The Root and the Tree

It is hard to overstate what the Vedas began. They gave Hinduism its sacred language, Sanskrit; its central ritual, the fire offering; its first gods and its first questions. They established the idea of revelation as something heard rather than written, and the practice of guarding truth through flawless recitation. And in their final pages, in the soaring speculation of the Upanishads, they planted the seed of every later flowering — the [pantheon](/article/hindu-gods) that would grow ever richer, the epics that would carry the teaching to ordinary people, and the philosophy that would spend the next two thousand years unfolding a single overwhelming intuition: that the self and the cosmos are, at the deepest level, one.
