## The Long Aftermath

When Cleopatra VII died in 30 BCE, Egypt became a province of **Rome**, then of **Byzantium**. In 641 CE the **Arab conquest** brought Islam and the Arabic language, which gradually became the country's faith and tongue. Over the centuries Egypt was ruled from afar and up close — by the Fatimids and Mamluks, by the Ottomans, and after Napoleon's brief expedition, increasingly under European influence.

## Building the Modern Nation

Two great engineering projects reshaped the country:

- The **Suez Canal** (opened 1869) cut a sea route between the Mediterranean and the Red Sea, making Egypt a strategic crossroads of world trade — and a target of imperial rivalry.
- The **Aswan High Dam** (completed 1970) finally tamed the annual Nile flood, generating power and securing water, but ending the natural cycle of silt that had fed Egyptian farming for five thousand years.

Egypt won full independence over the first half of the twentieth century, becoming a **republic in 1953**. Today it is the most populous Arab country and a cultural powerhouse of film, music, and scholarship.

:::note An identity that never broke
Few modern nations sit so directly on top of their ancient selves. Egyptians today live on the same river, beneath the same monuments, carrying a continuous sense of "Egypt" that stretches back five millennia.
:::

## The Past as a Living Resource

The legacy of [the pharaohs](/article/the-pharaoh) is not only a matter of pride; it is a pillar of the economy. The monuments of [ancient Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt) draw millions of visitors each year, making heritage one of the nation's great modern industries — the story we follow next, into [Egyptian tourism](/article/egypt-tourism).
