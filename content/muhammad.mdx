## An Orphan of Mecca

Muhammad was born around the year 570 in **Mecca**, a dusty trading town in the barren hills of western Arabia that was also a centre of pilgrimage, home to the ancient cube-shaped shrine called the **Kaaba**, then crowded with the idols of the Arabian tribes. His early life was marked by loss. His father died before his birth, his mother when he was six, and he was raised first by his grandfather and then by his uncle Abu Talib. He grew into a trusted merchant, known by the nickname **al-Amin**, "the trustworthy," and married a older widow and businesswoman named **Khadija**, who would become his first and most devoted supporter.

Arabia in his day stood at the edge of two great empires — the Christian Eastern Roman world to the north-west and the Zoroastrian Persian empire to the north-east — yet its own tribes worshipped many gods and were bound by no single faith or state. It was a society of fierce clan loyalty, blood feud and trade, where the weak had little protection. Into this world Muhammad would bring a message of one God and one community that cut across every tribal line.

:::insight The night of power
Tradition holds that around the year 610, when Muhammad was about forty, he had withdrawn to meditate in a cave on Mount Hira above Mecca when the angel Gabriel appeared and commanded him: **"Recite!"** Overwhelmed and terrified, he protested that he could not — and the words of the first revelation poured through him. Muslims call this the *Laylat al-Qadr*, the Night of Power, and reckon it the moment the [Quran](/article/the-quran) began to descend. For the rest of his life the revelations continued, and he proclaimed them not as his own words but as the speech of God.
:::

## The Preacher and His Persecution

Muhammad began to preach in Mecca a message of startling simplicity and danger: that there was only one God, that the idols of the Kaaba were nothing, that the rich owed justice to the poor and the orphan, and that all would be raised to face judgement. To the merchant aristocracy of Mecca, whose wealth and prestige flowed from the pilgrimage to those very idols, this was both blasphemy and a threat to their livelihood. His small band of followers — slaves, the young, the poor and a few of the well-born — were mocked, boycotted and persecuted.

His message had deep kinship with the faiths already in the region. Like the prophets of the [Hebrew Bible](/article/the-bible) and like [Jesus of Nazareth](/article/jesus-of-nazareth), Muhammad called people from idols to the one God of Abraham, warned of a coming judgement, and demanded mercy and justice. Muslims revere Jesus as a mighty prophet and the Messiah, born of the Virgin Mary, though they do not hold him to be divine. The God Muhammad preached was, in his teaching, the same God — the message restored to its original purity.

> Recite in the name of your Lord who created — created man from a clinging form. Recite! Your Lord is the Most Bountiful One, who taught by the pen, taught man what he did not know.

## The Hijra and the Birth of a Community

By 622 the pressure in Mecca had become unbearable, and Muhammad accepted an invitation from the town of **Yathrib**, to the north, to come and govern it. This migration — the **Hijra** — is the hinge of Islamic history, so decisive that Muslims count their calendar from that year, not from the Prophet's birth. Yathrib was renamed **Medina**, "the city," and there Muhammad became not only a prophet but a lawgiver, judge and head of state, binding the quarrelling clans and the growing body of believers into a single community, the **umma**.

The years that followed brought open war with Mecca, a series of hard-fought battles, and at last, in 630, the bloodless conquest of the city itself. Muhammad entered Mecca in triumph, cleansed the Kaaba of its idols, and rededicated it to the one God — restoring, as Muslims believe, the shrine that Abraham himself had first raised. The faith he had been mocked for now spread swiftly across the peninsula.

:::note Prophet, not divine
It is a point Muslims guard with great care: Muhammad is not worshipped, and is never held to be divine. He is the *seal of the prophets* — the final and greatest of a line stretching back to Adam — but he is a man, mortal and fallible in worldly matters, chosen to carry God's word. Where Christians came to confess Jesus as God incarnate, Islam draws an absolute line between the creator and every creature, however exalted. To honour Muhammad is one thing; to worship him would be the very idolatry he came to abolish.
:::

## A Life That Reshaped the World

Muhammad died in Medina in 632, only two years after the conquest of Mecca, leaving no clear successor — a silence that would soon open the great [Sunni–Shia divide](/article/sunni-shia-divide). He left behind no empire of stone, but something far more durable: a book held to be the words of God, a community that would within a century rule from Spain to India, and a model of life imitated in its smallest details by a quarter of humanity. He had united a peninsula, founded a religion and a civilization, and set in motion a transformation of the world. Unlike most founders of faiths, he did all this within the full light of recorded history, his life and sayings gathered, debated and preserved by those who knew him — which is why Muslims can follow not only what he taught but how he lived.
