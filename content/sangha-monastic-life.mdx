## The Third Jewel

When a Buddhist takes refuge, the third of the **Three Jewels** — after the [Buddha](/article/buddha) and the [Dharma](/article/buddhist-scriptures) — is the **Sangha**, the community. In its widest sense the word means all who follow the path; in its central and original sense it means the **monastic order** the Buddha founded on the afternoon of his first sermon, when the first of his five listeners asked to follow him. That order is the oldest continuously functioning institution on Earth, older than any surviving state or university, and it has been the living body through which Buddhism has travelled the centuries.

The Buddha built the Sangha with deliberate care, because he understood that a teaching survives only if a community keeps it. Monks and nuns would preserve the scriptures in memory, sustain the meditative disciplines, and embody, for everyone else, a visible proof that the path could actually be walked.

## Going Forth

To enter the Sangha is to **"go forth"** — the same phrase used of the Buddha's own [Great Renunciation](/article/buddha). The candidate shaves the head and beard, sets aside personal wealth and family life, dons the plain dyed robe — saffron and ochre in the south, maroon in Tibet, grey and black in East Asia — and takes up a life reduced to essentials. Traditionally a monk's possessions were few: three robes, an alms bowl, a razor, a needle, a water strainer, a belt. Everything not needed for the path was let go, so that nothing would feed the craving the path exists to end.

The Buddha admitted **women** to the order as well, founding the **bhikkhuni** (nun) Sangha at the request of his aunt and foster-mother Mahapajapati — a remarkable step in the ancient world, opening the full monastic path to women at a time when few traditions did. The lineages of fully ordained nuns endured for centuries, faded in some lands, and are being revived in others today.

:::note Monks, nuns, and laypeople
Buddhism is not only its monks. It rests on a **partnership** between the ordained Sangha and the lay community. Monastics keep the teaching and the discipline; laypeople sustain the monastics with food, robes, medicine and shelter. In giving that support, laypeople cultivate generosity and wholesome [karma](/article/karma-rebirth-samsara), while gaining teachers, ceremonies and a living example of the goal. Neither half could carry the tradition alone — the robe and the household have always leaned on each other.
:::

## The Vinaya — A Discipline of Freedom

The monastic life is governed by the **Vinaya**, the "basket of discipline" preserved in the [scriptures](/article/buddhist-scriptures). Its code — the *Patimokkha*, running to more than two hundred rules for monks and still more for nuns — regulates everything from the gravest offences to the smallest matters of eating, dress and courtesy. The rules were not handed down as a system but arose case by case, each in response to some incident in the early community, and each is remembered with its story.

Their purpose is not punishment but **simplicity and harmony**. By removing distraction, temptation and conflict, the Vinaya frees the mind for practice and holds the community together. Twice a month the monks of a district gather to recite the Patimokkha in full and to confess any lapses — a ritual of shared accountability that has kept the discipline alive, remarkably intact, for two and a half thousand years.

## The Shape of the Day

Monastic time has a steady, ancient rhythm. In the Theravada lands of Southeast Asia the day begins before dawn with chanting and meditation, followed by the **alms round**: monks walk barefoot and silent through the streets with their bowls, receiving whatever food households offer, and eat their single main meal before noon, taking nothing solid after. The rest of the day turns on study, teaching, chores, and long stretches of **meditation**.

That meditation is the disciplined heart of the whole life. Here the Sangha keeps alive the Buddha's science of the mind — the cultivation of calm (*samatha*) and insight (*vipassana*), the training of attention prescribed by the [Eightfold Path](/article/noble-eightfold-path). The tradition draws on, and refines, the older Indian arts of [yoga and meditation](/article/yoga-and-meditation) from which it partly grew, but bends them toward its own goal: not union with a cosmic self, but the clear seeing that loosens craving and leads toward [Nirvana](/article/nirvana-enlightenment).

Once a year, during the monsoon, monks observe the **rains retreat** (*vassa*), settling in one place for three months of intensified practice — the origin of the settled monastery itself, and, in some countries, of the custom by which young men ordain temporarily for a season before returning to lay life.

## Forest and Town

From early times two vocations grew within the Sangha, and the tension between them is fruitful still. Some monks are **town-** or **study-dwellers**, living in settled monasteries near the lay community, mastering the texts, teaching, and performing the ceremonies that mark a Buddhist society's births, deaths and festivals. Others are **forest-dwellers**, withdrawing to the wilderness to give themselves wholly to meditation, seeking awakening in solitude. The great monasteries preserved the learning; the forest hermitages renewed the practice — and the most revered teachers have often come from the forest to remind the whole tradition what the texts are *for*.

## The Vessel of the Teaching

> Well-gone is the Sangha of the Blessed One's disciples, practising the good way — worthy of gifts, worthy of hospitality, an unsurpassed field of merit for the world.

That verse, recited daily across the Buddhist world, states the community's role exactly: it is the *field* in which the teaching is planted, tended and passed on. Every scripture that survives, every meditation lineage still practised, every [school](/article/buddhist-branches) that carried the Dharma across Asia was borne by this community of shaved heads and dyed robes. When Buddhism [spread](/article/spread-of-buddhism) from India to Sri Lanka, Tibet, China and Japan, it spread as the Sangha spread — monk by monk, monastery by monastery. The Buddha built no temple to outlast him. He built a community, and it has outlasted empires.
