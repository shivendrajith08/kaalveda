## A Civilization in Search of Knowledge

When the Arab conquests of the seventh century swept across the old lands of the [Roman](/article/roman-empire) and Persian empires, they did not destroy the learning they found there — they absorbed it. The new Muslim rulers inherited the scholarly traditions of Greek Alexandria, Persian Gondishapur and Indian Ujjain, and rather than burning them they set about translating them. A famous saying attributed to the Prophet — "Seek knowledge, even unto China" — and the [Quran's](/article/the-quran) repeated calls to observe the wonders of creation gave the pursuit of learning the highest religious sanction. To study the world, Muslim scholars held, was to honour the One who made it.

The decisive moment came under the **Abbasid** caliphs, who in 762 founded a new capital at **Baghdad**. There, around the year 800, the great translation movement reached its height in an institution remembered as the **House of Wisdom** (*Bayt al-Hikma*), where scholars of every faith — Muslim, Christian, Jewish, Zoroastrian — worked side by side rendering the masterworks of antiquity into Arabic. The dialogues of Plato, the logic and science of Aristotle, the geometry of Euclid, the medicine of Galen, the astronomy of Ptolemy and the mathematics of India all flowed into a single language and a single conversation.

:::insight The bridge across the dark
This is the Golden Age's quiet gift to the whole world. After the [fall of the western Roman Empire](/article/fall-of-rome), much of the learning of [Greece](/article/greek-philosophy) was lost to Latin Europe — but it was not lost to the world, because the scholars of Islam had preserved and expanded it. Centuries later, as that knowledge passed back into Europe through Muslim Spain and Sicily, it helped ignite the medieval revival of learning and, in time, the Renaissance. Without the libraries of Baghdad and Córdoba, much of what we know of Aristotle and the Greek scientists might never have survived. Islam was the bridge across which the ancient world reached the modern one.
:::

## The New Sciences

The Muslim scholars were never mere copyists. They took what they inherited and drove it forward. In mathematics, the Persian scholar **al-Khwarizmi**, working in ninth-century Baghdad, wrote a treatise whose title gave the world the word **algebra** (*al-jabr*) and whose own Latinised name gave us **algorithm**. From Indian mathematicians the Muslim world took the place-value decimal system and the symbol for zero, refined them, and passed them to Europe as the "Arabic numerals" with which nearly all the world now counts.

In medicine, the great physician **Ibn Sina** — known to Europe as Avicenna — composed the *Canon of Medicine*, an encyclopedia so thorough it remained a standard textbook in European universities for six hundred years. **Al-Razi** distinguished smallpox from measles and wrote on the ethics of the physician; surgeons in Córdoba designed instruments still recognisable today. In astronomy, observatories from Baghdad to Samarkand refined Ptolemy's models and produced star charts so accurate that the names of many stars we use — Aldebaran, Betelgeuse, Vega — remain Arabic to this day. **Ibn al-Haytham** (Alhazen), studying the nature of light, insisted that theories be tested against careful observation, and is counted by many historians among the first true experimental scientists.

> The ink of the scholar is more sacred than the blood of the martyr.

That saying, long attributed to the tradition, captures the spirit of the age — a civilization that prized the seeker of knowledge above almost any other.

## The Splendour of the Cities

This learning was sustained by wealth and order on a scale Europe could not then match. Baghdad in its prime may have been the largest city on Earth, its markets fed by trade routes reaching to China and the East Indies. Paper-making, learned from Chinese prisoners, spread west through the Muslim world centuries before Europe, making books — and so learning — vastly cheaper. Far to the west, in Muslim **Spain**, the city of **Córdoba** rose in the tenth century to perhaps half a million people, with paved and lighted streets, public baths, and a library said to hold hundreds of thousands of volumes, at a time when the greatest libraries of Christian Europe counted their books in the hundreds.

:::note A meeting of inheritances
The genius of the age lay partly in its openness. In the translation houses and observatories, the rational inquiry of the [Greeks](/article/greek-philosophy), the medical and astronomical traditions of Persia, the mathematics of [India](/article/hindu-philosophy), and the new energy of Arab and Islamic thought met and cross-fertilised. Jewish and Christian scholars worked alongside Muslims; the philosopher-physician Maimonides, a Jew of Córdoba, wrote in Arabic. It was a civilization confident enough to seek truth wherever it was found, and to make of many inheritances something new.
:::

## The Long Twilight

No golden age lasts forever. The Abbasid centre weakened, riven by political fracture; the Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 destroyed the city and its libraries, and the books of the House of Wisdom were said to have turned the Tigris black with ink. In Spain, the slow Christian reconquest closed in on the last Muslim kingdoms. The flame of inquiry did not go out at once — it burned on in places for centuries more — but its great age passed. Yet its legacy was permanent. The numerals on every clock and page, the words algebra and algorithm and chemistry, the survival of Greek thought, the very practice of testing ideas against the world — all carry the fingerprints of the scholars who, for five luminous centuries, made Arabic the language of human knowledge.
