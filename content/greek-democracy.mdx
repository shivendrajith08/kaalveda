## A Word the World Did Not Have

Before Athens, no language had a word for rule by the people. Every great society had been governed by kings, priests, or warrior nobles, and the idea that the common run of citizens might rule themselves would have struck most of the ancient world as absurd or dangerous. The Greeks coined the word for it: **demokratia**, from *demos*, "the people," and *kratos*, "power" — the power of the people.

It did not arrive all at once. Athens, like its neighbours, had been ruled by aristocrats, and the road to democracy ran through a long struggle between the rich few and the poorer many. The lawgiver **Solon** eased the crisis around 594 BCE by cancelling debts and forbidding the enslavement of citizens for debt. But the decisive break came in **508 BCE**, when **Cleisthenes**, reorganising the citizen body to break the power of the old noble clans, gave Athens the institutions that would make it a democracy.

:::insight Direct, not representative
Athenian democracy was not like ours. There were no elected politicians ruling on the people's behalf. Citizens governed in person — debating and voting directly on laws, wars and budgets in a great open-air assembly. It was the most direct democracy the world has ever seen.

## How the Assembly Worked

The beating heart of the system was the **Ekklesia**, the Assembly, open to every adult male citizen. Forty times a year, thousands of men climbed the hill of the **Pnyx** to debate the business of the state. Any citizen could rise and speak; the people voted by a show of hands; and their decision was final. They declared war, made peace, set taxes, and put generals on trial — directly, with no monarch or senate able to overrule them.

Because the Greeks distrusted the corrupting effect of permanent officials, most public posts were filled not by election but by **lottery**. The Council of Five Hundred, which prepared the Assembly's business, was chosen by lot and rotated yearly, so that over a lifetime an ordinary citizen might expect to take his turn helping to run the city. Juries, too, were vast and chosen by lot — hundreds of citizens deciding cases with no professional judge.

> Our constitution is called a democracy because power is in the hands not of a minority but of the whole people.

So the statesman **Pericles** declared in his famous funeral oration, recorded by the historian Thucydides. Under Pericles in the mid-fifth century, Athenian democracy reached its height, and even the poorest citizen was paid a small sum for jury service so that office would not belong only to the leisured rich.

## The Limits of Greek Freedom

For all its boldness, Athenian democracy was narrow by our standards. Citizenship was a jealously guarded privilege of free adult men of Athenian parentage — perhaps only a tenth or a fifth of the people who actually lived in Attica. **Women** had no political voice. **Resident foreigners**, however long their families had lived there, could not vote. And beneath the whole free population lay a great mass of **slaves** who did much of the labour and had no rights at all.

:::caution Democracy could also be cruel
The same sovereign people that built the Parthenon could be swayed by demagogues into reckless wars and brutal decisions. The Assembly once voted to execute every man of a rebel city, then reversed itself the next day. Direct democracy gave the people real power — and put real power at the mercy of the passions of the crowd.

## The Seed That Survived

Athenian democracy did not last long. It was crushed, restored, and finally extinguished as Greece fell to [Macedon](/article/alexander-the-great) and then to [Rome](/article/roman-republic), whose own republic chose a different path of elected magistrates and a powerful Senate. For two thousand years "democracy" was mostly a word of warning, a byword for mob rule.

Yet the idea never quite died. When the modern world began to imagine government founded on the consent of the governed, it reached back to this small Greek city for its vocabulary and its inspiration. The thinkers of [Greek philosophy](/article/greek-philosophy) had argued fiercely over whether democracy was wisdom or folly, and that argument is still ours. Every parliament, every ballot, every appeal to the will of the people carries an echo of the men who once gathered on a bare Athenian hillside to rule themselves.
