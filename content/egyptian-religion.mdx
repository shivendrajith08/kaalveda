## Maat: The Order of Things

At the root of Egyptian religion lay a single idea: **maat** — truth, justice, and cosmic balance. Maat was personified as a goddess wearing an ostrich feather, but it was really a principle: the rightness that held the universe together against **isfet**, the ever-pressing force of chaos. Gods upheld maat in the heavens; the pharaoh upheld it on Earth; and ordinary people upheld it by living honestly. Religion was less a matter of belief than of *keeping things in order*.

## A Living Pantheon

The Egyptians worshipped a sprawling, flexible family of gods whose roles overlapped and merged over the centuries:

- **Ra** — the sun, the supreme creative power, sailing his solar barque across the sky.
- **Osiris** — murdered, restored, and made lord of the dead, the promise of resurrection.
- **Isis** — his devoted wife, mistress of magic and the archetypal mother.
- **Horus** — their falcon-headed son, whose living embodiment was the king.
- **Anubis** — the jackal who oversaw embalming and guided the dead.
- **Thoth** — ibis-headed god of writing, wisdom, and the reckoning of time.

Gods could fuse (Amun-Ra, Ptah-Sokar-Osiris) as cities and priesthoods rose and fell. This was not contradiction but a theology comfortable with many faces of one truth.

:::insight The first recorded monotheism?
Around 1350 BCE the pharaoh **Akhenaten** swept the old gods aside in favour of a single deity, the **Aten** (the sun-disk). The experiment collapsed within a generation of his death — but it remains one of history's earliest attempts at an exclusive, single-god religion.
:::

## The Afterlife

Egyptian funerary religion was an elaborate technology of continuation. The dead faced judgement in the **Hall of Two Truths**, where Anubis weighed the heart against the feather of Maat while Thoth recorded the verdict. A balanced heart earned eternity in the **Field of Reeds**, an idealised Egypt; a heavy one was devoured by the monster Ammit, and the soul was annihilated.

> To make the passage safe, the living equipped their dead with spells — the **Pyramid Texts**, **Coffin Texts**, and later the **Book of the Dead** — alongside the preserved body itself.

That insistence on preserving the body leads directly to the Egyptian art of [mummification](/article/mummification), and the conviction that the dead lived on places the king — both god and man — at the very centre of the cosmos, as we see in the role of [the pharaoh](/article/the-pharaoh).
