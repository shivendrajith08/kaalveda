## The Speech of God

The very name *Quran* means **recitation**, and that single word holds the key to what the book is. Muslims do not regard it as a record of religious experience or a collection of human testimony, as Christians read [the Bible](/article/the-bible). They hold it to be the direct, uncreated speech of God — the eternal word, existing with God before time, sent down in the Arabic tongue through the angel Gabriel to [Muhammad](/article/muhammad), who recited what he received. The Prophet is the channel; the words are God's own. This is why the Quran's authority in Islam is even more absolute than scripture in most other faiths: to a Muslim, to hear it recited is to hear God speak.

The revelations came not all at once but piecemeal, over the twenty-three years of Muhammad's prophetic life — sometimes a few verses, sometimes a whole passage, answering a question, meeting a crisis, consoling the persecuted or laying down a law. The earliest passages, given in Mecca, are short, fiery and lyrical, full of the wonder of creation and the terror of judgement. The later ones, from Medina, are longer and more concerned with how a community should live. Together they form a book of 114 chapters, called **suras**, arranged not in the order they were revealed nor by theme, but roughly from the longest to the shortest.

:::insight A miracle of language
Islam holds that every prophet was given a miracle suited to his age — Moses his staff, Jesus his healings — and that Muhammad's miracle was the Quran itself. Its claim to be divine rests above all on its language: a beauty, rhythm and power of Arabic that Muslims hold no human author could match. The Quran challenges any doubter to produce even a single chapter like it — and the tradition is that none ever has. This is the doctrine of *i'jaz*, the inimitability of the Quran, and it is why the book has never truly been "translated." Renderings into other languages are called *interpretations*; the Quran itself exists only in Arabic.
:::

## How a Recitation Became a Book

The Quran was a spoken thing before it was a written one. Muhammad's followers learned the revelations by heart as they came, and recited them in prayer; some wrote fragments on parchment, palm leaves, stone and bone. The faith placed extraordinary value on memorisation, and the *hafiz* — one who holds the entire Quran in memory — remains an honoured figure to this day. After the Prophet's death, as the generation who had heard him began to die in the wars of expansion, the community feared the text might fracture, and under the early caliphs an authoritative written version was gathered and standardised. That text has been transmitted, Muslims hold, unchanged ever since.

This careful guarding of an oral revelation invites comparison with the [Vedas](/article/vedas) of India, preserved across a thousand years by flawless recitation before they were ever committed to writing. Both traditions trusted the trained human memory above the fragile written page, and both treated the *sound* of the sacred words — not merely their meaning — as holy. To recite the Quran beautifully, with the rules of *tajwid* governing every vowel and pause, is itself an act of worship.

> This is the Scripture in which there is no doubt, a guidance for those who are mindful of God.

That verse, near the very opening, announces the Quran's purpose: not poetry for its own sake, but guidance — a map for living rightly before God.

## A Book of Guidance and Law

The Quran ranges across the whole of life. It tells the stories of the prophets — Adam and Noah, Abraham and Moses, and Jesus and Mary, who are honoured in its pages — drawing on the same sacred history as the [Bible](/article/the-bible) yet retelling it in its own voice. It paints vivid pictures of paradise and hell, proclaims the oneness and mercy of God, and lays down rules for prayer, charity, marriage, inheritance, trade and war. From its verses, together with the example of the Prophet, the scholars of Islam built the **sharia**, the comprehensive law of Muslim life.

:::note Faith and reason
The Quran repeatedly calls on its readers to *think*, to observe the order of the heavens and the earth as signs of their maker. That summons helped fuel the great flowering of learning in the [Islamic Golden Age](/article/islamic-golden-age), when Muslim scholars, holding that the study of nature honoured its creator, eagerly absorbed the reasoning of [Greek philosophy](/article/greek-philosophy) and pushed it further. Where some traditions set faith against inquiry, the Quran's many verses urging reflection gave the early Muslim world a powerful warrant for the pursuit of knowledge.
:::

## The Living Heart of a Faith

No book is woven more deeply into the daily life of those who hold it sacred. The Quran is recited in the five daily prayers, chanted from minarets, inscribed in gold across the walls of mosques, studied in vast schools and whispered over the newborn and the dying. Its opening chapter, the *Fatiha*, is repeated by a Muslim more often than any other words in any language on Earth. Across fourteen centuries and a quarter of humanity, in lands that share neither tongue nor custom, the same Arabic verses are recited in the same way — a single book binding nearly two billion people to one another and, they believe, to the very voice of God.
