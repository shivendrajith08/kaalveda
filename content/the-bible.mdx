## A Library in One Volume

To open the Bible is to open a whole shelf of books bound between two covers. It contains law and history, poetry and proverb, prophecy and parable, letters and visions. Its writings were composed across more than a millennium, from ancient Israelite songs perhaps a thousand years before Christ to the last of the early Christian letters near the end of the first century. They were written in Hebrew, a little Aramaic, and Greek, by shepherds and kings, prophets and fishermen, the named and the anonymous. The very word *Bible* comes from the Greek *ta biblia*, "the books" — a plural quietly remembering that this one book was always many.

Christians divide it into two great parts. The **Old Testament** is the Hebrew scripture Jesus himself knew and quoted — the law of Moses, the histories of Israel, the Psalms, and the prophets. The **New Testament** gathers the writings of the first Christian generations: the four **gospels** that recount the life of [Jesus of Nazareth](/article/jesus-of-nazareth), the Acts of the Apostles, the letters of Paul and others, and the final visions of Revelation. Read together, Christians hold, they tell a single story — creation, fall, promise, and the redemption of the world.

:::insight One story in two testaments
Christians read the Old and New Testaments as a single arc: the promises made to Israel finding their fulfilment in Christ. Jews, who gave the world the Hebrew scriptures, read the same texts as complete in themselves, the living covenant of their people. The very same words are sacred to both faiths and heard in profoundly different ways — a reminder that a scripture lives not only in its ink but in the community that reads it.
:::

## How the Canon Was Made

No council handed down the Bible whole. Its **canon** — the list of which writings belong — formed slowly, by use and consensus, over centuries. The Hebrew scriptures were largely settled before the time of Jesus. The New Testament writings circulated first as separate letters and gospels among the scattered churches; only gradually did the church recognise which of them carried the authentic apostolic voice. By the late fourth century the list we now know was widely agreed, though the precise contents still differ a little between Catholic, Orthodox and Protestant Bibles.

Unlike the [Vedas](/article/vedas) of India, preserved for a thousand years by flawless oral recitation, or the vast oral epic of the [Mahabharata](/article/mahabharata), the biblical writings were committed early to scrolls and codices and copied, generation after generation, by hand. The discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls in the twentieth century — manuscripts two thousand years old — showed how faithfully scribes had carried the text across the ages.

> Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path.

That line from the Psalms captures how the book has been used: not merely studied but lived by, carried as a guide through grief and joy, exile and homecoming.

## The Book That Made and Remade the World

For most of Christian history the Bible was a rare and costly thing, copied by monks and read aloud in Latin to congregations who could not read it for themselves. The invention of printing changed everything. The **Gutenberg Bible** of around 1455 was the first great book of the printing age, and within a century the [Reformation](/article/reformation) had made the translation of scripture into the everyday languages of Europe a revolutionary act. To put the Bible into German, English or French was to hand ordinary people the right to read it for themselves — and the world was never the same.

:::note A book of many readings
The Bible has been read as law and as love letter, as literal history and as soaring metaphor. Some approach it as the inerrant word of God; others as a human library wrestling with the divine; many hold a view between. The Greek habit of reasoned argument, sharpened in [Greek philosophy](/article/greek-philosophy), shaped how Christians learned to interpret it. What unites its readers across every divide is the conviction that here, somehow, the eternal speaks.
:::

## Why It Endures

No book has been translated into more languages, printed in more copies, or argued over more fiercely than the Bible. It has inspired cathedrals and symphonies, abolitionists and tyrants, the comfort of the dying and the courage of reformers. Its phrases are woven so deep into the world's languages that millions quote it without knowing. Whatever one believes about its origin, its sheer reach is a fact of history — a library of ancient writings that became, for a quarter of humanity, the living voice of God, and for everyone else, one of the foundations of civilization itself.
