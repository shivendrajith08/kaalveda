## A Prince Schooled by Aristotle

Alexander was born in 356 BCE in **Macedon**, a half-Greek kingdom on the northern fringe of the Greek world that the southern cities regarded as rough and backward. His father, **Philip II**, was a brilliant soldier-king who reformed the Macedonian army into the deadliest fighting force in Greece and brought the quarrelling city-states under his control. To educate his gifted son, Philip hired the finest mind of the age — the philosopher **Aristotle**, himself a student of Plato.

For three years the boy was tutored in [philosophy](/article/greek-philosophy), science, medicine, and the poetry of Homer. Alexander is said to have slept with a copy of the *Iliad*, the epic of the [Trojan War](/article/trojan-war), beneath his pillow, modelling himself on its hero Achilles. When Philip was assassinated in 336 BCE, the twenty-year-old inherited a battle-hardened army and an unfinished ambition: to lead the Greeks in revenge against the Persian Empire that had once invaded their homeland.

:::insight A perfect weapon, ready to hand
Alexander's genius was real, but he also inherited a finished instrument. Philip had already forged the Macedonian phalanx — bristling with eighteen-foot pikes — and the elite cavalry that Alexander would hurl at the enemy's centre. The son took up a sword his father had sharpened.

## The Unbeaten Decade

In 334 BCE Alexander crossed into Asia and never lost a major battle. At the **Granicus**, at **Issus**, and finally at **Gaugamela** in 331 BCE, he shattered far larger Persian armies, twice putting the Great King **Darius III** to flight in person. Between these victories he marched down the Mediterranean coast, and in **Egypt** he was welcomed as a liberator, hailed as a pharaoh and a son of the god Amun, and founded the city of **Alexandria** that would become the intellectual capital of the ancient world.

With the Persian heartland in his hands, Alexander could have stopped. He did not. Driven by a longing the Greeks called *pothos*, he pressed on east — over the mountains of Afghanistan, across the rivers of Central Asia, and into **India**, where his exhausted men at last refused to go further. Reluctantly he turned back, leading part of his army on a terrible march through the desert that killed thousands.

> I am dying from the treatment of too many physicians.

The bitter remark attributed to him on his deathbed catches the strangeness of his end. In 323 BCE, back in **Babylon** and planning still greater campaigns, Alexander fell ill — perhaps from fever, perhaps from drink, perhaps from poison — and died at thirty-two. Asked to whom he left his empire, he is said to have answered only, "To the strongest."

## The Empire That Tore Itself Apart

There was no strongest. Alexander left no capable heir, and his generals — the **Diadochi**, or "Successors" — fell to fighting over the spoils. Within a generation his vast empire had splintered into rival kingdoms: the **Ptolemies** in [Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt), the **Seleucids** across Asia, the **Antigonids** in Macedon. The political achievement collapsed almost as fast as it had been built.

:::note Conquest as cultural fusion
Alexander founded dozens of cities, settled Greek soldiers across Asia, and even encouraged his men to marry Persian wives — taking a Persian bride himself. Whether from policy or vision, he set in motion a mingling of Greek and Eastern worlds that long outlasted his throne.

## A Longer Kind of Victory

What survived was not the empire but its culture. In the kingdoms of the Successors, Greek became the language of government, trade, and learning from the Nile to the Hindu Kush. This was the [Hellenistic Age](/article/hellenistic-age) — a world in which Greek art, science, and ideas fused with those of Egypt, Persia, and beyond, and reached more people than the classical city-states ever had.

Centuries later, [Rome](/article/roman-empire) would inherit that Greek-speaking eastern world wholesale, and ambitious Romans from [Julius Caesar](/article/julius-caesar) onward measured themselves against Alexander's legend. He became the eternal model of the conqueror: the young king who wept, the story goes, because there were no more worlds to conquer — and who, by dying so soon, never had to grow old or be defeated.
