## More Than a Posture

To most of the modern world, yoga means a mat, a set of poses, and an hour of stretching. That practice is real and valuable — but it is a single branch of a far older and grander tree. The Sanskrit word **yoga** comes from a root meaning "to yoke" or "to join," and from the beginning it named not an exercise but a *union*: the joining of the individual self to the ultimate reality, the disciplined path by which a restless human being is brought into harmony with the divine ground of all things.

Its roots reach back to the very dawn of Indian civilisation; seals from the ancient Indus Valley appear to show figures seated in meditative postures more than four thousand years ago. By the time of the [Upanishads](/article/hindu-philosophy), yoga had become the practical companion of philosophy — for the great Indian insight that the self and the absolute are one is no use as a mere idea. It has to be *realised*, experienced directly in the silence of a stilled mind. Yoga is the technology built to make that experience possible.

:::insight Stilling the waves of the mind
The classical definition is the second line of Patanjali's Yoga Sutras: *yogas chitta vritti nirodha* — "yoga is the stilling of the fluctuations of the mind." The image is of a lake whipped into waves, on whose churning surface no true reflection can form. Calm the water, and the depths grow clear; calm the mind, and reality can at last be seen as it is. Everything in yoga — the postures, the breathing, the concentration — serves this single end: to quiet the restless ripple of thought until the self shines through.
:::

## The Eight Limbs

Around two thousand years ago the sage **Patanjali** gathered the scattered teachings into the **Yoga Sutras**, a chain of terse aphorisms that became the classical manual of the discipline. He set out the **ashtanga**, the "eight limbs" — a ladder of practice climbing from the outer life to the innermost. It begins not with the body but with ethics: restraints such as non-violence and truthfulness, and observances such as purity and contentment, for a turbulent or cruel life cannot be quieted by technique alone.

Only then come the limbs the world now knows: **asana**, steady posture, originally meant simply to let the body sit motionless for long meditation; and **pranayama**, the regulation of the breath, by which the practitioner learns to govern the flow of life-energy and, with it, the movements of the mind. The higher limbs turn wholly inward — the withdrawal of the senses, **concentration** fixed on a single point, **meditation** flowing unbroken, and at the summit **samadhi**, a state of complete absorption in which the meditator and the object of meditation dissolve into one. This was the goal all along: not health, not calm, but liberation.

> When the mind is stilled, the seer rests in its own true nature. Then the soul abides in itself, and is free.

## Many Roads of Practice

Just as Hindu philosophy offers several paths to the divine, so yoga developed into several disciplines suited to different temperaments — a kinship with the way the philosophers of [Greece](/article/greek-philosophy) tied the good life to the training of the soul, though the Indian methods went further into the body and the breath. There is the yoga of selfless **action**, of devotional **love**, of **knowledge**, and the famous **hatha yoga** of physical postures and breath, originally a preparation that would purify and strengthen the body so it could endure the long stillness of deep meditation. The vigorous poses of a modern class are the distant descendants of that preparatory craft.

:::note A science of the inner world
What is remarkable about the yogic tradition is how empirical it is. For thousands of years its practitioners treated the inner life as a field for careful experiment — observing exactly how breath affects attention, how attention affects emotion, how the relentless practice of stillness changes the mind over years. Long before modern neuroscience, India built a detailed, tested map of consciousness. Today that map is being explored anew, as researchers measure how meditation reshapes the brain, lowers stress and sharpens attention — confirming with instruments what the yogis claimed from the cushion.
:::

## India's Gift to the World

Of all the treasures of [Hinduism](/article/hinduism), yoga and meditation have travelled furthest. They have crossed every border and outgrown their origins, taken up by people of every faith and of none, who may never read a Sanskrit verse yet sit each morning to follow their breath. The United Nations now marks an International Day of Yoga; hospitals prescribe meditation; the word *guru* has entered a hundred languages. Behind the global wellness movement stands an ancient and serious science — a discipline that set out, thousands of years ago, to do the hardest thing a human being can attempt: to grow quiet enough, inside, to see what is really there.
