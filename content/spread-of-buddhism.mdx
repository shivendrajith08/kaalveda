## A Faith Carried, Not Conquered

Most great religions have spread, at least in part, by the sword. Buddhism is a striking exception. From its birthplace on the Ganges plain it travelled across Asia carried by **missionaries, monks and merchants** — by teaching, translation and trade rather than conquest. Its portability was built in: the [Sangha](/article/sangha-monastic-life) could plant itself anywhere a few monks and a lay community could be gathered, and the [Dharma](/article/buddhist-scriptures) asked no allegiance to a homeland or a language. What follows is the map of one of history's great peaceful expansions.

## Ashoka and the First Missions

The turning point came in the **third century BCE** with the emperor **Ashoka**, whose Mauryan realm spanned nearly all the Indian subcontinent. Sickened, tradition holds, by the carnage of his conquest of Kalinga, Ashoka embraced Buddhism and resolved to rule by *dhamma* — righteousness — rather than force. He had edicts of moral teaching carved on pillars and rocks across his empire, built stupas to enshrine the Buddha's relics, and, decisively, **sent missionaries beyond his borders.**

Ashoka's patronage lifted Buddhism from a regional movement to an international one. He is said to have dispatched teachers to the Hellenistic kingdoms of the west and, most consequentially, to have sent his own son **Mahinda** south to **Sri Lanka**, where the king converted and the island became the great stronghold of the **Theravada** tradition — the setting, two centuries later, where the Pali canon was first written down. From Sri Lanka the southern school would in time reach **Myanmar, Thailand, Laos and Cambodia**, becoming the enduring faith of mainland Southeast Asia.

:::note An emperor's change of heart
Ashoka is remembered as the model of the Buddhist ruler precisely because he turned from violence to compassion at the height of his power. His pillar edicts — proclaiming care for subjects, tolerance of all faiths, and kindness to animals — are among the earliest voices of conscience preserved in stone. The wheel of the Dharma from his pillar at Sarnath sits today at the centre of the flag of India, a memory of the emperor who first sent the teaching out to the world.
:::

## The Silk Road to China

Buddhism's second great journey ran **north and east**, along the **Silk Road** — the network of caravan routes linking India, through Central Asia, to the Chinese heartland. Monks travelled with the merchant caravans, and along the way rose the great cave-temple complexes — **Bamiyan** in Afghanistan with its colossal cliff-carved Buddhas, **Dunhuang** at the desert's edge with its thousand painted grottoes — that mark the road like milestones of the Dharma.

The teaching that reached China around the **first century CE** was the [Mahayana](/article/buddhist-branches), and its meeting with Chinese civilization was one of the great encounters in the history of religion. It required a monumental labour of **translation**, as centuries of scholars rendered the Sanskrit sutras into Chinese, and a slow negotiation with the native traditions of Confucianism and Daoism. Out of that meeting grew distinctively Chinese schools — the meditative **Chan** (which would become **Zen** in Japan), the devotional **Pure Land**, and others. From China the faith passed onward to **Korea** by the fourth century and across the sea to **Japan** by the sixth, where it wove itself into the culture and produced some of the most refined expressions of Buddhist art and practice in the world.

## Over the Passes into Tibet

Buddhism reached **Tibet** later and by a different door, climbing over the Himalayan passes directly from India from the **seventh and eighth centuries** onward. Here it was the [Vajrayana](/article/buddhist-branches), the Diamond Vehicle, that took root — carried by Indian masters like the semi-legendary **Padmasambhava** and, later, the great scholar **Atisha**, and nurtured by Tibetan kings. Over centuries Tibet built one of the most complete and rigorous of all Buddhist civilizations, its monasteries preserving in translation a vast library of Indian texts that would otherwise have been lost, and its lineages of lamas — including the **Dalai Lamas** — shaping the religious life of Tibet, Mongolia and the Himalayan world.

## The Fading in India

The strangest turn in the story is that Buddhism **declined in the land of its birth**. Several currents combined over the centuries. The [Hindu](/article/hinduism) tradition, endlessly absorptive, reabsorbed much of what Buddhism had offered — even coming to regard the Buddha as an avatar of the god Vishnu — while the surging devotional movements and the philosophy of teachers like Shankara won back popular allegiance. Buddhism had grown concentrated in great monastic universities dependent on royal patronage, and when that patronage waned and the **monasteries were destroyed** in the upheavals of the early second millennium — the famed university of **Nalanda** among them — the Indian Sangha lost the institutions that sustained it. By around 1200 CE, Buddhism had all but vanished from the plains where the Buddha had walked, even as it thrived from Sri Lanka to Japan.

> The Dharma is like a fire: it warms the house it burns in, then is carried, spark by spark, to kindle houses far away.

## A World Religion

By the end of the first millennium Buddhism had become what it remains — a genuinely **pan-Asian** faith, holding the spiritual imagination of the continent in three great forms: Theravada in the tropical south, Mahayana across the civilizations of the east, and Vajrayana in the northern mountains. In the modern era it has spread again, this time **westward**, as meditation and Buddhist thought have found a wide following in Europe and the Americas, and as the exile of Tibetan teachers after 1959 carried the Vajrayana across the world.

Today some **half a billion people** follow the teaching of the prince who could not un-see an old man, a sick man and a corpse. From a single voice in a deer park it became the inheritance of a continent — carried, across two and a half thousand years, not by empire but by the patient walking of monks along the roads of the world.
