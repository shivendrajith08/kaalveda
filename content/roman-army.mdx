## An Army of Citizens, Then of Professionals

In the early [Republic](/article/roman-republic) the army was a militia of citizen-farmers who supplied their own arms and went home after the campaign. This worked while Rome's wars were short and close. But as the empire grew and wars stretched across years and continents, the system buckled. Around **107 BCE** the general **Marius** opened the legions to the landless poor, equipping them at state expense and promising land on discharge.

The change was momentous. The legions became a professional standing army — better trained and more loyal than any militia — but loyal now to the general who paid and led them rather than to the state. That shift armed the ambitions of men like [Julius Caesar](/article/julius-caesar) and helped turn the Republic into the [Empire](/article/roman-empire).

## Built for Order

The legion's power lay less in any individual soldier than in its structure. A legion of roughly **5,000 men** was divided into ten **cohorts**, each cohort into six **centuries** of about eighty men, each century led by a **centurion** — the tough career officers who were the backbone of the whole machine. This modular design let commanders manoeuvre blocks of men with a flexibility that the dense, unwieldy phalanxes of their enemies could not match.

:::insight Discipline as a weapon
What truly set the legions apart was not equipment but discipline. Roman soldiers drilled constantly, obeyed instantly, and faced savage punishment for failure — including *decimation*, the execution of every tenth man in a disgraced unit. A Roman army that broke and ran was rare; one that held its formation could defeat forces many times its size.

The soldier carried a large curved shield, the **scutum**; a short stabbing sword, the **gladius**, ideal for the press of close combat; and the **pilum**, a heavy javelin whose soft iron neck bent on impact so it could not be thrown back. Behind the front ranks came artillery — bolt-throwers and stone-hurling catapults that could shatter a wall or a charge.

## The Soldier as Builder

Here is the secret most often forgotten: the legionary spent far more days digging than fighting. Every night on campaign the army built a fortified camp to the same exacting plan, ditch and rampart and ordered streets, so that a Roman force was never caught unprepared. In peacetime the same hands built the roads, bridges, walls, and aqueducts of [Roman engineering](/article/roman-engineering) that knit the empire together.

> The Romans owe the mastery of the world to military training, to discipline in their camps, and to practice in engineering.

An army that could march thirty kilometres in a day and throw up a fort by nightfall was a tool of conquest and administration at once. The frontier was not just a line of soldiers but a line of their stonework — the road they marched and the wall they raised.

## Defeat and Endurance

The legions were not invincible. **Hannibal** destroyed army after army in the Punic Wars, annihilating eighty thousand Romans in a single afternoon at **Cannae** in 216 BCE. In **9 CE** three whole legions were ambushed and wiped out in the **Teutoburg Forest** of Germany, a disaster that fixed the empire's northern border at the Rhine forever.

What made Rome formidable was not that it never lost but that it could absorb catastrophe and come back. After Cannae it simply raised new armies and ground Hannibal down through sheer persistence.

:::caution When the shield became the spear
The army that defended Rome could also turn on it. Emperors increasingly owed their thrones to the soldiers, and in the chaotic third century the legions made and murdered rulers at will. The force that built the empire would, in its disorder and its growing reliance on hired barbarian troops, become entangled in [the empire's fall](/article/fall-of-rome).
