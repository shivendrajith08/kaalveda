## The Door Opens

The Space Age began with a beep. On **4 October 1957**, the Soviet Union launched **Sputnik 1**, a polished metal sphere the size of a beach ball that circled the Earth every 96 minutes, broadcasting a simple radio signal. It carried no science instruments to speak of, but its meaning was enormous: humanity had, for the first time, placed an object into orbit.

The shock of Sputnik ignited a fierce **space race** between the Soviet Union and the United States. Just four years later, in **1961**, the Soviet cosmonaut **Yuri Gagarin** became the first human to orbit the Earth — a single, history-making lap of the planet. The sky was no longer a ceiling.

:::insight Why getting to orbit is so hard
Reaching space is not mainly about going up — it is about going *fast*. To stay in orbit, a spacecraft must travel sideways at roughly 28,000 kilometres per hour, fast enough that as it falls toward Earth, the planet's surface curves away beneath it. That immense speed is why rockets must be so powerful, and why spaceflight remained out of reach for so long.
:::

## Footprints on the Moon

The race reached its climax with the American **Apollo programme**. Riding the gigantic **Saturn V** rocket — to this day among the most powerful machines ever built — Apollo 11 carried three astronauts to the [Moon](/article/the-moon), and on **20 July 1969** Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin became the first humans to walk on another world while Michael Collins orbited above.

Over the following three years, six Apollo missions landed twelve men on the lunar surface, who returned with rock samples that revolutionised our understanding of the Moon's origin. It remains the farthest from Earth that human beings have ever travelled.

> Apollo was as much a feat of organisation as of engineering: at its peak the programme employed some 400,000 people, and it ran on computers less powerful than a modern pocket calculator.

## Living and Working in Orbit

After the drama of the Moon landings, human spaceflight turned to building a lasting presence closer to home. The American **Space Shuttle**, flying from 1981 to 2011, was the first reusable spacecraft, ferrying crews and cargo to orbit and back dozens of times.

Its greatest legacy is the **International Space Station** — a laboratory the size of a football field, assembled in orbit from the late 1990s and continuously inhabited since **November 2000**. A partnership of the United States, Russia, Europe, Japan, and Canada, the ISS has hosted astronauts from dozens of nations, who run experiments in the strange environment of weightlessness and study how the human body copes with long stays in space — knowledge essential for any voyage to [Mars](/article/mars).

## Robotic Pioneers

Humans have travelled no farther than the Moon, but our machines have explored the entire Solar System and beyond. Robotic missions are cheaper, can endure conditions no person could survive, and have been the true workhorses of exploration:

1. **The Voyagers** (1977) — twin probes that toured the outer planets and have now crossed into interstellar space, the most distant human-made objects in existence.
2. **Cassini** (2004–2017) — orbited Saturn for 13 years, revealing its rings and the ocean moons Titan and Enceladus in breathtaking detail.
3. **New Horizons** (2015) — gave us our first close look at Pluto, four billion miles from home.
4. **Mars rovers** — a lineage from Sojourner to Perseverance that has turned the red planet into a place we explore on the ground, as told in [Mars](/article/mars).

Landing these robots is a feat in itself — arriving at Mars, for instance, means slowing from interplanetary speed to a gentle touchdown in about seven minutes, with no chance for human control across the long radio delay.

## Eyes Above the Atmosphere

Some of the most transformative missions never leave orbit — because the prize is a clear view of the cosmos. The **Hubble Space Telescope**, launched in **1990**, escaped the blurring, dimming effect of Earth's atmosphere and delivered images of unprecedented depth and clarity, helping pin down the age of the universe and revealing galaxies near the edge of the observable cosmos.

Its successor, the **James Webb Space Telescope**, launched in **December 2021**, observes in infrared from a vantage point a million miles from Earth. Webb can see through cosmic dust and capture light stretched by the expansion of the universe — letting it study the very first galaxies, born within the universe's opening chapters after [the Big Bang](/article/the-big-bang), and probe the atmospheres of distant planets for signs of habitability.

## A New Era of Access

For its first half-century, spaceflight was almost entirely the domain of national governments. That has changed. **Private companies** now build rockets, fly cargo and crew to the space station, and — crucially — have made boosters that land themselves and fly again, driving the cost of reaching orbit sharply downward.

:::note Why reusability matters
Throwing away a rocket after a single flight is like scrapping an airliner after one trip. Reusable rockets that land and relaunch have begun to transform the economics of spaceflight, making ambitions once thought fantastical — from lunar bases to crewed Mars missions — start to look achievable.
:::

From a beeping sphere in 1957 to telescopes reading the universe's first light, space missions have compressed a cosmic perspective into a few human generations. Where that momentum carries us next — back to the Moon to stay, on to Mars, and perhaps farther still — is the subject of [the future of space](/article/the-future-of-space).
