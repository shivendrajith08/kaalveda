## A World Born of Catastrophe

The leading explanation for the Moon's origin is also one of the most dramatic stories in planetary science. About 4.5 billion years ago, when the [Solar System](/article/the-solar-system) was young and crowded, a Mars-sized body — sometimes named **Theia** — is thought to have slammed into the newly formed Earth. The colossal impact threw a huge spray of molten and vaporised rock into orbit, and from that ring of debris the Moon swiftly coalesced.

This **giant-impact hypothesis** explains several puzzling facts at once: why the Moon is relatively large compared with its planet, why it has only a small iron core, and why Moon rocks share so close a chemical kinship with Earth's mantle. The Moon, in this view, is partly made of the Earth itself.

:::insight A surprisingly large moon
The Moon is more than a quarter of Earth's diameter — a far greater ratio than any other major moon in the Solar System. It is so large relative to its planet that some astronomers describe the Earth–Moon system as almost a double world.
:::

## The Face We Always See

The Moon is **tidally locked** to the Earth, meaning it rotates exactly once for every orbit it makes. As a result, it always keeps the same hemisphere turned toward us. The familiar "near side" is what every human before the space age ever saw; the **far side** — often wrongly called the "dark side," though it gets just as much sunlight — was a complete mystery until a Soviet spacecraft photographed it in 1959.

The two faces look strikingly different. The near side is marked by broad, dark plains called **maria**, or "seas" — not water, but ancient floods of basalt lava that filled giant impact basins. The far side is more rugged and heavily cratered, with far fewer maria, for reasons still being studied.

## A Cratered, Airless Land

With almost no atmosphere, the Moon is a still and silent place. There is no wind, no weather, no sound, and nothing to soften the harsh extremes: surface temperatures swing from around 120°C in the blazing lunar day to below −170°C in the long, frigid night. With no air to burn them up, meteorites strike the surface directly, and over billions of years they have churned it into a fine grey dust called **regolith**.

Because there is no erosion, the Moon is a preserved record of the Solar System's history. The craters tell of an era of heavy bombardment that the Earth has long since erased — and a single human footprint left in 1969 may survive, undisturbed, for millions of years.

## Why the Tides Rise

The Moon's gravity pulls on the Earth, and on its oceans most visibly of all. The water on the side facing the Moon is tugged into a bulge, with a matching bulge on the opposite side — and as the Earth rotates beneath these bulges, every coast experiences the rhythmic rise and fall of the **tides**, usually twice a day.

> The Moon does more than move the seas. By gravitationally steadying the tilt of Earth's axis, it keeps our climate relatively stable over long ages — a quiet but profound gift to the development of life.

That same gravitational interaction is slowly pushing the Moon away: it recedes from Earth by about 3.8 centimetres a year, and Earth's days grow imperceptibly longer as a result. Long ago the Moon loomed much larger in the sky than it does today.

## The Day We Walked There

The Moon is the only world beyond Earth that human beings have visited. On **20 July 1969**, NASA's **Apollo 11** landed and **Neil Armstrong** stepped onto the surface with the words, "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind." Over six successful Apollo landings between 1969 and 1972, **twelve astronauts** walked on the Moon, returning with hundreds of kilograms of rock that transformed our understanding of its history.

The Apollo programme, and the space race that drove it, is told in full in [space missions](/article/space-missions). Those samples confirmed the Moon's great age and lent crucial support to the giant-impact story of its birth.

## Going Back to Stay

After half a century away, humanity is returning — and this time the goal is not flags and footprints but a lasting presence. NASA's **Artemis** programme, working with international and commercial partners, aims to land astronauts near the Moon's **south pole**, where deep craters hold water ice in permanent shadow.

That ice is the prize. It could supply drinking water and breathable oxygen, and be split into hydrogen and oxygen for rocket fuel — turning the Moon into a refuelling stop and a stepping stone deeper into space. A base on the Moon would be a proving ground for the technologies needed to reach [Mars](/article/mars) and beyond, a future explored in [the future of space](/article/the-future-of-space). Our oldest companion in the sky may yet become humanity's first home away from Earth.
