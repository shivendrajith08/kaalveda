## A Faith Built on Five Supports

When Muslims speak of the **arkan al-Islam**, the "pillars of Islam," they use a deliberate image: a building held up by columns. Belief alone is not the whole of the faith; it must be enacted, day by day and year by year, in deeds that any believer — learned or unlettered, rich or poor — can perform. The Five Pillars are those deeds. They were laid down in the [Quran](/article/the-quran) and the example of [Muhammad](/article/muhammad), and across fourteen centuries they have given Islam a remarkable unity of practice, so that a worshipper in Senegal and one in Indonesia perform the same acts in the same way.

The first pillar is the **shahada**, the testimony of faith: *There is no god but God, and Muhammad is the messenger of God.* To recite it sincerely, with understanding, is to become a Muslim. It is the creed in a single breath, whispered into the ear of the newborn and, where possible, the last words on the lips of the dying. The other four pillars are the living-out of what this declaration means.

:::insight The discipline of remembrance
The Arabic word *dhikr* means "remembrance," and in a sense all five pillars exist to keep God constantly in mind. Five times a day the believer stops to pray; once a year they fast from dawn to dusk; throughout the year they give from their wealth; once in a lifetime they journey to Mecca. Each is a discipline that interrupts ordinary life to turn the heart back toward God — a structure for not forgetting. In this the pillars resemble the contemplative disciplines of other traditions, from the repeated prayer of the [Christian mystics](/article/christian-mysticism) to the meditative practices of the [yogis of India](/article/yoga-and-meditation), all of them ways of holding the mind, again and again, on the divine.
:::

## Prayer, Alms and the Fast

The second pillar is **salat**, the ritual prayer performed five times each day — at dawn, noon, afternoon, sunset and night. Wherever they are, Muslims wash, face the direction of Mecca, and move through a fixed sequence of standing, bowing and prostration, reciting verses of the Quran in Arabic. It is at once intimate and communal: a person may pray alone, but the call to prayer ringing from the minarets gathers the community, and on Fridays the noon prayer is performed together in the mosque. To press one's forehead to the ground five times a day is the very posture of *islam*, of submission.

The third pillar is **zakat**, the giving of alms — a fixed portion, traditionally one-fortieth, of one's accumulated wealth, given each year to the poor, the indebted and the traveller. It is not charity in the sense of a free gift but a duty and a purification: the word itself means "to purify," for the Quran teaches that wealth is cleansed by sharing it. The fourth pillar is **sawm**, the fast of **Ramadan**, the holy month in which the first revelation came. From the first light of dawn until sunset, the faithful take no food, drink or other bodily pleasure, breaking the fast each evening and gathering in the nights for extra prayer. The fast teaches self-mastery, sharpens compassion for the hungry, and is broken at month's end by the joyous festival of Eid al-Fitr.

> The fasting is not from food and drink alone; the true fast is the restraint of the tongue, the eyes and the heart from all that is unworthy.

## The Pilgrimage to Mecca

The fifth pillar is the **hajj**, the great pilgrimage to Mecca, required once in a lifetime of every Muslim with the health and means to make it. In the appointed days of the pilgrimage season, some two million people converge on the holy city from every nation on Earth. Shedding the marks of rank and wealth, the men wrapped in two simple sheets of unsewn white cloth, the pilgrims become indistinguishable from one another — prince beside pauper, all equal before God.

They circle the **Kaaba** seven times, the cube-shaped shrine that Muslims believe Abraham raised to the one God; they run between the hills of Safa and Marwa, retracing the desperate search of Hagar for water for her son; they stand together on the plain of Arafat in the act that is the very heart of the hajj, begging God's mercy. For many it is the most powerful experience of a lifetime, a foretaste of the gathering of all humanity before God at the last day.

:::note A sixth striving
Some Muslims speak of *jihad* — "struggle" or "striving" — as a kind of sixth pillar, though most traditions do not formally count it among them. Its primary meaning in the Quran and the tradition is the inner struggle of every believer against their own selfishness and sin, what the Prophet is said to have called the "greater jihad." The word also carries the meaning of righteous struggle in defence of the community, hedged about with strict conditions. Like the pillars themselves, it is at root a call to exert oneself, body and soul, in the path of God.
:::

## The Architecture of a Devout Life

Taken together, the Five Pillars give Islam its extraordinary cohesion. They are simple enough that a child can learn them, yet they reach into every scale of time — the five daily prayers, the monthly fast, the yearly alms, the once-in-a-lifetime pilgrimage. They ask nothing of wealth or learning, only of will, so that the poorest believer can fulfil them as completely as the richest. And they bind the individual to the whole, for the same words, the same fast, the same circling of the same shrine unite nearly two billion people into a single worshipping body. In the pillars, the abstract creed of [Islam](/article/islam) — submission to the one God — becomes a way of moving through the world.
