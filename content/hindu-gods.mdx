## Thirty-Three Million Gods

To a visitor, the Hindu pantheon can seem bewildering — a swirl of blue-skinned heroes, many-armed goddesses, elephant-headed sons and divine animals. The tradition itself delights in the abundance, speaking of **thirty-three million** deities, a number meant less as a census than as a poetic figure for *the infinite*. But ask a thoughtful Hindu how many gods there really are, and the answer is older and simpler. The divine is one — **Brahman**, the supreme reality — and the many gods are its faces, the forms through which the formless lets itself be loved.

This is the crucial difference from the cheerful, jealous, all-too-human Olympians of [Greek mythology](/article/greek-mythology) or the absorbed state gods of [Rome](/article/roman-mythology). Hindu deities are not rivals competing for a worshipper's loyalty; they are windows onto a single light. A devotee chooses an **ishta-devata**, a cherished form of God, and pours devotion into that one face without ever denying the others.

:::insight The chosen deity
Because the divine wears many faces, Hindus need not all worship the same one. A family may keep Ganesha by the door, Lakshmi in the strongbox and Hanuman in the heart, while neighbours pour their devotion into Krishna or the Goddess. Each is held to be a full and sufficient gateway to the one reality — so that personal love, not exclusive doctrine, becomes the engine of worship.
:::

## The Trimurti: Creator, Preserver, Destroyer

At the centre of the classical pantheon stand three great gods who divide the work of the cosmos — the **Trimurti**, or triple form. **Brahma** is the creator who brings each universe into being; **Vishnu** is the preserver who sustains it in order and goodness; and **Shiva** is the destroyer who dissolves it again so that creation may begin afresh. These are not three competing powers but three rhythms of one endless process, the breathing in and out of existence itself.

Brahma, oddly, is little worshipped — his work is done once each cycle. It is Vishnu and Shiva who command the devotion of vast traditions. **Vishnu** descends into the world again and again, whenever order falters, in a series of **avatars** — most beloved among them **Rama**, the perfect king of the [Ramayana](/article/ramayana), and **Krishna**, the divine cowherd, charioteer and teacher of the [Mahabharata](/article/mahabharata). **Shiva**, by contrast, is the great paradox: ascetic and sensual, terrifying and serene, the meditating yogi of the Himalayas and the cosmic dancer whose drum beats out creation and whose fire consumes it.

> As a dancer gathers up the threads of a net, so at the end of time the Lord draws the worlds into himself.

## The Goddess and the Beloved Forms

To speak only of the gods would be to miss half the pantheon and much of its power. Across India the **Devi**, the great Goddess, is worshipped as the supreme reality in her own right — as **Durga**, the warrior riding her lion to slay the buffalo-demon; as **Kali**, the fierce dark mother who tramples death itself; and as **Parvati** and **Lakshmi** and **Saraswati**, consorts of the great gods who are no mere companions but the very energy, the **Shakti**, without which the gods are powerless. In the goddess traditions it is feminine power that animates the whole cosmos.

And then there are the deities every household knows and loves. **Ganesha**, the elephant-headed remover of obstacles, is invoked at the start of any venture. **Hanuman**, the mighty monkey-god, is the model of fearless devotion. **Saraswati** governs learning and music; **Lakshmi** brings fortune and abundance. These are the gods of the doorway and the festival, woven into the rhythm of ordinary life.

:::note Why so many arms?
The multiple arms, heads and weapons of Hindu deities are a visual language, not a literal portrait. Extra arms signify superhuman power and the many things a god can do at once; each object held — a discus, a conch, a lotus, a trident — carries a precise meaning. The blue skin of Vishnu and Krishna evokes the infinite, like the sky and the ocean. To read a Hindu image is to read a theology written in posture and gesture.
:::

## One Light, Many Lamps

The genius of the Hindu pantheon is that it holds together two ideas most religions keep apart: a rigorous philosophical insistence that ultimate reality is one, formless and beyond all description; and a warm, human longing to love a God with a face, a name and a story. The thinkers ascend toward the abstract **Brahman**; the millions garland Ganesha and weep before Krishna; and the tradition tells them, gently, that they are doing the same thing. The many gods are lamps, and the light in every lamp is one. To understand this is to understand how the [philosophy](/article/hindu-philosophy) and the devotion of Hinduism, so different in their language, have for thousands of years remained a single faith.
