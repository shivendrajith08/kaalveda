## A Prince Behind Palace Walls

He was born, tradition holds, around **563 BCE** in a grove at **Lumbini**, in the low country beneath the Himalayas that is today southern Nepal. His clan was the **Shakya**, a proud republican people of the Ganges frontier, and his father Suddhodana was their leading man — a *raja*, remembered by later devotion as a great king. The child was named **Siddhartha**, "he who achieves his aim," and given the family name **Gautama**. His mother Maya died within days of his birth, and he was raised by her sister in a household that wanted for nothing.

There is a legend, and beneath it a plainer truth. The legend says a sage foretold that the boy would become either a world-conquering emperor or a world-renouncing sage, and that his father, wanting an heir and not a hermit, resolved to bind him to the world with pleasure. Siddhartha was raised inside a walled paradise of gardens and music, married young to the princess Yasodhara, and screened from every ugliness of life. The plainer truth is only this: a young man of privilege was, for a time, kept from knowing what everyone eventually knows.

:::note What we can and cannot know
No writing survives from the Buddha's own lifetime; his teaching was preserved by memory and set down in full only centuries later. Scholars debate his dates — many now place his death nearer **400 BCE** than the traditional 483 — and treat the miraculous elements of his biography as the reverent embroidery of later ages. Yet almost all accept that behind the legend stood a real man, a teacher of the Gangetic plain whose life reshaped the thought of half the world. This article tells the story as the tradition tells it, while marking where faith outruns history.
:::

## The Four Sights

The turning came, the texts say, when the grown prince finally passed beyond the palace gates. On successive outings he saw four things his father's care had hidden from him — the **Four Sights** that open the whole drama of Buddhism. He saw a **frail old man**, and learned that youth ends. He saw a **sick man**, and learned that health fails. He saw a **corpse** carried to the burning ground, and learned that all life ends in death. And finally he saw a **wandering ascetic**, serene amid a world of decay, a man who had left everything to seek what lies beyond it.

The three first sights are simply the facts of existence, which the young see and forget and the old cannot. The fourth was a door. If age, sickness and death are the disease, the calm renouncer suggested there might be a cure — and Siddhartha resolved to find it.

> I too am subject to decay and am not free from the power of decay: what if I were to be repelled by that which is subject to decay?

## The Great Renunciation

At twenty-nine, on the night his own son Rahula was born, Siddhartha made the choice his father had dreaded. He looked a last time on his sleeping wife and child, rode to the edge of the forest, cut off his hair, exchanged his fine robes for a beggar's rag, and became a homeless seeker — one more of the many *shramanas*, the wandering renouncers who filled the roads of northern India in that restless age. The tradition calls it the **Great Renunciation**, the *Going Forth*.

The world he entered was seething with spiritual experiment. This was the same landscape that had produced the [Upanishads](/article/hindu-philosophy) of the older [Hindu](/article/hinduism) tradition, with their search for the deathless self behind all things, and it swarmed with rival teachers offering rival roads to liberation. Siddhartha went first to two celebrated meditation masters and swiftly reached the summit of what each could teach — states of formless, boundless calm. But the calm faded when he rose from his mat, and the problem of suffering remained. So he left them too.

## Six Years in the Forest

He turned then to the harshest path his age admired: extreme **asceticism**, the punishment of the body to free the spirit. With five companions he starved himself for years, eating, the texts say, a single grain of rice a day, until his spine could be felt through his belly and his skin hung on his bones. If liberation could be won by suffering, no one suffered more thoroughly than Siddhartha Gautama.

And it did not work. Half-dead and no nearer the truth, he remembered a moment from his childhood — sitting in cool shade at a ploughing festival, his mind spontaneously settling into a clear, unforced peace. Perhaps, he thought, the way forward was neither the indulgence of the palace nor the torment of the forest, but a **Middle Way** between them. He accepted a bowl of milk-rice from a village girl named Sujata and ate. His five companions, disgusted at what looked like surrender, abandoned him.

:::insight The Middle Way
The Buddha's first and most enduring discovery was not a doctrine but a temperament: that truth is not found at the extremes. Self-indulgence dulls the mind; self-torture exhausts it. Between the two lies a path of balance — enough discipline to sharpen awareness, enough kindness to the body to sustain it. This **Middle Way** would become the signature of everything he taught, and the reason his path could be walked by ordinary people and not only by heroes of endurance.
:::

## The Night Under the Tree

Alone now, restored, he sat down at **Bodh Gaya** beneath a spreading **pipal tree** — ever after called the **Bodhi tree**, the tree of awakening — and made a vow: he would not rise until he had understood. Through the long night, the tradition says, he passed through deepening states of meditation. He saw his own countless former lives unrolling behind him. He saw the whole turning wheel of beings dying and being reborn according to their deeds — the workings of [karma and rebirth](/article/karma-rebirth-samsara). And at last, as the morning star rose, he saw directly into the origin of suffering and its ending. The chains fell away. He was **awake**.

He was thirty-five. From that dawn he was no longer Siddhartha the prince but the **Buddha** — not a god, not a prophet, but simply "one who has woken up," seeing reality as it is while others dream. What he had found is the substance of the [Four Noble Truths](/article/four-noble-truths) and the [Eightfold Path](/article/noble-eightfold-path), and the freedom he had reached is [Nirvana](/article/nirvana-enlightenment).

## Turning the Wheel

He might have kept his silence — the insight was subtle, the world distracted, and who would listen? But out of compassion he chose to teach. He walked to the **Deer Park at Sarnath**, near the holy city of Varanasi, and found his five former companions. To them he gave his first sermon, an event Buddhists call the **First Turning of the Wheel of the Dharma**. He set out the Middle Way and the Four Noble Truths, and one of the five understood on the spot. The **[Sangha](/article/sangha-monastic-life)**, the community of followers, was born that afternoon, and with the Buddha and the [Dharma](/article/buddhist-scriptures) it became the third of the **Three Jewels** in which every Buddhist takes refuge.

For **forty-five years** the Buddha walked the roads of the Ganges plain — through the kingdoms of Magadha and Kosala, teaching kings and courtesans, farmers and philosophers, brahmins and outcastes alike. He admitted women to the monastic order, a startling step for the age. He turned away metaphysical speculation that did not lead to freedom, comparing the man who demands to know the ultimate nature of the universe before practising to a man shot by a poisoned arrow who refuses treatment until he knows the archer's name and caste.

> Just as the great ocean has one taste, the taste of salt, so this teaching and discipline has one taste, the taste of liberation.

## The Final Passing

At the age of eighty, worn out by a long life of walking, the Buddha died near the town of **Kushinagar**, lying on his side between two trees, surrounded by his monks. This death is called the **Parinirvana**, the final and complete nirvana — the passing beyond the wheel of rebirth altogether, from which there is no return. His reported last words gather his whole message into a single breath:

> All conditioned things are impermanent. Strive on with diligence.

He left no successor and appointed no head of the community. When his followers asked who would lead them, he told them to make the teaching itself their guide: *be a lamp unto yourselves.* His body was cremated, and the relics divided and enshrined in **stupas** — the mounded monuments that would become the seeds of Buddhist architecture across Asia.

## The Man Who Woke Up

What Siddhartha Gautama began was unlike the faiths that surrounded it. He founded no priesthood standing between the seeker and the truth, demanded no belief in a creator, and insisted that each person test his teaching for themselves, as one tests gold by fire, rather than accept it on his authority. From the frontier of northern India this quiet program of waking up would spread — carried by monks and merchants to [Sri Lanka, Tibet, China, Japan and beyond](/article/spread-of-buddhism), dividing into [great schools](/article/buddhist-branches) and shaping the inner life of a continent. It all traces back to a prince who could not un-see an old man, a sick man and a corpse, and who would not rest until he had found the way beyond them.
