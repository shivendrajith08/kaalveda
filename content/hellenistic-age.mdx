## A World Cut into Kingdoms

When [Alexander the Great](/article/alexander-the-great) died in 323 BCE without a competent heir, his generals — soldiers who had marched with him from Greece to India — fell upon his empire like wolves. After forty years of war, three great dynasties emerged from the wreckage. **Ptolemy** took [Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt) and founded a line of Greek pharaohs that would end, three centuries later, with **Cleopatra**. **Seleucus** seized the vast Asian heartland from Syria to the borders of India. The **Antigonids** held Alexander's homeland of Macedon and its grip on Greece itself.

Historians call the age that followed the **Hellenistic** period — from *Hellas*, the Greeks' own name for their land — to distinguish it from the earlier Hellenic age of the independent city-states. Its defining feature was that Greek culture now ruled over peoples who were not Greek, in lands Homer had never imagined, and absorbed influences from all of them. The small, intense world of the [polis](/article/ancient-greece) had given way to sprawling multi-ethnic kingdoms.

:::insight Greek as a world language
For the next thousand years, a simplified common Greek called **Koine** was the language of trade, government, and learning across the eastern Mediterranean and Near East. It was the Greek in which the New Testament would later be written — a measure of how far Alexander's tongue had travelled.

## The Age of the Great City

If the classical age belonged to the citizen of a small free city, the Hellenistic age belonged to the **metropolis**. New cities were founded by the dozen, often named for their royal founders, and the greatest of them dwarfed anything classical Greece had known. Above all there was **Alexandria** in Egypt, Alexander's own foundation, which grew into the largest and most dazzling city of the age — a cosmopolitan port of Greeks, Egyptians, Jews, and a hundred other peoples.

At its heart stood two of antiquity's wonders: the towering lighthouse, the **Pharos**, guiding ships into harbour, and the **Library** and its **Museum** — a state-funded institute where scholars from across the world were paid simply to study. The Ptolemies set out to collect every book in existence, and Alexandria became the brain of the ancient world.

> Give me a place to stand, and I will move the Earth.

So boasted **Archimedes** of Syracuse, the supreme genius of the age, who worked out the principle of the lever and buoyancy, approximated pi, and pioneered ideas that brushed against calculus. He was one of a constellation of Hellenistic minds: **Euclid**, who systematised geometry; **Eratosthenes**, who measured the circumference of the Earth with astonishing accuracy from the length of a shadow; **Aristarchus**, who dared to suggest the Earth went round the Sun. The questions first asked by [Greek philosophy](/article/greek-philosophy) now flowered into something close to organised science.

## A New Spirit in Art and Thought

The art of the age moved with its mood. Where classical [sculpture](/article/greek-architecture) had sought serene, ideal calm, Hellenistic art reached for drama, movement, and raw emotion — the writhing agony of the *Laocoön*, the windswept triumph of the *Winged Victory*, the wrinkled faces of old fishermen and the tenderness of sleeping children. It was an art of individuals and feeling rather than gods and ideals.

:::note Philosophy for an anxious world
In a cosmopolitan world where the old city no longer framed every life, philosophy turned to the inner self. **Stoicism**, founded in this age, taught calm acceptance of fate; **Epicureanism** sought freedom from fear; both offered the individual a way to find peace in a vast and uncertain world. Their counsel would later steady Roman emperors and console readers for two thousand years.

## The Road to Rome

The Hellenistic kingdoms were brilliant but quarrelsome, forever warring among themselves, and one by one they fell to a new power rising in the west. Over the second and first centuries BCE, [Rome](/article/rome) swallowed them whole — Macedon, then Greece, then Seleucid Asia, and finally Ptolemaic Egypt, when [Julius Caesar](/article/julius-caesar) and after him Augustus brought Cleopatra's kingdom into the Roman fold in 30 BCE.

Yet the conquered did the deeper conquering. Rome took Hellenistic Greek art, science, philosophy, and literature as its own, and carried them across its empire to the Atlantic. Far from ending Greek civilization, the Hellenistic age had spread it so widely and rooted it so deeply that, flowing onward through Rome and Byzantium, it became permanent. Alexander's true empire was never the one his generals divided — it was the Greek-speaking world of the mind, and that one lasted.
