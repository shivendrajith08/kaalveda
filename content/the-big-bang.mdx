## An Explosion That Was Not an Explosion

The name is a little misleading. The Big Bang was not a bomb going off at some point in a pre-existing void, with debris flying outward into empty space. There was no "outside" and no "before." Space and time themselves began in that first instant, and the expansion we still measure today is the stretching of space everywhere at once.

The clearest way to picture it is to run the film of the universe backwards. Galaxies are rushing apart, so in the past they were closer together. Keep rewinding and everything crowds in: the universe becomes denser, hotter, and more compact, until — 13.8 billion years ago — the whole observable cosmos was squeezed into a region smaller than an atom, at a temperature beyond imagining.

:::insight Everywhere is the centre
Because space itself is expanding, every observer in every galaxy sees all the others moving away from them. There is no special central point from which the Big Bang happened — it happened everywhere, because everywhere *was* the same place.
:::

## The First Three Minutes

The early universe passed through its most dramatic changes faster than any later age. In the first tiny fraction of a second, many physicists believe the cosmos underwent **inflation** — a brief, staggering burst of expansion that smoothed it out and stretched microscopic quantum ripples into the seeds of future galaxies.

As the universe expanded it cooled, and at each new temperature a new kind of structure could survive:

1. **The quark era** — for the first millionth of a second it was too hot for protons and neutrons to hold together; matter existed as a soup of fundamental particles.
2. **Nucleosynthesis** — within about three minutes the cosmos had cooled enough for protons and neutrons to fuse into the first atomic nuclei, forging the lightest elements.
3. **The long wait** — for hundreds of thousands of years the universe remained a glowing, opaque fog of nuclei and free electrons, too hot for whole atoms to form.

The recipe of that early forging is one of the theory's great triumphs. The Big Bang predicts that the young universe should be about three-quarters hydrogen and one-quarter helium by mass, with a trace of lithium — and that is almost exactly the mix we measure in the oldest stars and gas clouds.

## The Oldest Light in the Universe

About **380,000 years** after the beginning, the universe had cooled to a few thousand degrees — cool enough, at last, for electrons to settle into orbit around nuclei and form the first complete atoms. Suddenly the fog cleared. Light that had been trapped, endlessly scattering off free electrons, was free to stream across space.

That light still surrounds us today, stretched by 13.8 billion years of cosmic expansion into a faint glow of microwaves filling the entire sky. We call it the **cosmic microwave background** (CMB), and it is the single most powerful piece of evidence for the Big Bang.

> When Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson first detected this hiss in 1965, they thought their antenna was faulty — even suspecting pigeon droppings. They had stumbled onto the afterglow of creation, and the discovery won them a Nobel Prize.

Maps of the CMB made by satellites such as **COBE**, **WMAP** and **Planck** reveal it to be astonishingly uniform — but not perfectly so. Tiny temperature differences, just one part in a hundred thousand, mark where matter was very slightly denser. Those whispers of unevenness are the seeds from which every galaxy, star, and planet would eventually grow.

## How We Know the Universe Is Expanding

The Big Bang began as an inference from a simple, stunning observation. In **1929**, the astronomer **Edwin Hubble** found that almost every distant galaxy is moving away from us, and that the farther away a galaxy is, the faster it recedes. The light from those galaxies is shifted toward the red end of the spectrum — a cosmic version of the way a passing siren drops in pitch.

A Belgian priest and physicist, **Georges Lemaître**, had already worked out that an expanding universe implied a dense beginning, which he called the "primeval atom." Stitched together, these ideas pointed inescapably to a cosmos with a birthday.

:::note Three pillars of evidence
The Big Bang rests on three independent observations that all agree: the **expansion** of the universe seen in galaxy redshifts, the **cosmic microwave background**, and the measured **abundance of light elements**. No rival theory explains all three.
:::

## Dark Matter and Dark Energy

For all its success, the Big Bang model carries a humbling admission: the ordinary atoms that make up stars, planets, and people are only about **5 percent** of the universe. Roughly a quarter is **dark matter** — an unseen substance that feels gravity but neither emits nor absorbs light, and whose pull shaped how galaxies formed. The remaining 70 percent or so is **dark energy**, a mysterious property of space that is, remarkably, *accelerating* the cosmic expansion.

We can map their effects with precision, yet we do not know what either truly is. The Big Bang tells us how the universe began and grew; it also frames the deepest unsolved questions in physics. (The story of what happens next — to the galaxies, the stars, and to expansion itself — continues in [the future of space](/article/the-future-of-space).)

## From Smoothness to Structure

For perhaps a hundred million years after the light broke free, the universe was dark — a cooling sea of hydrogen and helium with no stars to light it. Astronomers call this the **cosmic dark ages**. Then gravity, working patiently on those faint density ripples, drew the gas into ever-denser knots until the first ones ignited.

The birth of the first [stars](/article/stars) ended the dark ages and began the long process of building everything we see. Inside those stars, and in their violent deaths, the simple early elements were cooked into carbon, oxygen, iron, and gold. Generations of stars gathered into galaxies; galaxies into clusters; clusters into the vast filaments and voids of the **cosmic web**.

## A Universe Still Unfolding

The Big Bang was not a single event finished long ago — it set in motion a story that is still being written. The same expansion Hubble measured continues today, and the faint light of the cosmic dawn is now being captured by telescopes like the James Webb, which can see galaxies as they were within the universe's first few hundred million years.

In a real sense, every atom in your body carries this history. The hydrogen in the water you drink was forged in those first three minutes; the calcium in your bones and the iron in your blood were assembled inside stars that lived and died before the Sun was born. To trace the Big Bang forward is to follow the road that leads to [the Solar System](/article/the-solar-system), to Earth, and to us.
