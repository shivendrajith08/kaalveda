## Why This Article Is Marked "Theory"

Almost no serious historian doubts that a Jewish man named Jesus lived in early first-century Galilee, gathered followers, and was crucified under the Roman prefect Pontius Pilate. The Roman writers Tacitus and the Jewish historian Josephus both mention him within a lifetime or two of his death, and the letters of Paul were written barely twenty years after the crucifixion. That much is the bedrock of mainstream scholarship.

What lies beyond proof is everything that matters most to faith. Whether Jesus was the Son of God, whether he worked miracles, whether he rose bodily from the tomb — these are claims of a different order, held by believers as the deepest truths there are and set aside by historians as beyond the reach of their tools. This article marks itself **theory** not to cast doubt on Christian conviction but to be honest about where the historian's road ends and the pilgrim's begins.

:::caution History and faith
The gospels are not neutral biographies; they are testimonies of faith, written to proclaim Jesus as Lord. That does not make them worthless to the historian — it makes them passionate, early and specific. But it does mean that reading them is always an act of interpretation. We present here both what scholarship reconstructs and what the church confesses, and we let the reader hold the two together.
:::

## A Life in Galilee

Jesus was born around **4 BCE**, in the last years of Herod the Great, into a world ruled at a distance by [Rome](/article/roman-empire) and close at hand by its client kings. He grew up in Nazareth, an unremarkable Galilean village, in a family of modest craftsmen — the Greek word used of him and his father suggests a builder or carpenter. For most of his life he is invisible to history.

Then, perhaps around the year 28, he appeared as a wandering teacher. He proclaimed the coming of the **Kingdom of God**, healed the sick, ate with outcasts and tax collectors, and taught in vivid parables drawn from the fields and households of ordinary people. He gathered an inner circle of twelve and a wider crowd of followers, women prominent among them. His message was urgent and unsettling: that God's reign was breaking in, that the last would be first, and that love of God and neighbour fulfilled the whole law.

> Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted. Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth.

The Sermon on the Mount, from which those lines come, remains one of the most influential texts ever spoken — its ethic of mercy, forgiveness and nonviolence has stirred reformers and revolutionaries far beyond the church.

## The Road to Jerusalem

The gospels converge on a final journey to **Jerusalem**, the holy city, at Passover. There Jesus caused an uproar in the Temple, was welcomed by crowds, and within days was betrayed, arrested, and handed to the Roman authorities. He was condemned and crucified — a punishment Rome reserved for those it deemed rebels — beneath a placard mocking him as "King of the Jews." He died, by the traditional reckoning, around the year **30**.

For his followers this was not the end but the turning point. They proclaimed that on the third day the tomb was found empty and that he appeared to them alive. Whatever a historian makes of those reports, something happened that transformed a scattered, frightened band into a movement willing to die for its message. That transformation is the seed from which [the early church](/article/early-church) and all of [Christianity](/article/christianity) grew.

:::note A Jewish life
It is easy to forget that Jesus lived and died a Jew, keeping the Sabbath and the festivals, steeped in the Hebrew scriptures and the prophets. He did not set out to found a new religion; he spoke to Israel, in the idiom of Israel. The faith that grew up in his name — drawing in Greeks and Romans and reaching far beyond the world of the synagogue and the gods of [Rome](/article/roman-religion) — would have astonished his first hearers as much as anyone.
:::

## The Question That Will Not Rest

Two thousand years on, the figure of Jesus refuses to settle into the past. Philosophers and poets, sceptics and saints have measured themselves against him. Some see a sublime moral teacher, some a prophet of the coming kingdom, some the very Word of God made flesh. The gospels themselves record his own question to his friends — *"Who do you say that I am?"* — and leave it ringing down the centuries. Every age has answered it again. That this unanswerable question still grips so much of humankind is, perhaps, the surest historical fact of all.
