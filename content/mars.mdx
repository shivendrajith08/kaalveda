## The Red Planet

Mars glows a distinctive rusty red in the night sky, and the reason is literally rust: its surface is rich in **iron oxide**, the same compound that reddens corroded metal on Earth. The ancients named it after gods of war for that blood-coloured light — the Romans called it Mars, and we still do.

It is a smaller, colder, drier sibling of Earth. Mars is about half our planet's diameter and has roughly a third of its gravity, so a person who weighs 60 kilograms on Earth would weigh barely 23 on Mars. Its thin atmosphere is almost entirely carbon dioxide and exerts less than one percent of Earth's surface pressure — far too little to breathe, and too thin to hold much warmth. Surface temperatures average a frigid −60°C.

:::insight A day that feels like home
Despite all its differences, Mars keeps a familiar rhythm. A Martian day — called a **sol** — lasts 24 hours and 39 minutes, remarkably close to Earth's. Its axis is tilted much like ours, too, so Mars has seasons. These similarities are part of why it feels, of all the planets, the most like a place humans could one day live.
:::

## A Landscape of Extremes

For a small world, Mars has the most dramatic terrain in the Solar System. It is home to **Olympus Mons**, the largest known volcano anywhere — a gently sloping mountain about 22 kilometres high, nearly three times the height of Everest, and so broad it would cover much of a country. Stretching across the equator is **Valles Marineris**, a system of canyons over 4,000 kilometres long and in places several kilometres deep, dwarfing the Grand Canyon.

At its poles lie shining **ice caps** of frozen water and carbon dioxide that grow and shrink with the seasons. Vast dust storms occasionally swell to engulf the entire planet, and fine red dust coats everything — a constant hazard for the robots, and one day the people, who explore it.

## The Ghost of Water

Mars today is bone dry at the surface, but it was not always so. Orbiters and rovers have found unmistakable signatures of a watery past: dried-up **river valleys**, deltas where rivers once emptied into lakes, rounded pebbles shaped by flowing water, and minerals that only form in its presence. Billions of years ago, Mars appears to have been warmer and wetter, with rivers, lakes, perhaps even a shallow ocean.

> Where there was water, there may have been life. The search for evidence of ancient Martian microbes — long extinct or perhaps still clinging on underground — is the single greatest driver of Mars exploration.

So what happened? Mars lost its protective magnetic field early in its history, and without that shield the solar wind gradually stripped away most of its atmosphere. As the air thinned, the planet grew cold, its water froze or escaped to space, and the world we now see was left behind. Much of its remaining water survives as ice, locked in the poles and buried beneath the surface.

## Two Little Moons

Mars has two small, lumpy moons, **Phobos** and **Deimos** — names meaning "fear" and "dread," the companions of the war god. They are most likely captured asteroids, just a few kilometres across. Phobos orbits so close and so fast that it crosses the Martian sky twice a day and is slowly spiralling inward; in tens of millions of years it will break apart, perhaps giving Mars a temporary ring.

## A Planet Full of Robots

No world beyond Earth has been studied as intensely as Mars. A fleet of orbiters maps it from above, while a lineage of **rovers** has trundled across its surface, growing ever more capable:

1. **Sojourner** (1997) — the first wheels on Mars, a microwave-sized proof of concept.
2. **Spirit and Opportunity** (2004) — twin geologists that found firm evidence of past water; Opportunity worked for nearly 15 years.
3. **Curiosity** (2012) — a car-sized nuclear-powered laboratory that confirmed Mars once had habitable conditions.
4. **Perseverance** (2021) — now caching rock samples for a future return to Earth, and carrying **Ingenuity**, the first aircraft to fly on another planet.

These missions, and the engineering feats behind landing on a distant world, are explored further in [space missions](/article/space-missions).

## The Next Giant Leap

Of all the planets, Mars is the one humans seriously plan to visit. It is reachable, it has water ice and raw materials, and its day-length and seasons make it comparatively hospitable — by the brutal standards of space. Both national space agencies and private companies are designing the rockets, habitats, and life-support systems that a crewed mission would require.

The obstacles are immense: a journey of six to nine months each way, exposure to space radiation, the challenge of living off the land, and the sheer cost. But Mars represents the most concrete answer to an old question — whether humanity will become a species that lives on more than one world. That ambition, and what a future on the red planet might look like, runs through [the future of space](/article/the-future-of-space).
