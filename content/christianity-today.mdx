## The Largest Faith on Earth

Roughly one in every three people alive today is a Christian — some **2.4 billion** souls, making Christianity the most followed religion in human history. It spans every continent and nearly every nation, speaks in thousands of languages, and gathers in forms as different as a silent Quaker meeting, a Roman Catholic high mass, a Pentecostal revival and an ancient Ethiopian liturgy older than most of Europe's churches. From the tiny band of [the early church](/article/christianity) the faith has grown into the broadest human community on the planet.

That single number, though, conceals a story of profound change. For most of the last thousand years Christianity was overwhelmingly the religion of Europe and, later, of the lands Europeans settled. That is no longer true. The faith's heartlands have moved decisively southward, and the consequences are reshaping it from the ground up.

:::insight The faith goes south
At the start of the twentieth century, the great majority of the world's Christians lived in Europe and North America. Today the majority live in **Africa, Latin America and Asia**. There are more churchgoers in Nigeria than in much of western Europe; Brazil and the Philippines are among the largest Christian nations on Earth; and sub-Saharan Africa is now the fastest-growing Christian region in the world. The typical Christian of the twenty-first century is young, brown or Black, and lives in the global South.
:::

## Branches of One Tree

The Christianity of today remains divided into the three great families that history bequeathed it. The **Catholic Church**, with well over a billion members, is the largest single body, led by the pope in Rome and present on every continent. The **Orthodox** churches of the East preserve the ancient liturgies and theology of the first millennium. And the many **Protestant** and independent traditions, heirs of the [Reformation](/article/reformation), range from historic denominations to the explosively growing **Pentecostal** and charismatic movements that have swept the global South in the last century.

These movements have given the old faith a new face — exuberant, miracle-minded, intensely personal, and astonishingly mobile. Where the [cathedrals](/article/christian-art) of Europe stand magnificent but increasingly empty, vast new congregations gather in converted warehouses and open fields across Africa and Latin America, singing in their own languages a gospel that feels to them brand new.

> Go therefore and make disciples of all nations.

The command with which the Gospel of Matthew closes — the "Great Commission" — still drives the missionary energy that has carried the faith into every corner of the Earth.

## Faith in a Secular Age

Even as it grows, Christianity faces searching challenges. In its old European heartland — the world that rose from the ruins of the [fallen Roman Empire](/article/fall-of-rome) and the medieval Christendom built on them — the faith has receded sharply, with empty pews and a rising tide of those who claim no religion at all. Across much of the developed world, secularism, science and individualism have loosened religion's hold on public life. The church wrestles, too, with painful reckonings over past failures and with deep internal debates about sexuality, authority and its place in modern society.

:::note An old faith among many
Christianity now lives, as never before, alongside the other great traditions of humanity — in daily, neighbourly contact with Islam, with [Hinduism](/article/hinduism), with Buddhism and with a vast secular world. The age of Christendom, when one church could claim to speak for a whole civilization, is gone. In its place is a quieter, humbler situation: one ancient faith among many, asked to make its case not by power but by persuasion and example — much as the [first Christians](/article/roman-empire) once did within the pluralist empire of Rome.
:::

## The Unfinished Story

Two thousand years after a teacher from Nazareth was executed on a Roman cross, the movement that grew from his memory is larger, more varied and more global than ever — and still changing fast. No one can say what shape it will take as the century unfolds, as Africa becomes its demographic heart and the old certainties of Europe fade. What seems clear is that the story is far from over. The faith that has already survived the fall of empires, the splitting of churches and the doubts of every age goes on, remade in each generation by the people who carry it — the latest chapter in one of the longest continuous stories humanity has ever told.
