## A Universe Without Edges

Most ancient peoples imagined a young, small world, created not long ago and bounded close at hand. The Hindu imagination went the other way entirely. From its earliest hymns it reached for a cosmos almost without limit — vast beyond counting in space, and in time stretching back through cycle after cycle with no first beginning and no final end. Where other traditions told of a single creation, Hinduism told of creation repeated forever, a universe that is made and unmade and made again like breath.

It is worth saying clearly what this article is and is not. The parallels drawn here between ancient Hindu cosmology and modern astronomy are **interpretive** — they are resonances and poetic echoes, not scientific claims, and the sages who imagined these cycles were doing metaphysics and myth, not physics. But the resonances are real and remarkable, and they are worth contemplating: a tradition of pure contemplation, working with no instrument but the disciplined mind, arrived at a sense of cosmic scale that the rest of the world would not reach until it built telescopes.

:::caution Poetry, not measurement
The huge and strangely modern-sounding numbers of Hindu cosmology were not derived from observation or calculation in the way that the age of the universe is measured today from the expansion of space and the [cosmic microwave background](/article/the-big-bang). They are the fruit of philosophical and devotional imagination. To notice that they happen to fall in the same dizzying range as modern figures is a genuine wonder — but it is a poetic correspondence, not evidence, and it should be enjoyed as such.
:::

## The Wheel of the Yugas

At the heart of Hindu cosmology lies a vision of time as cyclical rather than linear. History does not run once from a creation to an end; it turns like a wheel through four great ages, the **yugas**. The first, the **Satya Yuga**, is a golden age of truth and harmony. With each succeeding age — the **Treta**, the **Dvapara**, and finally our own **Kali Yuga** — virtue and order decline, like a bull standing on ever fewer legs, until the world grows so corrupt that it must be dissolved and renewed. Then the wheel turns and the golden age begins again.

These four ages together make a **mahayuga** of several million years. A thousand such cycles make a single **day of Brahma**, the creator — a **kalpa** of some 4.32 billion years — and his night, when the universe is withdrawn into dormancy, is just as long. Brahma lives a hundred such cosmic years before he too dissolves and a new creator arises. The sheer arithmetic is staggering: the full lifespan of one creator runs to more than three hundred trillion years.

> After a thousand ages have passed, the worlds dissolve into the unmanifest; and when the dawn of a new age breaks, they come forth once more. Thus the multitude of beings is born and born again.

## Strange Echoes of Modern Astronomy

It is here that the interpretive parallels grow striking. A single **day of Brahma**, 4.32 billion years, falls remarkably close to the age of the [Solar System](/article/the-solar-system) — about 4.6 billion years. The wider cosmic cycles, reaching into the billions and trillions of years, sit in the same vast register as the lifespan of [stars](/article/stars) and the deep-time future of the universe that astronomy now contemplates. And the very idea of a universe that expands from a seed, endures, and contracts again has an obvious kinship with the modern scientific question of whether our own cosmos, born in [the Big Bang](/article/the-big-bang), might one day collapse — or whether, as some speculative models imagine, it is one in an endless succession of cycles.

The old hymns even hesitated before the question of origins in a way that feels startlingly modern. The Rigveda's great *Hymn of Creation* refuses easy answers, asking whether anyone — even the gods, even the highest seer — can truly know how the world began, or whether it arose from being or from nothing at all. That ancient agnosticism, that willingness to stand in awe before an unanswerable beginning, is not so far from the humility of a scientist gazing back toward the first instant of [the cosmos](/article/the-big-bang).

:::insight The cosmic egg
One of Hinduism's oldest images of origins is the **Brahmanda** — the "cosmic egg," a golden seed afloat on the primal waters, from which the whole universe hatches and expands. It is, of course, a myth and not a measurement. Yet the picture of everything emerging from a single concentrated point and unfolding outward is one that the modern story of [the Big Bang](/article/the-big-bang) would, thousands of years later, render in the language of physics. The human imagination, it seems, reached for the same shape long before it had the means to test it.
:::

## Wonder Across the Ages

What should we make of these correspondences? Not that ancient India secretly knew modern physics — it did not, and to claim so is to mistake poetry for proof. The honest wonder is subtler and, in its way, greater. Working only with contemplation, [meditation](/article/yoga-and-meditation) and the disciplined [philosophical](/article/hindu-philosophy) imagination, a civilisation arrived at a sense of time and space that dwarfed every other ancient cosmology and that still, today, can make a reader catch their breath. The Hindu universe is endless, cyclical, breathing — a vision in which our entire history is a single morning in the life of a creator who is himself only one in an unending line. It is a humbling and exhilarating frame for the modern facts, and a fitting place to end a journey through one of humanity's oldest and most expansive ways of seeing the world.
