## Pictures That Are Also Sounds

The word *hieroglyph* is Greek for "sacred carving." The script appeared around **3100 BCE** and combines three kinds of sign:

- **Logograms** — a picture standing for a whole word (a sun-disk for "sun").
- **Phonograms** — signs standing for sounds, from single consonants to groups of two or three.
- **Determinatives** — silent signs added to the end of a word to clarify its meaning.

This blend made the system rich but demanding, and literacy was the prized preserve of trained **scribes**, who enjoyed status and exemption from hard labour. For everyday writing, Egyptians used faster cursive forms — **hieratic**, and later **demotic** — brushed in ink on papyrus.

## Writing for Eternity

Monumental hieroglyphs were never merely informative; they were **magical**. To write a thing was, in a sense, to make it real and lasting, which is why temple and tomb walls are covered in carved text meant to secure offerings, protection, and eternal life. Royal names were wrapped in a protective oval, the **cartouche**.

:::insight The silence and the key
After the temples closed in the early centuries CE, the knowledge of how to read hieroglyphs died out completely for some **1,400 years**. The script became beautiful, mysterious decoration — until the **Rosetta Stone**, carved with the same decree in hieroglyphic, demotic, and Greek, let **Jean-François Champollion** crack the code in 1822.
:::

## A Civilization Speaks Again

Decipherment turned silent walls back into a library, founding the science of **Egyptology** and opening the literature, religion, and history of [ancient Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt) to the modern world. The link between sacred writing and sacred belief runs deep — explored further in [Egyptian religion](/article/egyptian-religion) and across the [Sacred Books](/category/sacred-books) category.

> A script designed to defeat death did exactly that: the Egyptians wrote so that they would be remembered, and we remember them.
