## A City Born of the Tiber

Rome began where geography invited it. A bend in the river Tiber, fordable yet defensible, lay a short way inland from the sea — close enough to trade, far enough to be safe from pirates. Around it rose a ring of low hills whose villages, over generations, grew together into a single town. Tradition fixed the founding in **753 BCE** and gave it to **Romulus**, who according to legend killed his twin **Remus** and named the city for himself. The truth was slower and less dramatic: Rome was not founded so much as it accumulated, a settlement of Latins and Sabines that learned early to absorb its neighbours rather than merely conquer them.

That instinct for absorption became Rome's genius. Where other ancient cities guarded citizenship jealously, Rome extended it — first to defeated Latin towns, later to whole provinces — until a man born in Spain, Africa, or Syria could call himself Roman and even rule as emperor. A city that might have remained one town among many instead became a machine for turning outsiders into citizens.

:::insight Rome's secret weapon was citizenship
Most ancient empires ruled subjects. Rome, uniquely, manufactured Romans. By steadily widening who counted as one of its own, it bound conquered peoples to its fortunes — and gave them a stake in defending it.

## Three Constitutions, One Name

The thousand-year arc of Rome falls into three great political ages. It began as a **monarchy**, ruled by kings — some Latin, some Etruscan — until, tradition says, the Romans expelled the tyrant Tarquin in **509 BCE** and swore never again to bow to a king. What replaced him was the [Roman Republic](/article/roman-republic): a layered system of elected magistrates, a powerful Senate, and popular assemblies, designed so that no single man could seize total power.

For nearly five centuries the Republic carried Rome from a regional power to the master of the Mediterranean. But the very conquests that made it great also broke it. Generals grew richer and more loyal to their armies than to the state, and a century of civil wars ended only when one man, the adopted heir of [Julius Caesar](/article/julius-caesar), gathered every office into his own hands. As **Augustus**, he founded the [Roman Empire](/article/roman-empire) in **27 BCE** — a monarchy in all but name, which Romans accepted because it brought peace.

## What Rome Built

Roman power rested on more than [the legions](/article/roman-army). It rested on organisation made visible in stone. Roads ran arrow-straight across whole provinces; aqueducts carried mountain water into city fountains; concrete vaults spanned spaces no Greek architect had dared. The achievements of [Roman engineering](/article/roman-engineering) were not decoration but infrastructure — the plumbing of empire, built to move armies, grain, and water with relentless efficiency.

Above all, Rome built **law**. The idea that authority should rest on written, public rules rather than the whim of a ruler; that a person was innocent until proven guilty; that contracts and property had standing a magistrate must respect — these Roman habits of mind passed into the legal systems of Europe and, through them, much of the world.

> They make a desert and call it peace.

The historian Tacitus put those bitter words in the mouth of a conquered chieftain, and they capture the cost of Rome honestly. Empire was built on conquest, slavery, and the crushing of those who resisted. Yet within its borders Rome also built something durable: two centuries of relative peace, the **Pax Romana**, in which trade, cities, and ideas flowed freely from Britain to the Euphrates.

## A Mediterranean World

Rome did not rule in isolation. Its world was the whole Mediterranean basin, and it drank deeply from the cultures it absorbed. It took its gods and much of its art from Greece; it fed its people on Egyptian grain and was bewitched by Egyptian style, weaving the worship of **Isis** into its own [religion](/article/roman-religion). When Caesar sailed to [Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt) and met Cleopatra, two ancient worlds met at the height of their fame.

:::note An empire that never quite ended
When the western emperors fell in 476 CE, Rome's eastern half lived on for another thousand years as Byzantium, and its Church, law, and Latin tongue shaped everything after. Rome did not so much die as dissolve into the foundations of the world that followed — a story the [fall of Rome](/article/fall-of-rome) tells in full.

To study Rome is to study how a community organises power, defends itself, and decides who belongs — the oldest questions of political life, asked at the scale of a continent and answered, for a thousand years, in Latin.
