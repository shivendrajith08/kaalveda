## A Patrician on the Rise

Gaius Julius Caesar was born in **100 BCE** into an old but faded patrician family that claimed descent from the goddess **Venus** herself — a pedigree he would later turn to political advantage. He came of age in the violent, factional Rome of [the late Republic](/article/roman-republic), where the murders of Marius and Sulla had shown that armies, not arguments, now decided who ruled. A gifted speaker and a shameless borrower of money, Caesar climbed the ladder of offices by charm, bribery, and an unfailing instinct for where power was moving.

His decisive move was an alliance. In **60 BCE** he joined with Rome's richest man, **Crassus**, and its greatest general, **Pompey**, in the informal pact historians call the First Triumvirate. With their backing he won the consulship — and then the prize that would make him: a long military command in Gaul.

## The Conquest of Gaul

For eight years Caesar waged war across the lands of modern France and Belgium, and even raided Britain and crossed the Rhine into Germany. By the campaign's end he had defeated hundreds of tribes, killed or enslaved enormous numbers, and added a vast new province to the empire. He recorded it all himself in the *Commentaries on the Gallic War*, a masterpiece of clear Latin prose written, tellingly, in the third person — propaganda so well crafted that it is still read as literature two thousand years later.

:::insight Caesar wrote his own legend
By publishing dispatches in plain, vivid Latin, Caesar made himself the hero of his own story in the minds of ordinary Romans. He understood, as few generals have, that the account of a victory can matter as much as the victory itself.

Gaul made Caesar fabulously rich and gave him a battle-hardened [army](/article/roman-army) devoted to him personally. To the frightened Senate, that combination looked exactly like the tyranny the Republic had been built to prevent.

## Crossing the Rubicon

When the Senate, prodded by Pompey, ordered Caesar to disband his army and return to Rome as a private citizen — where his enemies waited to destroy him — he chose war instead. In **January 49 BCE** he led a single legion across the **Rubicon**, the small river marking the boundary a general was forbidden to cross under arms.

> The die is cast.

With those words, tradition says, he plunged Rome into civil war. The campaign that followed carried him the length of the Mediterranean. He broke Pompey's larger army at **Pharsalus** in Greece, and when Pompey fled to [Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt), Caesar pursued.

## Egypt and Cleopatra

In Alexandria, Caesar was drawn into a dynastic quarrel of the Ptolemies and into the arms of **Cleopatra VII**, the brilliant young queen who would become the last [pharaoh](/article/the-pharaoh) of Egypt. He restored her to a throne she shared, fathered a son she named Caesarion, and lingered in the richest kingdom of the ancient world. The meeting of the Roman master of the west and the Greek queen of the Nile became one of history's enduring legends — and bound Rome's fate ever closer to Egypt's grain and gold.

## The Ides of March

Returning to Rome supreme, Caesar gathered every honour into himself: **dictator for life**, his image on the coinage, a month renamed *July* in his honour. He reformed the calendar, relieved debt, and planned grand works — but he also wore the trappings of a king in a city that still swore its old oath against them.

To a band of senators led by **Brutus** and **Cassius**, this was tyranny that had to be cut out. On **15 March 44 BCE** — the Ides of March — they surrounded him in the Senate and stabbed him twenty-three times at the foot of Pompey's statue.

:::note The murder that backfired
The assassins killed Caesar to save the Republic, but they had no plan for what came next. Their act unleashed another round of civil war, at the end of which Caesar's heir Octavian — soon to be **Augustus** — gathered all power into one pair of hands and founded the [Roman Empire](/article/roman-empire). In death Caesar achieved what the conspirators most feared: he became a god, and his name became the very word for emperor — *Kaiser*, *Tsar*, *Caesar*.
