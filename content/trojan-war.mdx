## The Quarrel That Began It

The story the Greeks told was this. At a wedding of the gods, the goddess of discord threw down a golden apple marked "for the fairest," and three goddesses — Hera, Athena, and Aphrodite — fell to quarrelling over it. They asked a Trojan prince, **Paris**, to judge, and **Aphrodite** won his vote by promising him the most beautiful woman in the world. That woman was **Helen**, wife of the Greek king Menelaus of Sparta. Paris carried her off to Troy, and all the kings of Greece, bound by an oath, gathered a vast fleet under Menelaus's brother **Agamemnon** to win her back.

For ten years the Greek host besieged the great walled city of **Troy** on the coast of Asia Minor, and could not break it. The *Iliad* of **Homer**, the foundation poem of Western literature, tells only a few weeks of that final year, centred on the towering rage of the Greek hero **Achilles** — his quarrel with Agamemnon, his withdrawal from the fighting, the death of his friend Patroclus, and his terrible revenge on the Trojan champion **Hector**.

:::insight A poem about anger, not victory
The Iliad does not end with Troy's fall. It is not really about the war at all but about wrath, grief, and mortality — the price heroes pay for glory. Homer made the founding war of his people a meditation on the human cost of pride.

## The Wooden Horse

The city fell at last not by force but by cunning. The Greeks built an enormous hollow **wooden horse**, hid their best warriors inside it, and sailed away as if defeated, leaving the horse as a supposed offering to the gods. The Trojans, against the warnings of those who smelled a trap, dragged it within their walls in triumph. That night the hidden Greeks crept out, opened the gates to their returned comrades, and put Troy to the sword.

> I fear the Greeks, even bearing gifts.

The Trojan priest Laocoön's warning, immortalised later in Latin, has become a proverb for the danger hidden inside a tempting offer. The fall of Troy — the burning city, the slaughter, the queens led off into slavery — became one of the most powerful images in all of [Greek mythology](/article/greek-mythology), a byword for the ruin that pride and the gods can bring upon even the mightiest.

## Memory or Myth?

For most of history the Trojan War was taken as real, then dismissed as pure legend. Then in the 1870s a German businessman named **Heinrich Schliemann**, clutching his Homer, dug into the mound of **Hisarlik** in north-western Turkey and found not one city but the layered ruins of many, one atop another across thousands of years. He had found *a* Troy — a real city, repeatedly destroyed and rebuilt, in just the place Homer described, commanding the rich trade route to the Black Sea.

:::caution Why this story carries a "theory" badge
That a city stood at Troy is certain. That it was besieged and burned around 1200 BCE is likely. But whether a great coalition of Greeks fought there for a stolen queen, whether Achilles or Helen ever lived, and how much of Homer is history rather than centuries of poetic invention — none of that can be proven. The war hovers between archaeology and art.

Archaeology has shown that the late **Bronze Age** world Homer describes was real: the gold-rich **Mycenaean** kingdoms of Greece, the powerful citadel of Troy, the great powers like the Hittites and [Egypt](/article/ancient-egypt) whose records mention raiders and wars along these coasts. A real conflict, or a dim memory of many, may well lie behind the legend, polished by four centuries of oral poetry before Homer fixed it in verse.

## The War That Outlived Troy

Whatever its truth, the story refused to die. The Greeks treated the *Iliad* almost as scripture; [Alexander the Great](/article/alexander-the-great) is said to have slept with a copy beneath his pillow and to have visited the supposed tomb of Achilles. And when [Rome](/article/roman-mythology) sought a noble origin for itself, it claimed descent from the Trojan prince **Aeneas**, who in Virgil's *Aeneid* escapes the burning city to found the line of Rome — turning Troy's defeat into the seed of a greater empire.

So the Trojan War became less an event than a story the West has told and retold for three thousand years — about beauty and war, honour and ruin, the glory of heroes and the terrible cost of their pride. It may be the most influential war that we are not even sure took place.
