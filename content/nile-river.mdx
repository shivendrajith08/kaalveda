## Two Rivers Become One

The Nile is really two great rivers joined. The **White Nile** rises in the Great Lakes region of East Africa and flows steadily year-round; the **Blue Nile** descends from the Ethiopian Highlands, swollen each summer by monsoon rains. They meet at **Khartoum** in modern Sudan and run north as a single mighty stream to the Mediterranean. It was the Blue Nile's summer surge that produced Egypt's life-giving **annual flood**.

## The Engine of Egyptian Life

Because rain almost never falls on the Egyptian valley, the river was everything:

- It **watered the fields**, depositing fertile black silt each year.
- It was the **highway** of the land — flowing north, with a wind blowing south, so boats could travel both ways.
- It set the **calendar**, divided into three seasons: *Akhet* (flood), *Peret* (growing), and *Shemu* (harvest).
- It shaped **settlement**, concentrating people on the narrow strip of green between the deserts.

:::insight A river you can see from space
From orbit, Egypt is a stark image: a brilliant green thread and its delta, surrounded by tan desert. More than 90% of Egyptians still live on the few percent of the land the Nile makes habitable.
:::

## The River Today

The [Aswan High Dam](/article/modern-egypt) ended the natural flood in 1970, trading the silt cycle for stored water and electricity. Today the Nile remains the lifeline of [modern Egypt](/article/modern-egypt) and the heart of its [tourism](/article/egypt-tourism), even as its waters are increasingly contested among the nations that share its vast basin.

> "Egypt," wrote Herodotus, "is the gift of the river." Twenty-five centuries later, it still is.
