## A House for a God

Where a Greek temple was a beautiful box to shelter a statue and a Roman one a civic monument, the Hindu temple — the **mandir** — is something stranger and more inward. It is conceived as the literal dwelling-place of a deity, who is invited to inhabit the consecrated image within, and as a model of the whole cosmos rendered in stone. To enter it is to make a journey from the ordinary world toward the centre of all things.

The plan follows an ancient diagram, the **vastu-purusha-mandala**, a sacred grid laid over the ground that aligns the building with the cardinal directions and the order of the universe. At the heart of the temple lies its most important and least adorned space: the **garbhagriha**, the "womb-chamber," a small, dark, windowless cell that houses the central image of the god. Above it rises the tower — the curving **shikhara** of the north or the stepped, pyramidal **vimana** of the south — a man-made mountain pointing the eye and the soul upward toward heaven.

:::insight A journey from light into dark
The genius of the temple is its choreography of movement. The worshipper passes from the dazzling, crowded, sunlit exterior — its walls alive with carvings of gods, dancers, animals and lovers, the whole teeming abundance of creation — through a series of ever-darker, ever-quieter halls, until reaching the tiny, silent, lamp-lit cell at the centre, where there is nothing but the image of the god and the self that has come to meet it. The architecture itself enacts the inward turn of [Hindu philosophy](/article/hindu-philosophy): from the many to the one, from the outward world to the still point within.
:::

## Two Great Styles

Over the centuries Hindu architecture flowered into two great regional traditions. In the north arose the **Nagara** style, its temples crowned by a single soaring, beehive-curved **shikhara**, clustered sometimes with smaller echoing towers — as at the exquisite, sensuous shrines of **Khajuraho** or the great sun-temple of **Konark**, carved as a colossal chariot drawn by stone horses. In the south flourished the **Dravidian** style, with its stepped pyramidal towers over the sanctum and, later, its astonishing **gopurams** — the immense gateway-towers, encrusted from base to summit with brightly painted figures, that loom over the temple-cities of the Tamil south.

The high-water mark of the southern tradition came under the **Chola** dynasty, whose kings between roughly 900 and 1200 CE poured the wealth of a maritime empire into stone. The **Brihadisvara Temple** at Thanjavur, completed around 1010 CE, raised a tower more than sixty metres high, capped by a single dome-stone of many tonnes that engineers still marvel at — a feat of lifting and balancing to rank with the [pyramids of Giza](/article/pyramids-of-giza) or the vaulting of [Roman engineering](/article/roman-engineering).

> The temple is the body of God made visible, that those who cannot see the formless may worship the form.

## The Engineering of Awe

The ambition of these buildings demanded extraordinary craft. Temples were raised almost entirely without mortar, from precisely cut granite and sandstone blocks locked together by their own weight and shape, much as the [builders of Greece](/article/greek-architecture) fitted their marble. Massive stones were hauled up long earthen ramps; towers were corbelled inward course by course until they met at the summit. Every surface was then given over to the sculptor, so that the structure and its decoration became inseparable — the building is the carving, and the carving is theology.

For the figures that swarm across temple walls are not ornament for its own sake. They form a vast illustrated scripture: the avatars of [Vishnu](/article/hindu-gods), the cosmic dance of Shiva, scenes from the [Ramayana](/article/ramayana) and the [Mahabharata](/article/mahabharata), the gods and goddesses in their full multiplicity. A temple was a book the unlettered could read, a sermon in stone available to anyone who walked its corridors.

:::note Temples that never closed
Unlike the silent ruins of the ancient Mediterranean, India's great temples are mostly living shrines still. At Thanjavur, at Madurai, at Tirupati, the lamps are still lit, the bells still ring, and the same rites are performed that were performed a thousand years ago. Some draw more pilgrims in a year than any building on Earth. They are not monuments to a vanished faith but working houses of a god still believed to be at home.
:::

## Stone Made Sacred

The Hindu temple is one of humanity's great architectural achievements — comparable in ambition to the cathedrals of Europe or the pyramids of Egypt, yet built on an entirely different principle. It does not gather a crowd to face an altar; it draws the single soul inward to meet the divine in darkness and silence. In its rising tower it images the cosmic mountain at the centre of the world; in its sculpted walls it tells the whole story of the gods; and in its sequence of chambers it teaches, in stone, the central lesson of the tradition that built it — that the way to the infinite leads not outward but in.
