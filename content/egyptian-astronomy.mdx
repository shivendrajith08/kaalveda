## A Star That Started the Year

The single most important sight in the Egyptian sky was the **heliacal rising of Sirius** — the morning when the brightest star, called **Sopdet**, first reappeared on the eastern horizon at dawn after a season hidden in the sun's glare. Remarkably, this happened right around the time the **Nile flood** began. The Egyptians tied their new year to that rising, turning a star into the herald of the harvest.

## The 365-Day Year

From these observations the Egyptians built a **civil calendar** of 365 days: twelve months of thirty days, plus five extra "birthday of the gods" days at the end. It was one of the first calendars to abandon the awkward moon in favour of a clean solar count — and it is the direct ancestor of the calendar we use today.

:::insight The leap-year problem they spotted
A true year is about 365¼ days, so the Egyptian calendar slowly drifted against the seasons by one day every four years. Egyptian priests understood this drift; centuries later it was corrected by adding a leap day — the reform behind the Julian and then Gregorian calendars.
:::

## Clocks of Star and Shadow

Egyptian astronomy was intensely practical:

- **Decans** — 36 stars or star-groups that rose in succession through the night, used as a celestial clock to divide darkness into hours.
- **Merkhet and bay** — simple sighting instruments (a plumb-line and a slotted palm-rib) for aligning buildings and tracking stars.
- **Shadow clocks and water clocks** for measuring the daytime and temple-night hours.

## Aligning Earth to Heaven

This knowledge fed straight into architecture. To set the [Pyramids of Giza](/article/pyramids-of-giza) almost perfectly to true north, surveyors sighted on circumpolar "imperishable stars" — the ones that never set, and so became a natural symbol of eternal life in [Egyptian religion](/article/egyptian-religion).

> The same sky that timed the planting of grain also told the dead which way to face for resurrection. For the Egyptians, astronomy, farming, and faith were a single seamless craft.
